### Figure 1: Soil Composition Pie Chart
**Location**: End of "Soil Composition: The Four Parts" subsection (Concept Two)

**Purpose**: Show the relative volumes of mineral matter, organic matter, water, and air in healthy soil.

**Visual specs**:
- Pie chart with four segments
- Mineral matter (40-45%): tan/brown
- Organic matter (5%): dark brown
- Water (25%): blue
- Air (25%): light gray/white

**Caption**: "A healthy soil is roughly 45% mineral matter (sand, silt, clay), 5% organic matter (humus), and 50% pore space (half water, half air). The balance of water and air in the pore space is critical: too much water and roots suffocate; too much air and the soil cannot retain moisture."

**Callout boxes** (optional):

### Figure 2: Soil Profile Diagram (Horizons)
**Location**: End of "The Soil Profile: Layers with Identity" subsection (Concept Two)

**Purpose**: Show the vertical structure of soil from surface to bedrock, with labels for O, A, B, C horizons and their characteristics.

**Visual specs**:
- Vertical cross-section, roughly 6-8 feet deep
- Color-coded layers:
  - O horizon: reddish-brown, with visible leaf litter at top, more decomposed material at bottom
  - A horizon (topsoil): dark brown, with fine texture; depth marked as "2-3 inches (but varies)"
  - B horizon (subsoil): reddish/orange (oxidized iron), denser appearance; note some calcium carbonate nodules if present
  - C horizon: tan/gray, coarser texture; partially weathered rock fragments visible
  - Bedrock (R): solid gray rock at bottom

**Labels and annotations**:
- O horizon: "Leaf litter, decomposing plant material, humus. High in nitrogen and carbon. Biologically active."
- A horizon: "Topsoil: mineral particles mixed with organic matter. Dark color from humus. Where plant roots are densest. Where microbial decomposition occurs."
- B horizon: "Subsoil: accumulation of clay and minerals leached from above. Dense, less permeable. Fewer organisms. Sometimes contains calcium carbonate nodules."
- C horizon: "Parent material: partially weathered rock. Resembles original bedrock but broken into fragments. Little biological activity."

**Optional feature**: Arrows showing water percolating downward (dotted lines), showing how materials are leached from A to B horizon.

### Figure 3: Nutrient Deficiency Comparison
**Location**: After the macronutrient section (end of Concept One, subsection "Macronutrients: The Heavy Lifters")

**Purpose**: Show visual differences between deficiency symptoms for nitrogen, magnesium, potassium, phosphorus.

**Visual specs**:
- Four plant illustrations side by side, each showing a different deficiency
- Nitrogen deficiency: entire leaves yellow, starting with older leaves; overall stunted growth
- Magnesium deficiency: older leaves show interveinal chlorosis (green veins, yellow between); veins stay green
- Potassium deficiency: leaf margins brown/scorched; rest of leaf may be yellow; overall leaf appears burned at edges
- Phosphorus deficiency: older leaves turn purple or red; veins may have darker color; overall slow growth

**Caption**: "Nutrient deficiency symptoms are often diagnostic. Nitrogen deficiency yellows entire leaves (older leaves first, due to plant mobilization). Magnesium deficiency yellows only the tissue between veins, leaving veins green. Potassium deficiency burns leaf margins. Phosphorus deficiency turns leaves purple or red due to anthocyanin pigments showing through where chlorophyll is lacking. These visual cues help identify which nutrient is limiting."

### Figure 4: Legume-Rhizobium Symbiosis (Root Nodule Cross-Section)
**Location**: In Concept Three, "The Legume-Rhizobium Partnership" subsection

**Purpose**: Show the structure of a root nodule and the relationship between plant cells, bacterial cells, and leghaemoglobin.

**Visual specs**:
- Cross-section of a single root nodule, enlarged to show cellular detail
- Outer layer: plant root tissue
- Nodule center: cluster of plant cells (infected cells) with bacterial cells inside
- Key cellular features:
  - Bacterial cells (small rods, dark) shown inside plant cells
  - Leghaemoglobin (pink-red protein, shown as a halo around bacterial cells): "Maintains anaerobic environment by binding oxygen"
  - Membrane surrounding bacterial cells (plant-derived): "Peribacteroid membrane"
  - Vascular bundle at periphery: "Transports sugars to bacteria and ammonia to rest of plant"

**Labels**:
- "Root nodule (enlargement)"
- "Plant cell nuclei" (shown in corners)
- "Rhizobia bacteria" (in center)
- "Leghaemoglobin" (as colored halo)
- "Nitrogenase enzyme at work here: N₂ → NH₃"
- Arrow pointing down: "NH₃ absorbed by plant; used to make amino acids"
- Arrow pointing up: "Sugars (photosynthate) exported from plant to feed bacteria"

**Optional callout**: A small inset showing the nitrogenase reaction: N₂ + 16 ATP + 8 e⁻ + 8 H⁺ → 2 NH₃ + 16 ADP + 16 Pi + H₂

### Figure 5: Mycorrhizal Fungi and Root Hairs (Comparison)
**Location**: In Concept Three, after the discussion of nutrient depletion zones and mycorrhizal extension

**Purpose**: Show how mycorrhizal hyphae extend the reach of roots beyond the nutrient depletion zone.

**Visual specs**:
- Two side-by-side illustrations showing a plant root
- Left panel: Root alone, with nutrient depletion zone shown as a colored halo around the root (darker = more depleted). Caption: "Root hair alone: nutrients in the depletion zone are absorbed, but phosphate beyond the zone diffuses slowly and is unavailable."
- Right panel: Same root, but with mycorrhizal hyphae extending outward (thin, branching filaments, different color—e.g., fungal color). The hyphae extend far beyond the depletion zone. Caption: "Root with mycorrhizal fungi: hyphae reach into areas the root cannot, delivering phosphorus and other nutrients back to the plant."

**Labels**:
- "Root hair" (enlarged view of single root hair cell)
- "Depletion zone" (colored halo, orange or yellow gradient)
- "Phosphorus-rich soil" (further out, blue or green color)
- "Mycorrhizal hyphae" (thin filaments, darker color, sometimes shown with swollen structures—arbuscules—at the root cell interface)
- Arrow: "Hyphae deliver P and other minerals"
- Reverse arrow: "Plant provides sugars to fungus"

### Figure 6: Soil Particle Size Classification (Sand, Silt, Clay)
**Location**: In Concept Two, "Soil Composition" subsection, after the text defines sand, silt, and clay

**Purpose**: Show relative sizes of soil particles and their properties.

**Visual specs**:
- Scale diagram (like a ruler) showing relative sizes
- Sand grain: large, clearly visible to eye, roughly 1-2 mm
- Silt grain: much smaller, visible under magnification, 0.002-0.1 mm
- Clay particle: tiny, visible only under electron microscope, <0.002 mm
- Optional: magnified images or electron micrograph-style illustrations of each

**Accompanying table**:
| Particle | Size | Property | Implication |
|----------|------|----------|------------|
| Sand | 0.1-2 mm | Large, angular, coarse | Drains quickly, does not hold water or nutrients well |
| Silt | 0.002-0.1 mm | Medium, fine texture | Moderate drainage, moderate water-holding |
| Clay | <0.002 mm | Tiny, platelets | Holds water and nutrients, but becomes waterlogged; hard when dry |
| Loam | Mix of all three | Balanced | Ideal for most crops |

### Figure 7: The Nitrogen Cycle (Simplified, Focused on Biological Aspects)
**Location**: At the end of Concept Three, in the "The Broader Nitrogen Cycle" subsection

**Purpose**: Show the major pathways of nitrogen in nature, highlighting biological nitrogen fixation, nitrification, and denitrification.

**Visual specs**:
- Circular diagram (cycle) showing:
  - **Atmosphere** (top): N₂ gas (blue cloud)
  - **Biological nitrogen fixation** (left): Arrow from N₂ → NH₃, labeled "Rhizobia, cyanobacteria, Haber-Bosch"
  - **Nitrification** (left side): Arrow from NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻, labeled "Nitrifying bacteria"
  - **Plant uptake** (bottom): Arrow from NO₃⁻ into plant (green box), labeled "Plant absorbs nitrate"
  - **Organic nitrogen** (bottom): Dead organisms and plant debris, labeled "Proteins, nucleic acids in dead matter"
  - **Decomposition** (right): Arrow from organic N → NH₃, labeled "Decomposer bacteria and fungi"
  - **Denitrification** (right side): Arrow from NO₃⁻ → N₂, labeled "Denitrifying bacteria in waterlogged soil"
  - Back to atmosphere (top): Denitrification closes the loop

**Key features**:
- Color code: blue for gaseous nitrogen, orange/red for ammonia and nitrite, green for nitrate and organic nitrogen
- Boxes for "Plant," "Atmosphere," "Soil microbes," "Soil organic matter"
- Small icons or labels: "Legume nodule" (at N-fixation step), "Nitrifying bacteria" (at nitrification step), "Waterlogged soil" (at denitrification step)

**Caption**: "The nitrogen cycle shows how atmospheric N₂ becomes available to plants through biological nitrogen fixation, then cycles through the plant-soil-organism system. Denitrification in waterlogged soils returns nitrogen to the atmosphere. The Haber-Bosch process (industrial fixation) now fixes nearly as much nitrogen as all biological fixation combined, with consequences for the global nitrogen budget."

### Figure 8: Alternative Feeding Strategies (Comparison of Four Plant Types)

- Label: "Dodder (Cuscuta): No leaves. Holoparasite. Coils around host and draws water, sugars, minerals via vascular connection."

- Decomposing organic matter (brown)

- Tree branch (gray/brown)
- Orchid plant (green, with flowers) growing on branch
- Aerial roots (gray filaments) dangling or clinging to bark

- Label: "Epiphyte (orchid or bromeliad): Grows on tree without parasitizing. Absorbs moisture and minerals from humus in tree crevices or from rainfall."

**Bottom-right: Insectivorous plant (Venus flytrap)**:
- Small rosette plant with trap-like leaves
- One trap shown open, one partially closed
- Insect (fly) in a trap

- Label: "Venus flytrap: Traps insects with modified leaves. Digestive enzymes break down prey. Absorbs nitrogen from insect body, supplementing low-nitrogen soil."

## Figure Sourcing Notes

The source OpenStax Biology modules (66602-66605) included references to figures, but the actual images are not included in the text files provided. For publication, obtain or create:

1. Pie charts can be generated from the composition percentages given.
2. Soil profile diagram can be adapted from standard geological/agronomic references.

4. Root nodule cross-section and mycorrhizal diagrams can be simplified line drawings or adapted from plant biology textbooks.
5. Nitrogen cycle diagrams are standard in ecology textbooks; create a simplified version focused on biological steps.

## Captions and Alt Text Standards

All captions should:
- Answer "What is being shown?" in one sentence.
- Answer "Why does it matter?" in one more sentence.
- Include all labels visible in the figure.

All figures should have alt text for accessibility:
- Describe the figure type (diagram, chart, photograph, etc.)
- Name key elements
- State the main takeaway
- Keep under 150 words for complex figures

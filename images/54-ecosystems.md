### Energy Flow & Trophic Levels

**[FIGURE: Trophic Pyramid—Energy]**
- Base: Primary producers (plants, algae) with gross primary productivity label (e.g., 20,810 kcal/m²/yr)
- Show net primary productivity as subset (e.g., 7,633 kcal/m²/yr after respiration)
- Level 2: Primary consumers (herbivores), ~10% of NPP (e.g., 1,103 kcal/m²/yr)
- Level 3: Secondary consumers (small carnivores), ~10% of level 2 (e.g., 110 kcal/m²/yr)
- Level 4: Tertiary consumers (apex), ~10% of level 3 (e.g., 11 kcal/m²/yr)
- Arrow labeled "Heat loss from respiration at each level"
- Annotation: "Energy pyramid always upright; 2nd law of thermodynamics"

**[FIGURE: Trophic Pyramid—Biomass (Upright)]**
- Terrestrial ecosystem example (grassland or forest)
- Show that biomass generally decreases with trophic level
- Primary producers have greatest total mass
- Example: Forest with large trees, fewer herbivores by total weight, even fewer carnivores

**[FIGURE: Trophic Pyramid—Biomass (Inverted)]**
- Aquatic ecosystem example (phytoplankton → zooplankton → fish)
- Explain: phytoplankton are small and fast-reproducing; even though they turn over rapidly, zooplankton biomass at any moment exceeds phytoplankton biomass
- Annotation: "Inverted biomass pyramid possible when primary producers have high turnover rate"

**[FIGURE: Trophic Pyramid—Numbers (Variable)]**
- Grassland: Many plants → many herbivores → fewer carnivores (upright)
- Forest: Few large trees → many insects → fewer birds (inverted)
- Annotation: "Numbers pyramid depends on organism size and life history"

**[FIGURE: Lake Ontario Food Chain]**
- Linear sequence: Green algae (phytoplankton) → Zooplankton → Smelt (fish) → Trout → Chinook salmon
- Label trophic levels (producer through apex consumer)
- Show energy values declining at each step

**[FIGURE: Food Web vs. Food Chain]**
- Simple food chain: Linear path A → B → C → D
- Compare to food web: Same organisms but showing that organisms eat at multiple trophic levels
  - Example: Herbivore eats plant AND detritus; omnivore eats plant AND herbivore; decomposer eats everything
- Annotation: "Food chains are simpler but incomplete; food webs are accurate but complex"

**[FIGURE: Ecological Efficiency—Net Production Efficiency (NPE)]**
- Compare endotherm (mammal/bird) vs. ectotherm (fish/reptile)
- Endotherm uses 30% of consumed energy for respiration/heat; 10% for growth → 1–3% NPE
- Ectotherm uses 10% for respiration/heat; 20% for growth → 15–20% NPE
- Show consequence: endotherm must eat more frequently
- Example: Caterpillar (18% NPE) vs. squirrel (1.6% NPE)

**[FIGURE: Bioaccumulation in Aquatic Food Chain]**
- Phytoplankton: Low toxin concentration
- Zooplankton: Higher concentration (eaten many phytoplankton)
- Small fish: Even higher
- Large fish (walleye): 4+ times the concentration of phytoplankton
- Apex predator (bird): 10+ times the concentration of fish
- Example: DDT or PCBs accumulating from lake to eagle

### Biogeochemical Cycles

**[FIGURE: Water (Hydrologic) Cycle]**
- Sun evaporates water from ocean surface → rises as water vapor in atmosphere
- Condensation in atmosphere → clouds
- Precipitation over land and ocean
- Surface runoff: rain flows downslope → streams → rivers → ocean
- Infiltration: rain soaks into ground → groundwater reservoir → slow seepage to ocean or streams
- Subsurface flow: groundwater movement
- Transpiration: plants uptake water and release vapor through leaves
- Residence times in boxes: Ocean 3,000 yr; Atmosphere 9 days; Groundwater 10,000+ yr; Lakes/rivers 16 days (average)
- Annotation: "Water cycle is the transport mechanism for nutrient cycles"

**[FIGURE: Carbon Cycle—Biological Fast Loop]**
- Atmosphere (CO₂)
- Photosynthesis: CO₂ → Glucose in plants
- Respiration: Glucose → CO₂ (organisms)
- Consumption: Plant → Herbivore → Carnivore (each respire, release CO₂)
- Decomposition: Dead organisms → CO₂ via bacteria/fungi
- Arrows showing rapid cycle (days to years)
- Annotation: "Biological carbon cycle is fast; atmosphere and biosphere exchange continuously"

**[FIGURE: Carbon Cycle—Geological Slow Loop]**
- Photosynthesis (ancient): CO₂ → Glucose → Dead organic matter
- Sedimentation and burial: Organic matter → Peat → Coal/Oil/Gas (millions of years)
- Fossilization box showing "100 million+ years residence time"
- Combustion: Fossil fuels → CO₂ (fast, seconds to hours)
- Volcanism: Crustal carbon → CO₂ (slow, geological timescale)
- Limestone formation: Calcium carbonate shells → CaCO₃ sediment → Limestone (largest carbon reservoir)
- Annotation: "Geological cycle is slow, but human combustion extracts carbon 100× faster than it accumulated"

**[FIGURE: Ocean Acidification from Rising CO₂]**
- Show three time points: Pre-industrial (280 ppm) → Industrial (380 ppm) → Current (420 ppm)
- For each: Atmospheric CO₂ → Dissolves in seawater → Forms carbonic acid → Lowers pH
- Graph showing pH drop from 8.2 to 8.1 (0.1 unit = 30% increase in acidity)
- Impact on shell-forming organisms (corals, pteropods, mollusks): Reduced calcification rate

**[FIGURE: Nitrogen Cycle]**
- Atmosphere (N₂, 78%)
- Nitrogen fixation: N₂ → NH₃/NH₄⁺ by bacteria (*Azotobacter*, *Cyanobacteria*, *Rhizobium*)
- Uptake: NH₄⁺/NO₃⁻ → Organic N in plants
- Food chain: Plants → Herbivores → Carnivores
- Excretion/death → Nitrogenous waste
- Ammonification: Organic N → NH₄⁺ (bacteria/fungi)
- Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (*Nitrosomonas* etc.)
- Denitrification: NO₃⁻ → N₂ (*Pseudomonas*, *Clostridium*)
- Return to atmosphere
- Annotation: "Three bacterial steps: ammonification → nitrification → denitrification"
- Add human inputs: Synthetic fertilizers (Haber-Bosch), fossil fuel combustion (NOx)
- Consequences: Eutrophication, acid rain (HNO₃), nitrous oxide (N₂O, greenhouse gas)

**[FIGURE: Nitrogen Cycle—Fixation Problem]**
- Zoom in on barrier: Atmospheric N₂ has triple bond; most organisms cannot break it
- Show *Rhizobium* in legume root nodule fixing N₂ → NH₃
- Show farmer benefit: Growing legumes (peas, beans) adds nitrogen to soil naturally
- Historical note: Ancient farmers rotated legumes and grain crops (pre-scientific understanding)
- Modern: Synthetic fixation (Haber-Bosch) now contributes ~50% of fixed N globally

**[FIGURE: Phosphorus Cycle]**
- Source: Phosphate rock (ancient ocean sediments on land)
- Weathering: Rock → Phosphate ion (PO₄³⁻) in soil
- Uptake: PO₄³⁻ → Organic P in plants/animals (nucleic acids, ATP, bones)
- Food chain: Plants → Herbivores → Carnivores
- Excretion/death/decomposition → Phosphate back to soil
- Runoff: Phosphate → Rivers → Lakes/Oceans
- Sedimentation: Phosphate settles → Ocean sediment
- Geological uplift: Ocean sediment → Land (millions of years; 20,000–100,000 yr residence time in ocean)
- Annotation: "No atmospheric reservoir; much slower cycle than N or C; often limiting in aquatic systems"
- Add human input: Fertilizer runoff, sewage discharge
- Consequences: Eutrophication (dead zones)

**[FIGURE: Eutrophication Mechanism]**
- Start: Clear water with low N and P
- Add nutrients: N and P runoff
- Result 1: Algal bloom (excess primary production)
- Result 2: Algae die and sink
- Result 3: Decomposition consumes dissolved oxygen
- Result 4: Hypoxia (low oxygen)
- Result 5: Fish kill, benthic fauna death
- Dead zone established
- Timeline: Can persist years even after nutrient input stops (sediments remain hypoxic, benthic recovery slow)

**[FIGURE: Gulf of Mexico Dead Zone]**
- Map showing Mississippi River delta
- Shaded area: Dead zone (8,463 square miles as of 2008)
- Source: Agricultural fertilizer runoff from Corn Belt (upper Mississippi basin)
- Annotation: "Largest dead zone in North America; eutrophication from nitrogen and phosphorus"

**[FIGURE: Chesapeake Bay Case Study]**
- Map showing Chesapeake Bay estuary
- Historical data: Oyster reefs covered 200,000 acres in 1700s → 36,000 acres today
- Oyster harvesting decline: 88% drop between 1982–2007
- Causes: Overharvesting + eutrophication + hypoxia
- Restoration: Disease-resistant oyster strains; oyster aquaculture (filter feeding benefits water quality)
- Challenges: Interstate cooperation needed (150+ rivers/streams from multiple states feed the bay)

**[FIGURE: Sulfur Cycle]**
- Atmosphere (SO₂)
- Sources: Volcanic activity, decomposition (H₂S), fossil fuel combustion
- Deposition on land: Precipitation (H₂SO₃ acid), fallout, rock weathering, geothermal vents
- Incorporation: SO₄²⁻ → Disulfide bonds in cysteine (proteins)
- Decomposition in anaerobic conditions → H₂S
- Ocean input: Runoff, atmospheric fallout, hydrothermal vents
- Marine ecosystems: SO₄²⁻ and H₂S used by chemoautotrophs (hydrothermal vents)
- Human disruption: Doubled SO₂ from coal and oil combustion
- Consequences: Acid rain (pH damage to lakes, monuments); sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) formation in atmosphere

**[FIGURE: Hydrothermal Vent Ecosystem]**
- Deep ocean, no sunlight
- Hot alkaline fluid rich in H₂S from vents
- Chemoautotrophic bacteria oxidize H₂S → chemical energy
- Food chain: Bacteria → Tube worms (with bacterial symbionts) → Crustaceans → Fish
- Annotation: "Chemosynthesis, not photosynthesis, powers this ecosystem; sulfur cycle provides energy"

### Disturbance & Resilience

**[FIGURE: Ecosystem Resistance and Resilience]**
- Left panel (Low diversity ecosystem): Forest fire → severe damage → slow recovery
- Right panel (High diversity ecosystem): Forest fire → moderate damage → faster recovery (more species available to recolonize)
- Y-axis: Ecosystem state; X-axis: Time after disturbance
- Annotation: "Diversity increases both resistance and resilience"

**[FIGURE: Resilience Threshold]**
- Y-axis: Ecosystem productivity/health
- X-axis: Disturbance intensity or duration
- Show stable state at high productivity
- As disturbance increases, ecosystem degrades but returns to high state
- Beyond critical threshold: Ecosystem shifts to degraded stable state
- If threshold crossed, recovery may take decades or be impossible
- Example: Overfished cod stocks; dead zones even after nutrient reduction

## Teaching Notes on Figures

1. **Pyramids:** Use colored areas (not just lines) to show relative energy/biomass/numbers. Make the inefficiency visceral—a wide base narrows dramatically by the third or fourth level.

2. **Cycles:** Use different colors for different forms of each element (CO₂ blue, organic C green, fossil fuels black, etc.). Make the distinction between fast and slow loops visually obvious.

3. **Bioaccumulation:** Use a progressive magnification or size increase of toxin molecules as you move up trophic levels. Make the concentration visible.

4. **Dead zones:** Show the mechanism step-by-step: input → bloom → death → O₂ consumption → anoxia. Temporal sequence is critical.

5. **Comparison figures:** Side-by-side images (diverse vs. monoculture, healthy estuary vs. dead zone, undisturbed vs. acid-rain-damaged forest) make trade-offs visually obvious.

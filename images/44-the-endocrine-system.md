## Figure 1: Hormone Classes by Chemistry and Speed

**Context:** Concept 1, after "The three classes" section.

**What it shows:**
- Three hormone types in a 2×2 grid:
  - **Peptide/protein**: water-soluble, surface receptor, fast (minutes), short duration
  - **Steroid**: lipid-soluble, intracellular receptor, slow (hours), long duration
  - **Amino acid-derived**: mixed (some fast/surface, some slow/intracellular)

- For each: show the membrane, the hormone approaching, the receptor location, and a timeline of effect (spike vs. plateau).

**Why it matters:** Visually anchors the speed-vs.-persistence trade-off. Drives home the membrane permeability concept.

## Figure 2: The Second-Messenger Cascade (cAMP)

**Context:** Concept 1, "Amplification" section.

**What it shows:**
- Hormone binding to surface receptor
- G-protein activation (GDP → GTP)
- Adenylyl cyclase activation
- ATP → cAMP conversion
- cAMP activating protein kinases
- Protein kinases phosphorylating substrates
- PDE breaking down cAMP

**Why it matters:** This is the mechanism that allows peptide hormones to achieve exponential amplification. Each step recruits more copies of the next step. Essential to understanding why peptide hormones produce large effects from small concentrations.

**Status:** Needed. Not present in source.

## Figure 3: The Hypothalamic-Pituitary Axis

**Context:** Concept 2, opening.

**What it shows:**
- Brain, showing hypothalamus position
- Hypothalamus connected to anterior pituitary via hypophyseal portal blood system
- Hypothalamus connected to posterior pituitary via nerve axons
- Anterior pituitary releasing hormones (TSH, ACTH, GH, FSH, LH, prolactin) into bloodstream
- Posterior pituitary releasing ADH and oxytocin
- Feedback arrows showing how target gland hormones inhibit hypothalamus and pituitary

**Why it matters:** The architecture of control. Shows why the system is called an "axis" — it is a two-way street with feedback.

**Status:** Needed. Likely available in sources but needs adaptation to show feedback loops clearly.

## Figure 4: Thyroid Hormone Synthesis

**Context:** Concept 2, "Thyroid's Ticking Clock" worked example.

**What it shows:**
- Thyroid follicle cross-section
- Iodide ions being transported into follicle cells
- Thyroglobulin protein with tyrosine residues
- Iodine atoms attaching to tyrosine (one, two, three, four iodines)
- T3 (three iodines) and T4 (four iodines) being released
- Conversion of T4 to T3 by removal of iodine in peripheral tissues

**Why it matters:** Concretizes the chemistry. Iodine is not magical; it's a chemical component that gets counted. If you have zero iodine, you get zero T3/T4. Explains goiter (TSH screaming at a gland with no iodine).

**Status:** Needed. Conceptually simple but worth a diagram.

## Figure 5: Insulin and Glucagon Regulation

**Context:** Concept 3, opening section and "Pancreatic Duet."

**What it shows:**
- Pancreatic islet with alpha and beta cells
- Beta cells releasing insulin in response to high glucose
- Insulin traveling to muscle/fat cells
- Glucose transporters inserting into membrane
- Glucose uptake by cells
- Alpha cells releasing glucagon in response to low glucose
- Glucagon triggering glycogen breakdown and gluconeogenesis
- Feedback: high glucose inhibits alpha cells, low glucose inhibits beta cells

**Why it matters:** Visual representation of the antagonistic pair. Shows the logic of the system: antagonists, opposite stimuli, self-correcting.

**Status:** Needed.

## Figure 6: Type 1 vs. Type 2 Diabetes

**Context:** Concept 3, worked example on Type 1 and discussion of Type 2.

**What it shows:**
- **Type 1**: Beta cells destroyed (X mark), no insulin release, glucose piling up in blood, glucose spilling into urine, cells starving, ketone production
- **Type 2**: Beta cells present, releasing insulin (but high levels), cells not responding (resistance symbolized), insulin levels climbing, glucose still high

**Why it matters:** Contrasts the two pathways to hyperglycemia. Type 1 is hormone deficiency. Type 2 is hormone resistance. Different problems, different solutions.

**Status:** Needed.

## Figure 7: The Two-Phase Stress Response

**Context:** Concept 4, opening section.

**What it shows:**
- **Phase 1 (seconds)**: Threat perceived → Sympathetic nervous system → Adrenal medulla → Epinephrine/norepinephrine release → Heart rate, blood glucose, breathing rate spike
- **Phase 2 (hours)**: If threat persists → Hypothalamus → Anterior pituitary → ACTH → Adrenal cortex → Cortisol release → Gluconeogenesis, protein breakdown, immune suppression

- Timeline should show speed (phase 1 fast) vs. duration (phase 1 minutes, phase 2 hours).

**Why it matters:** Shows the architecture of the stress response. Two different timescales, two different chemistry, two different purposes.

**Status:** Needed.

## Figure 8: Cortisol's Systemic Effects in Chronic Stress

**Context:** Concept 4, "Cortisol's Collateral Damage" worked example.

**What it shows:**
- Chronic elevated cortisol at center
- Arrows radiating to effects:
  - Liver: gluconeogenesis, hyperglycemia
  - Pancreas: high insulin, then beta cell exhaustion
  - Muscle: protein breakdown, muscle wasting
  - Immune: suppression, infection risk
  - Hippocampus: shrinkage, memory impairment
  - Adipose: fat accumulation (metabolic syndrome)
  - Blood vessels: atherosclerosis risk

**Why it matters:** Chronic cortisol touches many systems. Shows why chronic stress produces diverse health problems. No single system is spared.

**Status:** Needed. A "hub and spoke" diagram works well.

## Notes on Diagram Style

- Use consistent color coding: **peptide hormones** = blue, **steroid hormones** = yellow, **amine hormones** = orange, **water** = cyan, **lipid** = red
- Arrows show direction of action (solid) and feedback (dashed, often red)
- Timescales should be explicit when shown (seconds, minutes, hours)
- Chemical structures unnecessary except for iodine in thyroglobulin (where the number of atoms is the point)

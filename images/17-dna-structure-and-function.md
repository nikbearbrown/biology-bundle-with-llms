## Concept 1: Transformation and Genetic Material

### Image 1A: Griffith's Transformation Experiment
**Purpose:** Show the logic of the transformation experiments step by step.

**Visual structure:**
- Panel 1: Rough strain injected → mice survive.
- Panel 2: Smooth strain injected → mice die.
- Panel 3: Heat-killed smooth strain injected → mice survive.
- Panel 4: Live rough + heat-killed smooth injected → mice die, smooth cells recovered.

**Key features:**
- Color coding: rough = tan/brown, smooth = red (with capsule envelope).
- Show what enters (whole bacteria or extracted material).
- Outcome labeling: ✓ (mice live) or ✗ (mice die).
- Final result: label the recovered smooth cells to show transformation.

**Student should notice:** 
Something *invisible* passes from dead smooth cells to live rough cells and changes them. The transforming principle is not the capsule itself (dead cells have capsules but don't protect mice). It must be molecular.

### Image 1B: Chargaff's Data as Evidence of Base Pairing
**Purpose:** Show how numerical patterns reveal structure without seeing it directly.

**Visual structure:**
- Bar chart or stacked bar chart showing % composition of A, T, G, C across three organisms (human, *E. coli*, *Streptococcus*).
- Color code: A (blue), T (orange), G (green), C (purple).
- For each organism, show that A bar = T bar, G bar = C bar.
- Different organisms have different A-T to G-C ratios, but the pairing rule holds universally.

**Annotation:**
- "A = T always" with arrow linking adenine and thymine bars.
- "G = C always" with arrow linking guanine and cytosine bars.
- "But A-T ratio differs by species" comparing human to bacteria.

**Student should notice:**
The equality is not random — it's a chemical signature. What chemical process produces this signature? Answer: if two strands are complementary, every A on one strand pairs with a T on the other, so total A = total T in the molecule.

## Concept 2: The Double Helix — Structure as Explanation

### Image 2A: DNA Nucleotide Structure (Building Block)
**Purpose:** Show the three components of a nucleotide before assembling them into a strand.

**Visual structure:**
- Three labeled parts arranged around a central nucleotide:
  - **Nitrogenous base** (with purine and pyrimidine examples): adenine (purine, two rings), thymine (pyrimidine, one ring), guanine (purine), cytosine (pyrimidine).
  - **Deoxyribose sugar** (five-carbon sugar) with carbons labeled 1', 2', 3', 4', 5'.
  - **Phosphate group** (PO₄).
- Show how they connect: base to 1' carbon, phosphate to 5' carbon.

**Key features:**
- Color-code atoms by element (carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, phosphorus).
- Highlight the positions where bonds form (1' for base, 5' for phosphate).
- Show the 3' carbon with a free OH group (important for later polymerization).

**Student should notice:**
These three parts are simple and modular. They combine in different orders (different bases) to create variety. The sugar-phosphate backbone is identical in all nucleotides; the base varies.

### Image 2B: DNA Strand Assembly — Sugar-Phosphate Backbone
**Purpose:** Show how nucleotides link to form a continuous strand.

**Visual structure:**
- Linear chain of 4–5 nucleotides linked together.
- Each nucleotide shown with base (abbreviated A, T, G, C), sugar, and phosphate.
- Bonds highlighted: phosphodiester bond between 5' phosphate of one sugar and 3' OH of the next.
- Label the 5' end (at the phosphate) and 3' end (at the OH).
- Direction arrow showing 5' → 3' convention.

**Key features:**
- Show the continuous backbone of alternating sugars and phosphates.
- Bases sticking out from the sugar, ready to pair with a complementary strand.
- Emphasize that the backbone is the same regardless of which bases are present.

**Student should notice:**
The strand has directionality (5' to 3'). The bases are on the outside at this point — they will move inside when the helix forms. Every nucleotide contributes the same backbone; information comes from the base sequence.

### Image 2C: Complementary Base Pairing — Geometry and Hydrogen Bonds
**Purpose:** Show why certain bases pair and others do not.

**Visual structure:**
- Side-by-side comparison of the four possible base pairings:
  1. **A-T pairing** (shown, with two hydrogen bonds marked).
  2. **G-C pairing** (shown, with three hydrogen bonds marked).
  3. **A-G pairing** (shown as impossible or misaligned).
  4. **T-C pairing** (shown as impossible or misaligned).
- Each pairing should indicate width (distance across the pair).

**Key features:**
- Use dashed lines for hydrogen bonds.
- Show the purine-pyrimidine size difference: purines are wider (two rings), pyrimidines are narrower (one ring).
- Indicate that A-T spacing = G-C spacing = 2 nm (uniform diameter).
- Mark the geometry that allows strong bonding (A-T, G-C) vs. misalignment (A-G, T-C).

**Student should notice:**
Base pairing is not arbitrary — it follows from geometry. A purine must pair with a pyrimidine to maintain uniform width. Only certain pairings form stable hydrogen bonds. This is engineered geometry, not random chemistry.

### Image 2D: The Double Helix — Full Structure
**Purpose:** Show the three-dimensional shape and how all parts fit together.

**Visual structure:**
- 3D perspective view or "ribbon" diagram of the double helix.
- Two strands in different colors (e.g., blue and orange) twisted around a central axis.
- Sugar-phosphate backbone visible on the outside (thick ribbons).
- Base pairs stacked inside, perpendicular to the helix axis.
- Major and minor grooves indicated (the wider and narrower gaps between the strands).
- Annotation: spacing of 0.34 nm between base pairs, 3.4 nm per turn, 10 base pairs per turn, 2 nm diameter.

**Key features:**
- Show the bases are protected inside, not exposed.
- Indicate the right-handed helical twist.
- Mark the antiparallel orientation: one strand 5' → 3' (top to bottom), the other 3' → 5'.
- Color-code the grooves: major and minor grooves have different chemical properties.

**Student should notice:**
The structure is compact and symmetrical. The bases are hidden inside — this explains stability. The grooves (particularly the major groove) are where proteins will eventually bind to read the code. The antiparallel orientation creates the asymmetry that leads to Okazaki fragments.

### Image 2E: Rosalind Franklin's Photo 51
**Purpose:** Connect the abstract model to real experimental evidence.

**Visual structure:**
- Photograph of Photo 51 (or high-quality reproduction).
- Annotate key features: X-shaped pattern of spots, central cross indicating helical structure, spacing of reflections indicating periodicity.
- Overlay a diagram showing how the spots correspond to the helical pitch, base pair spacing, and helix diameter.

**Key features:**
- Label the spots that indicate a helical structure (not a ladder or a random coil).
- Show the relationship between the photo pattern and the numerical values (pitch, spacing, diameter).
- Brief caption acknowledging Franklin's contributions and the ethical complexities of her role in the discovery.

**Student should notice:**
Abstract chemistry becomes concrete when you see the experimental data. The X-ray pattern is not a guess — it's physical evidence of a helical structure with specific dimensions. This is how Watson and Crick built their model: by explaining all the data (Chargaff's rules, Franklin's X-rays, chemical structure) in a single coherent structure.

## Concept 3: Semiconservative Replication

### Image 3A: Three Replication Models — Predictions Illustrated
**Purpose:** Show how each model makes different predictions about the outcome of replication.

**Visual structure:**
- Three columns, one per model: Conservative, Semiconservative, Dispersive.
- Start with one double helix (both strands colored the same, e.g., both blue to indicate "heavy" or "original").
- After one replication, show the predicted outcome for each model:
  - **Conservative:** Original molecule intact; new molecule is entirely new (original strands both blue, new molecule is two new/light strands).
  - **Semiconservative:** Each new molecule has one original strand (blue) and one new strand (light).
  - **Dispersive:** Each new molecule is a mix of old and new segments throughout.

**Key features:**
- Use color to track original (heavy) vs. new (light) DNA.
- After a second replication, show the predicted distributions for each model.
- For semiconservative, clearly show that one molecule is hybrid (1 blue, 1 light) and one is fully light (2 light strands).

**Student should notice:**
Different models make different, testable predictions. The experiments (like Meselson-Stahl) can distinguish between them by looking at the density of the products.

---

### Image 3B: Meselson-Stahl Experiment — The Centrifuge and the Bands
**Purpose:** Show the experimental technique and how it reveals replication mode.

**Visual structure:**
- Left side: illustration of density gradient centrifugation.
  - A centrifuge tube filled with a salt gradient (more dense at bottom).
  - DNA of different densities floats at different heights.
  - Show three DNA samples at different positions: heavy ($^{15}N$/^{15}N$), hybrid ($^{15}N$/^{14}N$), light ($^{14}N$/^{14}N$).

- Right side: timeline of the Meselson-Stahl experiment.
  - Generation 0: Start with all-heavy DNA. Single band at the heavy position.
  - Generation 1: After one replication in normal nitrogen. Single band at intermediate (hybrid) position.
  - Generation 2: After two replications in normal nitrogen. Two bands: one hybrid, one light.

**Key features:**
- Color-code the density gradient (denser toward bottom).
- Show where each type of DNA floats in the gradient.
- Use the band positions to "read" the result: one band = all molecules are the same density.
- Annotation: "This result only fits the semiconservative model."

**Student should notice:**
Experimental data tell a story. The intermediate-density band after one generation is the key observation — it proves that each new DNA molecule contains one old strand and one new strand. This observation rules out both conservative and dispersive models.

### Image 3C: Leading and Lagging Strand Synthesis — The Replication Fork
**Purpose:** Show why the two strands must be synthesized differently.

**Visual structure:**
- Diagram of a replication fork (Y-shaped structure where DNA is being unwound and copied).
- Show the two parental strands with their 5' → 3' and 3' → 5' orientations.
- As the fork moves in one direction (e.g., left to right):
  - One template strand (3' → 5') allows continuous synthesis of the leading strand (5' → 3') in the direction of fork movement.
  - The other template strand (5' → 3') requires discontinuous synthesis of the lagging strand, synthesized in fragments away from the fork.
- Label the leading strand (continuous synthesis, single primer) and lagging strand (fragmented synthesis, multiple primers).

**Key features:**
- Arrows showing the direction of fork movement and the direction of new strand synthesis.
- Highlight that DNA polymerase only works 5' → 3', never 3' → 5'.
- Show the RNA primers (small segments) that prime each lagging strand fragment.
- Label the Okazaki fragments (short pieces on the lagging strand).

**Student should notice:**
The geometry of the double helix (antiparallel strands) combined with the directionality of DNA polymerase (5' → 3' only) creates an asymmetry. One strand can be made continuously, the other must be made in pieces. This is not a flaw — it's how the cell solves the problem of copying both strands fast.

### Image 3D: Okazaki Fragments — Joining Them Together
**Purpose:** Show how short fragments are synthesized and then joined into a continuous strand.

**Visual structure:**
- Lagging strand with multiple Okazaki fragments (each ~1,000 nucleotides in prokaryotes).
- Each fragment initiated by a short RNA primer (shown in a different color, e.g., red).
- Arrows showing the direction of synthesis for each fragment (5' → 3', away from the replication fork).
- After synthesis, show the joining process:
  - DNA polymerase I removes the RNA primers and fills gaps with DNA.
  - DNA ligase seals the nicks between adjacent fragments, forming phosphodiester bonds.

**Key features:**
- Show that each fragment is a "normal" piece of DNA — not damaged or temporary.
- Indicate the gap between fragments before ligase seals them (nick = broken sugar-phosphate backbone).
- Use different colors for RNA primer, gap, and final sealed DNA.
- Timeline or sequence annotation showing synthesis → primer removal → gap filling → ligation.

**Student should notice:**
Fragmentation is a strategy, not a problem. It allows fast replication of the lagging strand. The fragments are then "glued" together seamlessly by ligase. The final lagging strand is continuous and identical in every way to the leading strand (same sequence, same structure, same stability).

## Synthesis: Integration Image

### Image S1: DNA Replication Cycle — From Structure to Accuracy to Evolution
**Purpose:** Connect the molecular mechanisms to organismal-level consequences.

**Visual structure:**
- Three linked panels or a single infographic:

**Panel 1: Molecular (Zoom in)**
- Double helix with complementary base pairs highlighted.
- Two strands separating (helicase).
- Each strand serving as a template.
- New strands synthesized with correct bases (A-T, G-C pairing).
- Result: two identical DNA molecules.

**Panel 2: Cellular (Zoom out)**
- Many DNA molecules replicating simultaneously in a cell.
- Error rate indicated: 1 per billion nucleotides (after proofreading).
- Cell division: each daughter cell gets a copy.
- Rare errors (mutations) introduced.

**Panel 3: Organismal/Evolutionary (Zoom out further)**
- Population of organisms with slightly different DNA sequences (due to rare mutations).
- Natural selection acting on variation.
- Over many generations: evolution in action.
- Caption: "The error rate is not perfect—it's near-perfect. Perfect replication would stop evolution. Too many errors would kill the organism. DNA has found the balance."

**Key features:**
- Use zoom metaphor to show scales (molecule → cell → organism → population).
- Show proofreading mechanism briefly (DNA polymerase backing up, removing mismatches).
- Quantify the error rate and connect it to evolutionary timescales.

**Student should notice:**
The structure of DNA (complementary base pairing) is not just about copying — it's about accuracy. That accuracy is good enough to maintain life but loose enough to allow evolution. The same molecule does both: it preserves information and allows information to change. This is the deep elegance of the system.

## Technical Notes for Image Production

1. **Color conventions:**
   - Adenine (A): blue
   - Thymine (T): orange
   - Guanine (G): green
   - Cytosine (C): purple
   - Heavy nitrogen ($^{15}N$): darker shade
   - Light nitrogen ($^{14}N$): lighter shade
   - RNA primers: red or pink
   - Protein enzymes (helicase, polymerase, ligase): neutral gray or tan

2. **Annotations:**
   - Every label should be in the student's vocabulary (define jargon or point to where it's explained in the chapter).
   - Include scale markers and measurements (nm, base pairs, turn frequency) where relevant.
   - Use captions that ask questions or highlight the student's learning objectives, not just describe the picture.

3. **Accessibility:**
   - Ensure sufficient contrast for color-blind readers (avoid red-green combinations without additional markers).
   - Provide alt text for each image describing the key features and what the student should notice.
   - Consider including a "what you should see" callout box for each image.

4. **Integration:**
   - Each image should be referenced explicitly in the chapter text.
   - A caption and a caption credit should accompany each image.
   - Images should appear near the section where they are discussed (not all at the end).

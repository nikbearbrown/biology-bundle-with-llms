## Scene-setting figure: Wound progression (opening)

**Placement**: Following the opening section, before "The First Defense"

**Content**: Cross-section illustration of skin showing a wound at day 1 post-injury. Include:
- The cut itself, with bacteria visible at the edge
- Neutrophils (small cells with multilobed nuclei) clustering at the wound
- Blood vessel opening (endothelial cells retracting)
- Fluid leakage (swelling in tissue)
- Macrophages (large cells) engulfing bacteria
- Complement proteins (depicted as a cascade of activating molecules)

**Caption**: A kitchen wound at 12-24 hours post-injury. Neutrophils (small, abundant cells with multilobed nuclei) have flooded the wound in response to inflammatory signals. Macrophages engulf bacteria and dead cells. Blood vessels have opened to allow immune cell passage. The redness, swelling, and warmth are visible manifestations of immune machinery at work—not signs of failure but signs of defense.

## Barrier defenses schematic

**Placement**: After "The First Defense: Barriers Before Battle" section

**Content**: A diagram showing the body's surfaces and their defenses:
- Skin with an impenetrable barrier illustration, showing microbiota layer
- Eyes with tears flowing and lysozyme molecules
- Mouth/throat with mucus and ciliated cells
- Lungs with ciliated epithelium
- Stomach with acid (pH 1.5) and bacteria being dissolved
- Urinary tract with urine flow flushing pathogens

**Caption**: Physical and chemical barriers are the first line of defense. The skin is an impassable barrier; the stomach's acidity kills most pathogens; tears and mucus physically trap invaders; cilia row them toward exits. These defenses require no memory and no specificity—they work the same way whether you encounter a pathogen for the first time or the thousandth.

## Innate immune response mechanisms (3-panel figure)

**Placement**: In "The Innate Immune Response" section

**Panel 1 - Macrophage recognition and engulfment**

**Content**: Macrophage with pattern recognition receptors on its surface binding to PAMP (pathogen-associated molecular pattern) on bacterial surface. The bacterium is shown entering the cell in a vesicle.

**Caption**: A macrophage recognizes a pathogen through pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) that bind to molecular signatures (PAMPs) found on bacteria but not on host cells. This binding is the signal: engulf and destroy.

---

**Panel 2 - Complement cascade**

**Content**: A pathogen surface showing sequential binding of complement proteins (C1 → C3 → C5 → MAC). Show the membrane attack complex (MAC) as a ring of proteins boring a pore in the pathogen membrane, with the pathogen's contents leaking out.

**Caption**: The complement cascade is a chain reaction of protein activation. Each activated protein activates the next. The cascade marks the pathogen for destruction (opsonization) and bores holes in pathogen membranes. The entire cascade progresses in minutes.

---

**Panel 3 - Natural killer cell recognition and killing**

**Content**: Two scenarios: (A) healthy cell with normal MHC I on surface, NK cell touching it with inhibitory signal active, NK cell moving on. (B) virus-infected cell with reduced MHC I, NK cell contacting it, perforin and granzyme being released into the target cell.

**Caption**: Natural killer cells patrol the bloodstream scanning for the presence of MHC I molecules. A healthy cell displaying normal MHC I sends inhibitory signals—*leave this cell alone*. A virus-infected cell with abnormally low MHC I sends no inhibitory signal, triggering the NK cell to release perforin (boring a hole) and granzyme (triggering apoptosis). The missing self signal is death warrant for the infected cell.

## Antigen presentation and clonal selection

**Placement**: In "The Specificity Problem and the Adaptive Response" section

**Content**: A multi-step illustration:
1. Dendritic cell engulfs pathogen and migrates to lymph node
2. Dendritic cell displays antigen fragments on MHC II molecules
3. Many T cell clones in lymph node; most do not match
4. One T cell whose receptor matches the antigen activates
5. That T cell undergoes rapid division → thousands of clones

**Caption**: Clonal selection: The adaptive system does not instruct cells what to make. Instead, it selects them. A dendritic cell presents pathogen fragments to thousands of T cells. Only a tiny fraction have receptors that match the antigen. When they do, they activate and proliferate explosively, creating clones—thousands of identical cells specific to this one pathogen. This is how the body generates specificity from diversity.

## Cell-mediated immunity: CTL killing

**Placement**: In "The Two Adaptive Pathways: Cellular and Humoral" section, under Cell-mediated immunity

**Content**: A virus-infected cell displaying viral antigen on MHC I. A CTL recognizing the complex. Perforin boring holes. Granzyme entering the cell and triggering apoptosis. Show the cell rounding up, blebbing, and fragmenting.

**Caption**: A cytotoxic T lymphocyte recognizes a viral antigen displayed on the surface of an infected cell. The CTL releases perforin, boring pores in the target cell membrane. Granzyme flows through the pores and triggers the infected cell's programmed death (apoptosis). The virus does not get a chance to replicate further.

## Antibody structure and diversity

**Placement**: In "The Two Adaptive Pathways: Cellular and Humoral" section, under Humoral immunity

**Content**: 
- Top: Y-shaped antibody showing heavy chains and light chains, variable regions at the tips
- Middle: Detail of antigen binding site showing variable domains interacting
- Bottom: Multiple different antibodies with different shapes at the binding tips, illustrating diversity

**Caption**: An antibody is a Y-shaped protein made of two heavy chains and two light chains. The variable regions at the tips of the Y form the antigen-binding site. The constant regions determine the antibody class and function. The variable regions are generated by random recombination of gene segments, allowing the body to produce antibodies against virtually any pathogen.

## Antibody functions (4-panel figure)

**Placement**: In "The Two Adaptive Pathways: Cellular and Humoral" section

**Panel 1 - Neutralization**: Antibody binds toxin, blocking it from damaging cells

**Panel 2 - Opsonization**: Antibodies coat bacteria, marking them for phagocytosis

**Panel 3 - Complement activation**: Antibody bound to antigen recruits complement cascade

**Panel 4 - Agglutination**: Antibodies link multiple bacteria together, immobilizing them

**Caption**: Antibodies perform multiple roles in immune defense: they neutralize toxins and prevent pathogens from entering cells; they mark pathogens for destruction by coating them (opsonization); they activate the complement cascade; and they agglutinate (cluster) pathogens, preventing their spread.

## Immunological memory: Primary vs. secondary response

**Placement**: In "Immune Memory: The System That Learns" section

**Content**: A graph showing two response curves:
- Primary response: gradual rise in IgM (peaking around day 10-14), then switch to IgG (which builds slowly)
- Secondary response: rapid surge in IgG (within days), reaching much higher levels than primary

Label the axis: Days (0-60 on x), Antibody level (arbitrary units on y)

Annotate: "Memory cells awaken immediately during reinfection" pointing to the steep secondary curve

**Caption**: The primary immune response (first encounter with a pathogen) takes weeks. IgM appears first (weak binding), then IgG (strong, long-lived). During the secondary response (reinfection with the same pathogen), memory cells differentiate into effector cells immediately, producing a surge of IgG that is much faster and stronger. This is why vaccines work—they induce a primary response, creating memory cells, so the secondary response is ready if the actual pathogen arrives.

## Tolerance and autoimmunity: Thymic selection

**Placement**: In "The Trade-Off: Tolerance and Its Failure" section

**Content**: Two-panel figure:
- Left: Normal thymic selection. Show thymocytes, some with TCRs binding self-antigen (→ death by negative selection), some with TCRs binding MHC with foreign-like peptide (→ survival by positive selection)
- Right: Failed tolerance. Show a self-reactive T cell escaping the thymus and attacking self-tissue (pancreatic beta cell in type 1 diabetes example)

**Caption**: In the thymus, T cells that bind self-antigen too strongly (left, red cells) are eliminated by negative selection. T cells that bind self-MHC in the context of harmless or absent peptide survive. Occasionally, self-reactive T cells escape selection (right). If they target self-tissue, autoimmunity results. Type 1 diabetes occurs when T cells attack insulin-producing beta cells.

## HIV and immunodeficiency: CD4+ T cell depletion

**Placement**: In "The Trade-Off: Tolerance and Its Failure" section, under Immunodeficiency

**Content**: A graph showing CD4+ T cell count over time:
- Y-axis: CD4+ count (cells/mm³), normal range 500-1500
- X-axis: Time (years)
- Line starting normal, declining gradually over 5-10 years to <50

Annotate: Opportunistic infections increase as CD4 count falls

**Caption**: HIV specifically targets CD4+ helper T cells. Over years, the infection gradually depletes the CD4+ population. Without helper T cells, the rest of the immune system cannot function. Opportunistic infections (Pneumocystis pneumonia, tuberculosis, certain cancers) appear as CD4 counts fall below thresholds. Modern antiretroviral drugs can halt the decline if treatment begins before severe immunodeficiency.

## Integration: The innate-adaptive conversation

**Placement**: In "Integration: How Two Systems Become One" section

**Content**: A timeline diagram showing:
- Hours 0-24: Macrophages engulf pathogen, release cytokines
- Cytokine signals guide dendritic cells to lymphoid tissue
- Days 1-7: Adaptive system activates
- Th1 cytokines (if intracellular pathogen) → CTLs activate
- Th2 cytokines (if extracellular pathogen) → B cells activate
- Antibodies and/or CTLs target the specific pathogen
- Innate system provides continuous background support

**Caption**: The innate and adaptive immune systems are not sequential—they are concurrent and conversational. Innate cells release cytokines that signal *this type of threat has arrived*, directing which adaptive response dominates (cell-mediated or humoral). The adaptive system then targets the pathogen specifically while innate responses continue in the background.

## Size and scale: Immune cells in context

**Placement**: Optional, after Chapter Summary, for comparison

**Content**: A visual size comparison:
- Virus: 20-300 nanometers
- Bacterium: 1-10 micrometers
- Neutrophil: 10-15 micrometers
- Macrophage: 20-30 micrometers
- Lymphocyte: 6-8 micrometers

Show them to scale relative to each other, perhaps overlaid on a human hair (70 micrometers).

**Caption**: Scale matters in immunology. A virus is so small that hundreds can fit inside a single cell. A neutrophil is roughly the same size as the bacteria it engulfs. Macrophages are substantially larger and can contain multiple bacteria. These size differences determine mechanism: neutrophils can engulf bacteria but not viruses; macrophages can phagocytose both but viruses replicate inside cells beyond antibody reach.

## Summary diagram: The two immune systems

**Placement**: In Chapter Summary, or as a standalone visual reference

**Content**: A side-by-side comparison table format shown as a visual diagram:

**Left side - Innate Immunity**:
- Timing: Minutes to hours
- Specificity: Generic (all pathogens treated the same)
- Mechanisms: Physical barriers, phagocytosis, complement, NK cells
- Memory: No
- Examples: Skin, neutrophils, macrophages, complement, interferon

**Right side - Adaptive Immunity**:
- Timing: Days to weeks
- Specificity: Specific (tailored to each pathogen)
- Mechanisms: T cells, B cells, antibodies
- Memory: Yes
- Examples: CTLs, helper T cells, plasma cells, memory cells

**Center - Integration**:
- Cytokine signaling directs adaptive response
- Adaptive system amplifies innate response
- Both systems active simultaneously

**Caption**: The immune system comprises two complementary defenses. The innate response is fast but generic. The adaptive response is slower but specific and remembers. They are not sequential stages but concurrent partners in a constant conversation.

# Figures: Conservation Biology and Biodiversity

## Suggested Figures (Attenborough × Feynman v1.1)

### Figure 1: Lake Victoria Map and Timeline
- **Caption:** Lake Victoria (68,900 km²) in the Great Rift Valley. Timeline showing: cichlid diversity peak (~1980s, 500 species), Nile perch introduction (1963), perch population surge (1980s), species collapse (1980s–1990s, ~200 species lost in ~10 years).
- **Type:** Geographic map + timeline overlay
- **Purpose:** Open chapter with specific place, specific crisis; ground the abstraction of extinction in concrete geography

### Figure 2: Three Levels of Biodiversity
- **Caption:** Biodiversity at three scales. (A) Genetic diversity: alleles within a population of lions (coat color, disease resistance variation). (B) Species diversity: five fish species from Lake Victoria with specialized feeding strategies (planktivore, algae-scraper, insectivore, egg-eater, larval insect hunter). (C) Ecosystem diversity: three distinct habitats (tropical rainforest, coral reef, temperate prairie) with characteristic species assemblages and abiotic conditions.
- **Type:** Three-panel comparative diagram
- **Purpose:** Teach the specification move; show why measuring all three matters; illustrate loss at each level

### Figure 3: Latitudinal Biodiversity Gradient
- **Caption:** Species richness increases toward the equator across major taxonomic groups (vertebrates, insects, plants). Tropical regions contain ~80% of described eukaryotic life despite occupying smaller fraction of land. Hypotheses for pattern: older tropical ecosystems (less ice age impact), greater solar energy (more niches), more stable climate (specialization advantage), higher ecosystem complexity.
- **Type:** Map showing richness gradient + histogram by latitude
- **Purpose:** Show where biodiversity is concentrated; ground why tropics are hotspots and also under greatest threat

### Figure 4: Extinction Rate Comparison
- **Caption:** Background extinction rate (1 E/MSY, gray baseline) vs. current estimate (100–1000 E/MSY, red range). Historical record method for birds (26 E/MSY, documented since 1500) and habitat-loss extrapolation (500 E/MSY by 2100). Current rate 100–1000× baseline; comparable to mass extinction events in fossil record.
- **Type:** Bar chart with error ranges; timeline overlay showing five previous mass extinctions
- **Purpose:** Visceral comparison of natural vs. anthropogenic rates; ground that we are in a sixth mass extinction

### Figure 5: The Five Mass Extinctions
- **Caption:** Fossil record showing five mass extinction events: End-Ordovician (450 Ma, 85% marine loss), Late Devonian (375 Ma, 75% loss), End-Permian (251 Ma, 96% marine/70% terrestrial, most severe), End-Triassic (205 Ma, 76–80%), End-Cretaceous (65.5 Ma, asteroid impact + volcanism, 75% loss). Recovery times: 30 Ma (Permian), 10 Ma (Cretaceous). Current extinction: first anthropogenic, first on decadal timescale.
- **Type:** Timeline with species richness curve showing plummets and recovery slopes
- **Purpose:** Show historical precedent; emphasize recovery timescales (millions of years); highlight uniqueness of current event

### Figure 6: HIPPO Threats Interaction Diagram
- **Caption:** Habitat Destruction + Invasive Species + Pollution + Population Growth + Overharvesting. Lake Victoria example: habitat degradation (agriculture), invasive Nile perch (predator), overfishing (pressure). Interactions compound; removing one might not prevent collapse if others are strong. Climate change amplifies each threat.
- **Type:** Five-node network diagram with bidirectional arrows; Lake Victoria as worked example
- **Purpose:** Show mechanistic thinking; avoid listing threats as independent; emphasize cumulative effects

### Figure 7: Biodiversity Hotspots Map
- **Caption:** 34 recognized biodiversity hotspots (shaded regions). Contain ~50% of endemic plants globally. High endemism + high human-driven habitat disturbance. Sumatra, Borneo, Sundaland (orangutans, Asian elephant, tiger). Madagascar. Atlantic Forest. Congo Basin. East African Mountains. Examples of triple threat: high biodiversity, high endemism, high deforestation rates.
- **Type:** World map with hotspot regions shaded; insets showing endemic species in 2–3 hotspots
- **Purpose:** Show where conservation triage is most critical; illustrate asymmetry between biodiversity and habitat protection

### Figure 8: Invasive Species Case Study
- **Caption:** (A) Nile perch size and predatory capability vs. Lake Victoria cichlid morphology (small, specialized). (B) Kudzu growth rate (1 foot/day) and range in southeastern U.S. (7 million acres). (C) Brown tree snake on Guam: entry point (aircraft 1950) and spread, showing extirpation of endemic bird and reptile species. Invasive species particularly damaging to islands/lakes (endemic species lack predator/competitor history).
- **Type:** Three-panel case study with range maps and timelines
- **Purpose:** Make abstract threat concrete; show rapid ecosystem restructuring in real time

### Figure 9: Climate Change and Range Shifts
- **Caption:** European bird species shifted northward ~91 km in recent decades; optimal shift based on warming ~182 km (deficit = lag). Phenological mismatch example: breeding time no longer synchronizes with food availability. Polar bear range overlap with grizzly bears (new species contact, viable hybrid offspring). Sea ice decline in Arctic (polar bear seal-hunting platform loss).
- **Type:** Map showing current and expected ranges; timeline of ice coverage decline; phenological mismatch graph
- **Purpose:** Show mechanism of climate impact; illustrate lag between ecosystem change and species response

### Figure 10: Pharmaceutical Diversity and Drug Origin
- **Caption:** Modern drugs derived from natural compounds: aspirin (willow bark), codeine (opium poppy), digoxin (digitalis foxglove), vincristine (Madagascar periwinkle). Animal venoms: 5 FDA-approved drugs from snake/fish/scorpion toxins. 35% of new drugs (1981–2002) from natural compounds. ~1/3 of pharmaceutical R&D budget on natural compounds. Undiscovered species = lost drug opportunities.
- **Type:** Illustrated examples (plant/animal + structural formula + application) + bar chart showing % new drugs by origin
- **Purpose:** Make biodiversity value tangible and economic; show research gap

### Figure 11: Ecosystem Services Diagram
- **Caption:** Intact ecosystems provide: pollination ($1.6B annually, U.S.; 150 crops dependent), soil nutrient cycling (decomposer + mycorrhizal networks), pest control (predators + parasitoids), flood/water regulation (wetlands, floodplain forests), climate regulation (carbon storage, evapotranspiration). Loss of ecosystem = loss of these services; replacement costs mount (artificial irrigation, artificial pollination, synthetic fertilizers).
- **Type:** Central ecosystem icon with radial arrows showing service flows and economic values
- **Purpose:** Bridge between abstract ecosystem diversity and concrete human welfare

### Figure 12: Island Biogeography and Reserve Design
- **Caption:** Larger islands support more species (species-area relationship). Distance from mainland affects immigration rate. Applied to reserves: larger preserves support more species; connectivity via corridors enables gene flow; square/circular shapes minimize edge effects; buffer zones protect core habitat. Climate change challenges: reserve location becomes mismatched to species' shifting suitable climate.
- **Type:** Conceptual diagrams comparing reserve shapes; species-area curve; climate envelope shift illustration
- **Purpose:** Teach applied ecology principle; show both utility and limitation of protected areas

### Figure 13: Yellowstone Wolf Reintroduction Cascade
- **Caption:** Gray wolves reintroduced 1995. Effects: (1) wolf predation on elk → elk population decline; (2) riparian vegetation recovery (less browsing); (3) vegetation supports herbivores, insects; (4) coyote decline; (5) small predator/scavenger increase (from reduced coyote pressure); (6) wolf kill carrion feeds scavenger guild. Ecosystem diversity increased through keystone species restoration. Mechanism: trophic cascading, not just adding one species.
- **Type:** Flow diagram showing trophic cascade; before/after comparison photos of riparian vegetation
- **Purpose:** Show that restoration ecology works via mechanism; emphasize ecosystem-level thinking vs. species-only focus

### Figure 14: Conservation Strategy Comparison
- **Caption:** Protected areas (11.5% land, 2003): static, location-bound, vulnerable to climate change. Habitat restoration: dynamic, can address underlying mechanisms, requires long-term commitment. Captive breeding: limited scale, primarily educational. Legislation (CITES, ESA): necessary but procedurally limited. Integrated approach: most likely to scale.
- **Type:** Table or matrix comparing strategies by scale, effectiveness, limitations, timescale to impact
- **Purpose:** Synthesize conservation tools; show no single tool sufficient; emphasize integration

---

## Implementation Notes

- All figures should follow Attenborough × Feynman visual voice: specific detail, mechanism visible, comparisons structured
- Avoid generic "biodiversity" icons; use real species, real places, real numbers
- Colors: use gradient for latitudinal richness (darker at equator), red for extinction/threat, green for recovery/restoration
- Typography: clear legends; no jargon without definition in caption
- Source attribution where possible (e.g., "Mora et al. 2011" for species estimates)

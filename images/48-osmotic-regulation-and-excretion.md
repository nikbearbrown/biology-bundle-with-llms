# Image Briefs: Osmotic Regulation and Excretion

## Figure 1: Three Cell Environments (Concept 1)

**Location:** Concept 1, after the "Watch what happens when you place a cell in three environments" paragraph.

**Purpose:** Show the mechanism of osmosis by illustrating cell behavior in hypotonic, isotonic, and hypertonic solutions.

**Description:** Three panels, each showing a red blood cell or plant cell in a different solution:

1. **Hypotonic (left):** Cell in pure water. Cell is swollen, membrane bulging outward (or in a plant cell, large turgor pressure, chloroplasts pressed against the cell wall). Annotation: "Water moves in → cell swells." Show net arrow of water movement pointing inward.

2. **Isotonic (center):** Cell in 0.9% saline. Cell appears normal, size unchanged. Water moves equally in and out. Annotation: "Water moves in and out at equal rates → cell maintains size."

3. **Hypertonic (right):** Cell in 3% saline. Cell is shrunken, membrane pulled inward. In plant cell, the membrane pulls away from cell wall (plasmolysis visible). Annotation: "Water moves out → cell shrinks." Show net arrow of water movement pointing outward.

**What the student should notice:**
- The cell's size and shape change dramatically based on the environment.
- The direction of water movement is toward the higher solute concentration.
- The membrane itself is the barrier; the cell cannot prevent water from moving, but the osmotic pressure is a consequence of solute concentration, not a force.
- In the hypertonic case, the distortion (especially plasmolysis) shows that water movement is real and powerful.

**Visual cues:**
- Use contrasting colors for the three environments (blue for hypotonic, green for isotonic, red for hypertonic).
- Show solute particles (small dots) more densely in the hypertonic solution, less densely in the hypotonic.
- Use arrows to show net water movement direction.
- Include measurements or labels of osmolarity if possible (0 mOsm/L, 300 mOsm/L, 1000 mOsm/L).

**Avoid:**
- Making the cell look like it's "trying" to move or "seeking" something.
- Showing pumps or channels in this figure (those are for active transport, not osmosis).
- Ambiguity about which direction the water moves.

## Figure 2: The Sodium-Potassium Pump in Action (Concept 2)

**Location:** Concept 2, in the "Mechanism: Pumping Against the Tide" section.

**Purpose:** Show how active transport moves ions against their concentration gradient, using the Na+/K+-ATPase as the example.

**Description:** Cross-section of a cell membrane showing the Na+/K+-ATPase pump in action. Show three steps (either as three panels or as a cycle):

1. **Step 1:** ATP binds to the pump protein; Na+ ions (inside the cell) bind to the pump.
2. **Step 2:** ATP is hydrolyzed; the pump releases Na+ outside and picks up K+.
3. **Step 3:** The pump ejects K+ inside and releases the pump back to its original state. The cycle repeats.

**Annotations:**
- Label the three Na+ ions and two K+ ions moved per ATP cycle.
- Show ATP → ADP + energy release at the step where it powers the conformational change.
- Show the concentration gradients: Na+ high outside, K+ high inside. Emphasize that the ions are moving *against* these gradients.

**What the student should notice:**
- The pump is a protein that changes shape.
- Energy is required to move ions against their gradient.
- The pump is selective: it moves Na+ out and K+ in, not the reverse.
- This works at the molecular scale but operates in every cell constantly.

**Visual cues:**
- Use different colors for Na+ (e.g., yellow) and K+ (e.g., purple).
- Show the pump protein as a channel that rotates or changes shape.
- Use arrows to show the movement direction and energy input.
- Use a gradient bar on the left and right to show [Na+] higher outside, [K+] higher inside.

**Avoid:**
- Showing too many molecular details (this is not biochemistry; focus on the conceptual mechanism).
- Ambiguity about the direction of ion movement or the role of ATP.
- Making the pump look too mechanical or factory-like (it's a protein responding to energy, not a machine).

## Figure 3: Osmoregulatory Strategies in Fresh vs. Saltwater Fish (Concept 3)

**Location:** Concept 3, in the "Mechanism: Three Strategies for Three Environments" section.

**Purpose:** Contrast the opposite strategies a fish uses to maintain osmotic balance in freshwater vs. seawater.

**Description:** Two side-by-side diagrams of fish, each showing the osmotic pressures and fish responses:

**Left panel (Freshwater Fish):**
- Fish in freshwater (very dilute).
- Environment labeled: "Hypotonic environment (~10 mOsm/L)"
- Fish's blood labeled: "300 mOsm/L"
- Show water entering the fish via osmosis (arrow pointing inward across gills).
- Show dilute urine being excreted (arrow downward).
- Show active salt uptake through the gills (pump icon, salt ions moving inward against concentration gradient).
- Annotation: "Excretes dilute urine, actively absorbs salt"

**Right panel (Saltwater Fish):**
- Fish in seawater (very concentrated).
- Environment labeled: "Hypertonic environment (~1000 mOsm/L)"
- Fish's blood labeled: "300 mOsm/L"
- Show water leaving the fish via osmosis (arrow pointing outward across gills).
- Show concentrated urine being excreted (arrow downward, smaller volume).
- Show active salt excretion through the gills (pump icon, salt ions moving outward against concentration gradient).
- Show seawater being drunk (arrow pointing inward through mouth).
- Annotation: "Drinks seawater, excretes excess salt through gills and concentrated urine"

**What the student should notice:**
- The fish's blood osmolarity stays the same (300 mOsm/L) in both environments.
- The osmotic challenges are *opposite*: freshwater fish must get rid of water and conserve salt; saltwater fish must conserve water and get rid of salt.
- The solutions are *opposite*: freshwater fish produce dilute urine and absorb salt; saltwater fish produce concentrated urine and excrete salt.
- Active transport is essential in both cases; it is just pointed in opposite directions.

**Visual cues:**
- Use arrows of different sizes to show relative water loss or gain.
- Use color or shading to show osmolarity: light blue for dilute, dark blue for concentrated.
- Make the pump icons visually similar but pointed in opposite directions.
- Label the urine as "dilute" and "concentrated" explicitly.

**Avoid:**
- Making the fish look like they're "trying" to solve a problem (they are, but this is evolution, not deliberate thinking).
- Showing the internal anatomy (keep it simple; focus on the problem and the solution, not the histology).
- Ambiguity about which environment is which.

## Figure 4: The Countercurrent Multiplier in the Loop of Henle (Concept 3 Integration)

**Location:** Integration and Synthesis section, in the "Mechanism: The Countercurrent Multiplier in the Loop of Henle" subsection.

**Purpose:** Show how the opposing flows of the loop of Henle create and maintain an osmotic gradient across the medulla.

**Description:** A vertical schematic of the loop of Henle and surrounding tissue, showing the concentration gradient from top to bottom. The figure should show the path of filtrate through the loop and the mechanism creating the gradient.

**Layout (vertically):**
- **Top (proximal tubule):** Filtrate at 300 mOsm/L enters the descending limb.
- **Descending limb (left side, flowing downward):** Concentration increases from 300 → 1200 mOsm/L as water exits by osmosis. Label: "Permeable to water, impermeable to salt."
- **Loop bend (bottom):** Filtrate reaches maximum concentration of 1200 mOsm/L.
- **Ascending limb (right side, flowing upward):** Concentration decreases from 1200 → 100 mOsm/L as salt is actively transported out. Label: "Impermeable to water, active salt transport outward."
- **Medullary tissue (surrounding the loop):** Show concentration gradient: 300 mOsm/L at the top, increasing to 1200 mOsm/L at the bottom. Use color gradient (light blue at top, dark blue at bottom) or numerical labels at regular intervals (300, 400, 600, 800, 1000, 1200).
- **Collecting duct (on the right, parallel to the ascending limb):** Show it flowing downward through the medulla. Label: "Permeable to water when ADH is present."

**Arrows:**
- Show filtrate flowing down the descending limb and up the ascending limb (opposing currents).
- Show water moving outward from the descending limb (osmosis).
- Show salt moving outward from the ascending limb (active transport, ATP pump icon).
- In the collecting duct, show water moving outward (when ADH is present) due to the osmotic gradient.

**What the student should notice:**
- The two limbs of the loop flow in opposite directions (countercurrent).
- The descending limb is permeable to water (passive); the ascending limb is impermeable (and active).
- The medullary osmotic gradient is not uniform; it increases from top to bottom.
- The gradient is *established* by the loop's activity but *used* by the collecting duct to concentrate urine.
- The countercurrent arrangement allows a small active transport step (ascending limb) to create a large gradient.

**Visual cues:**
- Use color to show osmotic concentration (gradient from light to dark).
- Use different line styles for different segment types (solid for permeable to water, dashed for impermeable).
- Use pump icons to show active transport in the ascending limb.
- Use arrows of varying thickness to show relative water/salt movement.
- Label the osmolarity at key points (top, middle, bottom).

**Avoid:**
- Showing molecular detail (this is physiology, not biochemistry).
- Making the ascending limb look like a simple channel (it is a complex epithelium with active transport).
- Ambiguity about the direction of flows or the direction of osmotic gradients.
- Oversimplifying the gradient as uniform or showing it incorrectly.

## Figure 5: Water Movement Through the Nephron in Response to Blood Osmolarity (Worked Example Integration)

**Location:** Integration and Synthesis section, in the "Worked Example: Tracing Filtrate Through the Entire Nephron" subsection (or as a summary figure at the end of Concept 3).

**Purpose:** Show how the nephron's response to blood osmolarity changes the fate of a water molecule.

**Description:** Two parallel diagrams of the entire nephron (glomerulus through collecting duct), side-by-side, showing two scenarios:

**Left panel (High blood osmolarity):**
- Blood osmolarity: 310 mOsm/L (high)
- ADH: HIGH (circles representing hormone)
- Filtrate path:
  - Glomerulus: filtrate 300 mOsm/L
  - Proximal tubule: 65% water reabsorbed
  - Descending limb: water reabsorbed, concentration increases
  - Ascending limb: salt reabsorbed, filtrate dilutes
  - Distal tubule: some water reabsorbed (ADH present)
  - Collecting duct: water reabsorbed (ADH present, permeable) → concentrated urine (800-1200 mOsm/L)
- Urine volume: small

**Right panel (Low blood osmolarity):**
- Blood osmolarity: 290 mOsm/L (low)
- ADH: LOW or ABSENT (few hormone circles)
- Filtrate path:
  - Glomerulus: filtrate 300 mOsm/L
  - Proximal tubule: 65% water reabsorbed
  - Descending limb: water reabsorbed, concentration increases
  - Ascending limb: salt reabsorbed, filtrate dilutes
  - Distal tubule: little water reabsorbed (ADH absent)
  - Collecting duct: water NOT reabsorbed (no ADH, impermeable to water) → dilute urine (100-150 mOsm/L)
- Urine volume: large

**Annotations:**
- Label each segment with the osmolarity of the filtrate at that point.
- Show ADH as small circles or icons; more circles on the left (high), fewer on the right (low).
- Highlight the collecting duct as the key point where the response differs.
- Show the medullary osmotic gradient in the background (as a faint color gradient).

**What the student should notice:**
- The nephron's response to blood osmolarity is to change water reabsorption in the collecting duct, not to change the entire nephron's function.
- The descending limb, ascending limb, and early tubule segments behave the same regardless of blood osmolarity; they do the "baseline" job of reabsorbing solutes and establishing the medullary gradient.
- The collecting duct is the decision point: if ADH is present, water is reabsorbed (concentrated urine); if ADH is absent, water is not reabsorbed (dilute urine).
- This is a feedback loop: high blood osmolarity → more ADH → more water reabsorption → blood osmolarity returns to normal.

**Visual cues:**
- Use color or shading to show osmolarity (light for dilute, dark for concentrated).
- Use hormone icons (circles or logos) to show ADH levels.
- Use arrows to show water and salt movement, with size of arrow corresponding to amount.
- Make the collecting duct the visual focal point, showing it as the site where the response happens.

**Avoid:**
- Making the diagram too detailed (this is about the principle, not every cell type).
- Showing both scenarios in a single diagram (side-by-side is clearer).
- Ambiguity about which way the hormone works or which segment responds.

## Figure 6: Ion Channels and Active Transport Across a Semi-Permeable Membrane (Optional, for supplemental understanding)

**Location:** (Optional) Could appear as a supplemental figure or in the pantry materials.

**Purpose:** Help students understand the difference between facilitated diffusion and active transport at a mechanistic level.

**Description:** Two side-by-side diagrams of a cell membrane, showing the same ion (e.g., Na+) moving through two different mechanisms:

**Left (Facilitated Diffusion Channel):**
- Channel protein spans the membrane.
- Na+ ion (high concentration outside, low inside) is shown approaching the channel from outside.
- The ion passes through the channel and exits into the cytoplasm.
- Annotation: "Diffusion is *always* downhill. This channel works equally well in both directions."

**Right (Active Transport Pump):**
- Pump protein spans the membrane, shown in two conformations (before and after ATP hydrolysis).
- Na+ ion (high concentration outside, low inside) is shown approaching the pump from inside.
- ATP provides energy; the pump changes shape and ejects the Na+ outside against the concentration gradient.
- Annotation: "Active transport is *uphill*, powered by ATP. The pump has a direction."

**What the student should notice:**
- A channel is passive and bidirectional; a pump is active and directional.
- To maintain a concentration gradient (like keeping Na+ low inside), you need active transport, not just a channel.
- The energy cost is the price of maintaining the gradient against diffusion.

**Visual cues:**
- Use color to distinguish Na+ (and the direction it would naturally go) from the pump protein.
- Use two stages for the pump to show the conformational change.
- Use an ATP/ADP symbol to show where energy comes in.
- Make the directionality visually obvious.

## Accessibility Notes

- Use color wisely; ensure figures are readable in grayscale and by colorblind readers. (Use patterns or labels in addition to color.)
- Provide alt text for all figures describing the main concept, not just what is visible.
- Ensure arrows and directional cues are clear and labeled explicitly.
- Use consistent labeling across figures (e.g., always use "mOsm/L" for osmolarity, always show Na+ in the same color).

## FIGURE 1: Hypha Structure and Branching Mycelium

**Caption:** A single hypha extends as a filament, growing at the tip. As it branches, the branching network forms a mycelium. Cross-section shows cell wall (chitin), cytoplasm, nucleus, and vacuole.

**Description:** Left panel shows a single hypha as a tube-like structure with a growing tip. The tip is densely packed with organelles and vesicles (showing active growth). The body of the hypha is uniform, with distributed organelles. Right panel shows three hyphae branching from a common point, creating a network pattern. A cross-section circle inset shows the ultrastructure: thick chitin cell wall (dark), cytoplasm (light), nucleus (dark circle with nucleolus), large central vacuole, mitochondria scattered throughout.

**Labels:** 
- Hypha (growth tip, body)
- Mycelium (branching network)
- Cell wall (chitin)
- Nucleus
- Vacuole
- Cytoplasm

**Source/Origin:** Synthesized from standard mycology textbook diagrams. Hypha diameter 5-10 micrometers. Structure based on typical ascomycete or basidiomycete hyphae.

## FIGURE 2: Absorptive Nutrition and External Digestion

**Caption:** A fungal hypha secretes exoenzymes into the substrate, breaking down complex polymers. Smaller molecules diffuse back into the hypha and are absorbed across the cell membrane.

**Description:** Left panel shows a hypha in contact with substrate (wood or soil, depicted as irregular fragments). Arrows labeled "exoenzyme secretion" point outward from the hypha. The substrate fragments are color-coded: cellulose (light blue), hemicellulose (medium blue), lignin (dark brown). Arrows labeled "enzyme breakdown" show these polymers fragmenting into smaller pieces. Right panel shows the hypha with arrows labeled "molecular absorption" pointing inward, representing glucose, amino acids, and fatty acids crossing the cell membrane.

**Labels:**
- Hypha
- Cell wall (chitin)
- Substrate (wood/soil)
- Exoenzyme secretion
- Cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin (polymers)
- Glucose, amino acids, fatty acids (monomers)
- Absorption

**Source/Origin:** Conceptual diagram based on fungal physiology. Cellulase, protease, lipase enzymes named. Absorptive nutrition is the key mechanism.

## FIGURE 3: Asexual and Sexual Spore Production

**Caption:** Left: asexual reproduction produces genetically identical spores from a hypha tip. Right: sexual reproduction involves fusion of two compatible strains, brief diploidy, and meiosis producing genetically diverse spores.

**Description:** Left panel shows a hypha with a specialized tip swelling with nuclei and cytoplasm. The tip seals off as a spore (shown as a small round cell with a thick wall). Multiple spores are shown being produced in sequence. Right panel shows two hyphae (labeled Mating Type A and Mating Type B) approaching. They fuse. Nuclei fuse (shown as merging circles). The resulting diploid nucleus immediately undergoes meiosis (shown as division producing four haploid nuclei). These form four spores, genetically distinct from each other and the parents.

**Labels:**
- Asexual spore production
- Hypha
- Spore (haploid)
- Sexual reproduction
- Mating Type A, B
- Hyphal fusion
- Diploid nucleus
- Meiosis
- Genetic diversity

**Source/Origin:** Standard mycology representation of fungal reproduction. Asexual spores are mitotic clones. Sexual spores are products of meiosis.

## FIGURE 4: Cross-Section of Mushroom Cap — Spore-Producing Structures

**Caption:** Underneath the mushroom cap, gills (or pores, depending on fungal group) support basidia or asci that produce spores. A single mushroom can release billions of spores.

**Description:** Top view shows a mushroom cap from below, with gills radiating from the center like spokes. Cross-section through the gill shows a gill wall with basidia (club-shaped cells) lining both sides. Each basidium produces four spores on stalks (sterigmata). Spores are shown being released from the sterigmata into the air space below the cap.

**Labels:**
- Mushroom cap
- Gills
- Basidium (club fungus)
- Sterigma (spore stalk)
- Spore
- Air space

**Source/Origin:** Standard mushroom anatomy. Shows basidiomycete example. Note: ascomycetes have asci (sac-like structures) instead of basidia, but concept is similar.

## FIGURE 5: Timeline of Wood Decomposition by Fungi

**Caption:** Over 2–5 years, fungal mycelium gradually breaks down a fallen log, converting it from intact wood to crumbly material to soil.

**Description:** Horizontal timeline with five stages. Stage 1 (Day 0): intact log with bark, fresh cut ends. Stage 2 (Week 2–4): fungal mycelium visible as white threads penetrating bark and wood. Stage 3 (Month 3–6): log is darkened, softened slightly, visible fungal fruiting body (small mushroom) growing from it. Stage 4 (Year 1–2): log is crumbly, heavily colonized, no fruiting body visible but mycelium throughout. Stage 5 (Year 3–5): log is mostly disintegrated, mixed with soil, brown crumbly texture, insects visible, new vegetation beginning to colonize.

**Labels:**
- Day 0: Fresh log
- Weeks 2–4: Mycelial colonization
- Months 3–6: Fungal fruiting
- Years 1–2: Advanced decomposition
- Years 3–5: Soil incorporation

**Source/Origin:** Conceptual timeline based on typical temperate forest decomposition rates. Timing varies with wood type, moisture, temperature, and fungal species.

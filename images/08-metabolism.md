## Figure 1: Cell Entropy & Open Systems

**Caption:** A sealed aquarium (left) reaches equilibrium and disorder. A living cell (right) maintains low entropy locally by exporting disorder to its surroundings.

**Layout:** Two side-by-side panels, each showing a transparent container with water.

**Left panel (sealed):**
- Closed container with salt and water molecules
- Molecules shown distributed evenly (high entropy)
- Label: "Sealed system at equilibrium — ΔG = 0, no work done"
- Shows arrow: "Disorder increases"

**Right panel (open):**
- Cell shown as circle with internal structure (proteins, DNA, organized)
- Arrow labeled "Energy in (glucose, light)" entering cell
- Arrow labeled "Heat + waste out (CO₂, entropy)" leaving cell
- Cell interior labeled "Low entropy (organized)"
- Surroundings labeled "Increasing disorder"
- Label: "Open system far from equilibrium — cell exports entropy"

**Style:** Simplified, clean; use color to highlight high/low entropy (cooler colors for order, warmer for disorder)

## Figure 2: Free Energy & Reaction Favorability

**Caption:** The Gibbs free energy equation predicts spontaneity. Exergonic reactions (ΔG < 0) release energy and proceed. Endergonic reactions (ΔG > 0) require energy input.

**Layout:** Two energy diagrams side-by-side (exergonic left, endergonic right).

**Both diagrams:**
- Vertical axis: Free energy (G)
- Horizontal axis: Reaction progress
- Reactants shown as starting point
- Products shown as end point
- Curved line showing path between them

**Left (exergonic):**
- Products lower than reactants
- Arrow pointing down from reactants to products
- Label: "ΔG < 0 — energy released — exergonic"
- Label: "Reaction proceeds spontaneously"

**Right (endergonic):**
- Products higher than reactants
- Arrow pointing up from reactants to products
- Label: "ΔG > 0 — energy required — endergonic"
- Label: "Reaction does not proceed without energy input"

**Style:** Matching color scheme; use consistent shape for molecules

## Figure 3: ATP Structure & Hydrolysis

**Caption:** ATP is adenosine (adenine + ribose sugar) bonded to three phosphate groups. The high-energy phosphoanhydride bonds release energy when broken.

**Layout:** Large structure drawing at top; hydrolysis reaction equation below.

**Structure:**
- Central ribose sugar (pentagon shape, labeled)
- Adenine base attached above sugar (ring structure, labeled)
- Three phosphate groups extending right, linked by bonds
- Label phosphates: Alpha (closest to ribose), Beta, Gamma (farthest)
- Highlight the two high-energy bonds (between alpha-beta and beta-gamma) in bold or color
- Label: "Phosphoanhydride bonds — high energy"

**Hydrolysis reaction (below structure):**
- Show: ATP + H₂O → ADP + Pi + energy
- Curved arrow showing which bond breaks (between beta and gamma)
- Energy released shown numerically: "~7.3 kcal/mol (standard), ~14 kcal/mol (cellular)"

**Style:** Ball-and-stick or space-filling model; color-code atoms (C = gray, N = blue, O = red, P = orange)

## Figure 4: Sodium-Potassium Pump Cycle

**Caption:** The Na⁺/K⁺ pump couples ATP hydrolysis to active transport. One ATP molecule drives one complete cycle: three Na⁺ out, two K⁺ in.

**Layout:** Circular sequence of seven small diagrams showing pump protein in the cell membrane, progressing clockwise.

**Step 1 (12 o'clock):** Pump protein in resting state. ATP approaching from cytoplasm. Label: "1. ATP binding"

**Step 2 (2 o'clock):** ATP bound to pump. Phosphate transferring to pump. Label: "2. Phosphorylation"

**Step 3 (4 o'clock):** Pump changes shape (zigzag through membrane). Three Na⁺ ions now bound inside. Label: "3. Na⁺ binding & pump conformational change"

**Step 4 (6 o'clock):** Three Na⁺ expelled to outside. Label: "4. Na⁺ ejection to outside"

**Step 5 (8 o'clock):** Two K⁺ ions bound on outside. Label: "5. K⁺ binding"

**Step 6 (10 o'clock):** Phosphate falls off pump. Pump begins to return to resting shape. Label: "6. Phosphate release"

**Step 7 (back to 12):** Two K⁺ released inside. Pump fully returned to resting state. Label: "7. K⁺ release to inside"

**Central annotations:**
- Show membrane as solid line cutting through all seven steps
- Inside (left side) labeled "Cytoplasm"
- Outside (right side) labeled "Extracellular"
- Energy released from ATP shown as lightning bolt or glow

**Style:** Cartoon protein shape; clear ion symbols (Na⁺, K⁺); arrows showing movement

## Figure 5: Activation Energy & Enzyme Catalysis

**Caption:** Enzymes lower the activation energy (EA) without changing ΔG. Both pathways go from reactants to products at the same heights, but the enzyme provides a lower-energy route.

**Layout:** Single energy diagram with two overlaid curves.

**Axes:**
- Vertical: Free energy (G)
- Horizontal: Reaction progress

**Reactants:** Starting point on left, set at arbitrary zero level

**Products:** Ending point on right, at a lower energy level than reactants (exergonic reaction)

**Two curves:**
1. **Without enzyme:** Tall peak (high EA) between reactants and products. Peak labeled "Transition state (high EA)" or "Activation energy barrier"

2. **With enzyme:** Lower peak (low EA) at same horizontal position. Peak labeled "Transition state with enzyme (low EA)"

**Annotations:**
- Arrow from reactants to first peak labeled "EA (no enzyme)" — tall, steep
- Arrow from reactants to second peak labeled "EA (enzyme)" — shorter, less steep
- Arrow from both peaks downward to products labeled "ΔG — same for both pathways"
- Label: "Enzyme = lower-energy path to same destination"

**Style:** Use different line styles (dashed vs. solid) or colors (red no enzyme, green with enzyme) to distinguish

## Figure 6: Lock-and-Key vs. Induced Fit

**Caption:** The lock-and-key model describes rigid fitting. Induced fit shows that enzyme and substrate both deform slightly upon binding, achieving optimal catalytic geometry.

**Layout:** Two side-by-side comparisons.

**Left (Lock-and-Key):**
- Enzyme active site shown as rigid shape (like a keyhole)
- Substrate shown as rigid shape (like a key)
- Label: "Lock-and-Key Model (outdated)"
- Caption: "Rigid fit; instant binding; enzyme unchanged"

**Right (Induced Fit):**
- Same enzyme shape, but with slight deformation
- Same substrate shape, but contorted slightly
- Show how they fit together with both molecules adjusted
- Transition state or reaction coordinate shown at bottom of active site
- Label: "Induced Fit Model (current)"
- Caption: "Both enzyme and substrate deform; optimal binding; stabilized transition state"

**Additional detail (below both):**
- Show the enzyme's active site lined with amino acid residues (balls with different colors for different side chain types: positive, negative, hydrophobic, etc.)
- Show substrate approach, binding, reaction occurring, product release

**Style:** Cartoon protein shapes; substrate shown in contrasting color; amino acids as colored spheres

## Figure 7: Allosteric Inhibition

**Caption:** An allosteric inhibitor binds away from the active site, inducing a conformational change that reduces the enzyme's affinity for substrate.

**Layout:** Three stages of inhibition shown horizontally.

**Stage 1 (left):** Enzyme with open/active active site. Substrate approaching. Label: "Enzyme active — substrate binds well"

**Stage 2 (center):** Inhibitor molecule approaching allosteric site (shown as a distinct pocket, away from active site). Label: "Allosteric inhibitor approaches"

**Stage 3 (right):** Inhibitor bound to allosteric site. Enzyme active site has shifted shape (shown as subtle distortion). Substrate present but not binding well. Label: "Active site shape changes — substrate affinity reduced"

**Comparison box (bottom):**
- Show competitive inhibition for contrast: inhibitor competes for active site, blocks substrate directly
- Label: "Compare to competitive inhibition (inhibitor blocks active site directly)"

**Color scheme:**
- Enzyme: one color
- Active site: highlighted
- Allosteric site: different location, highlighted
- Substrate: one color
- Inhibitor: contrasting color

**Style:** Cartoon protein; side-by-side snapshots showing progression

## Figure 8: Feedback Inhibition in Glycolysis

**Caption:** ATP inhibits phosphofructokinase (PFK), an early enzyme in glycolysis. When ATP accumulates, glucose breakdown slows. When ATP is depleted, breakdown accelerates.

**Layout:** Vertical pathway showing glycolysis steps, with feedback loop.

**Main pathway (vertical):**
- Show ~10 boxes representing glycolysis reactions
- Label first few: Glucose → Glucose-6-phosphate → Fructose-6-phosphate → [PFK here, label clearly] → Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate → ... → ATP (product, at bottom)

**Feedback loop:**
- Arrow looping from ATP (bottom) back to PFK (third enzyme)
- Label on arrow: "ATP inhibits PFK (allosteric inhibition)"
- Red color or inhibition symbol (⊣) on the loop

**Regulatory effects (side annotations):**
- Left side of pathway: "High ATP → Low activity" with icon showing slowed reactions
- Left side: "Low ATP → High activity" with icon showing faster reactions
- Central theme: "Cells regulate their own energy production"

**Additional detail (optional):**
- Show ADP activating the same enzymes (green arrow, opposite direction, labeled "ADP activates")

**Style:** Clean cascade with clear loop; use color to show positive (green) vs. inhibitory (red) effects

## Figure 9: Integration — Glucose Metabolism Overview

**Caption:** Thermodynamics, ATP coupling, enzyme kinetics, and feedback inhibition work together. Glucose breakdown (exergonic) drives ATP regeneration. ATP powers endergonic reactions. Feedback inhibition regulates the rate.

**Layout:** Comprehensive pathway diagram combining elements of glycolysis, ATP cycle, and regulation.

**Main structure:**
- Glucose entering from top-left
- Glycolysis pathway shown as cascading reactions (6–8 enzyme steps, simplified)
- Energy release shown at several steps (lightning bolt or glow symbol)
- Energy captured as ATP formation (Py + ADP → ATP) shown at one or two key steps
- ATP/ADP cycle shown as circular exchange (ATP ↔ ADP + Pi + energy) on right side
- Feedback inhibition loop from ATP back to an early enzyme (PFK or glucose-6-phosphate level)
- Final product(s) shown exiting (pyruvate, to citric acid cycle)

**Annotations (scattered strategically):**
- "ΔG << 0 for glucose breakdown (overall very favorable)" — at top
- "Enzymes lower EA for each step" — at one of the pathway steps
- "ATP hydrolysis: ΔG = −14 kcal/mol (cellular)" — at ATP energy symbol
- "Couples endergonic reactions" — at one of the energy-requiring steps
- "Feedback inhibition slows pathway when ATP high" — at the loop

**Energy flows (shown as arrows or flows):**
- Chemical energy in glucose → kinetic energy of reactions → free energy in ATP
- ATP → mechanical work, transport, synthesis, heat loss

**Style:** Integrated diagram; use consistent icons/symbols; color gradient (high energy = warm colors, low energy = cool colors) or heat map to show energy flow

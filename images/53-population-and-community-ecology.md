### Section 1: Population Measurement

1. **Quadrat sampling method**
   - Shows 1 m² quadrat frames placed in habitat (forest floor with plants)
   - Multiple quadrats shown scattered across landscape to illustrate random sampling
   - Labels: "Quadrat," "Sample area," "Count individuals within boundary"
   - Source concept: standard ecological survey method

2. **Mark-and-recapture process (three-panel)**
   - Panel A: First capture—animals marked (e.g., fish with tags, mammals with paint)
   - Panel B: Release and mixing—marked animals dispersed in population
   - Panel C: Second capture—showing mix of marked and unmarked individuals
   - Labels: "Marked," "Unmarked," "Population estimate = (M₁ × N₂) / M₂"

3. **Species distribution patterns (three-panel)**
   - Panel A: Uniform distribution (e.g., evenly spaced plants, territorial animals like penguins)
   - Panel B: Random distribution (e.g., wind-dispersed seeds)
   - Panel C: Clumped distribution (e.g., trees clustered from seed drop, fish schools)
   - Labels: "Uniform," "Random," "Clumped"

### Section 3: Population Growth Models

4. **Exponential growth curve (J-shaped)**
   - X-axis: Time (hours or generations)
   - Y-axis: Population size (N)
   - Shows characteristic J-shape with accelerating curve
   - Example: bacteria culture starting at 1,000, doubling each hour
   - Label: "Exponential growth, dN/dt = rN"
   - Note: Include annotation showing doubling times

5. **Logistic growth curve (S-shaped)**
   - X-axis: Time
   - Y-axis: Population size (N)
   - Shows characteristic S-shape: slow → fast → plateau
   - Carrying capacity (K) marked as horizontal dashed line
   - Shows data points (real population, e.g., yeast or sheep) with slight oscillation around K
   - Label: "Logistic growth, dN/dt = r_max × N × (K-N)/K"

6. **Comparison: Exponential vs. Logistic**
   - Two curves overlaid
   - Exponential diverges to infinity
   - Logistic plateaus at K
   - Shaded region shows "sustainable zone" below K
   - Caption: "Exponential growth (unlimited resources) vs. logistic growth (limited resources)"

### Section 5: Community Interactions

7. **Lynx-hare predator-prey cycle**
   - Two overlaid lines (or different colors) showing populations over ~100 years
   - Hare population peaks first; lynx population peaks 1–2 years later
   - Shows repeated cycles with ~10-year period
   - X-axis: Years; Y-axis: Population (thousands of animals)
   - Labels: "Snowshoe hare," "Canada lynx," "Time lag: 1–2 years"
   - Source: historical fur trapping records from Hudson's Bay Company

8. **Predator and prey defenses (six-panel)**
   - Mechanical defense: Thorns on plants, turtle shell
   - Chemical defense: Poisonous frog (bright colors), toxic plant
   - Behavioral defense: Fish school, camouflaged insect (walking stick), playing dead
   - Labels: "Mechanical," "Chemical," "Behavioral"

9. **Mimicry types (four-panel)**
   - Panel A: Batesian mimicry—harmless butterfly mimics wasp stripes
   - Panel B: Müllerian mimicry—multiple toxic butterflies share warning colors
   - Panel C: Model vs. mimic side-by-side comparison (e.g., coral snake vs. milk snake)
   - Panel D: Predator learning curve (benefits of mimicry decline if frequency is too high)
   - Labels: "Model," "Mimic," "Benefit to mimic"

10. **Competitive exclusion (Paramecium)—three-panel time series**
    - Panel A: *P. aurelia* and *P. caudatum* alone—both thrive
    - Panel B: Both species together—*P. aurelia* beginning to dominate
    - Panel C: Final outcome—*P. caudatum* extinct, *P. aurelia* only
    - X-axis: Time (days); Y-axis: Population density
    - Label: "Competitive exclusion principle"

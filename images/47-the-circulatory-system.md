# Images Brief — The Circulatory System (Ch. 47)

## Overview

Five figures support this chapter, each tied to a learning objective. All should be rendered in Attenborough × Feynman voice: clear labels, no decoration, focused on the concept being taught.

---

## FIGURE 1: Open vs. Closed Circulation

**Concept tie:** Core Concept 2—circulatory system architectures and the evolution of the heart.

**What it shows:**
- Left side: An insect (e.g., a grasshopper) with an open circulatory system.
  - Visible tubular heart running along the midline.
  - Arrows showing hemolymph pumped into a body cavity (hemocoel), circulating around organs, and returning through ostia (pores) to the heart.
  - Label the hemocoel, ostia, hemolymph path.
  - Color hemolymph blue-green to indicate it is not as oxygen-rich as closed-system blood.

- Right side: A fish with a closed circulatory system.
  - Heart with two chambers (atrium and ventricle).
  - Arrows showing blood path: atrium → ventricle → gills → body → return to atrium (unidirectional).
  - Label arteries (red, away from heart), veins (blue, to heart), gills.

**Purpose:** Students should see that open systems are simpler (no vessels, lower pressure) but also less efficient (slower, lower oxygen delivery). Closed systems use more energy but reliably reach all tissues.

**Student should notice:**
- Pressure is higher and more sustained in the closed system.
- The open system relies on the body cavity as a blood reservoir.
- Metabolically active animals (birds, mammals, fast-moving fish) evolved closed systems.

**Annotations:**
- "Hemolymph bathes organs directly in this open system. Pressure is low; muscles rely on local contractions to circulate fluid."
- "Closed systems maintain pressure throughout, ensuring consistent oxygen delivery even to distant tissues."

## FIGURE 2: The Four-Chambered Human Heart

**Concept tie:** Core Concept 2—the mammalian four-chambered heart and the pulmonary/systemic circuits.

**What it shows:**
- Anterior (front) view of the human heart in situ (in the body).
- Four chambers clearly labeled: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle.
- Major vessels labeled: superior vena cava (from upper body), inferior vena cava (from lower body), pulmonary arteries (to lungs), pulmonary veins (from lungs), aorta (to body).
- Four valves labeled: tricuspid (right atrium to ventricle), mitral/bicuspid (left atrium to ventricle), pulmonary semilunar (right ventricle to pulmonary artery), aortic semilunar (left ventricle to aorta).
- Color coding:
  - Deoxygenated blood: dark blue/purple.
  - Oxygenated blood: bright red.
  - Mixed (fetal structures or transitional): lighter purple.
- Arrows showing the complete blood path in a continuous circuit.

**Purpose:** Students should trace the path blood takes: body → right atrium → right ventricle → lungs → left atrium → left ventricle → body. They should see that the two circuits (pulmonary and systemic) are separate.

**Student should notice:**
- The left ventricle's wall is thicker than the right (it must pump blood to the entire body).
- The atria are smaller than the ventricles (atria just receive and funnel; ventricles do the pumping).
- Oxygenated and deoxygenated blood never mix (unlike in a three-chambered heart).
- The heart occupies the space between the two lungs (visible in a faint outline of the rib cage).

**Annotations:**
- "Deoxygenated blood from the body returns here (right atrium). It is pumped to the lungs, oxygenated, and returns to the left atrium."
- "The left ventricle pumps oxygenated blood to the entire body. Its thick wall generates the pressure needed."
- "Valves prevent backflow. When they close, you hear the heart's 'lub-dub' sound."

## FIGURE 3: Blood Vessel Structure and Pressure Gradient

**Concept tie:** Core Concept 3—vessel structure and the trade-off between pressure and filtration.

**What it shows:**
- Top half: Cross-sectional diagrams of five vessel types arranged left to right (aorta → arteries → arterioles → capillaries → venules → veins).
  - Each vessel shown at the same scale (so the difference in size is visible).
  - Artery and vein: three layers (endothelium, smooth muscle, connective tissue).
  - Arteriole: endothelium + thinner smooth muscle layer.
  - Capillary: endothelium only (single layer of thin cells).
  - Vein: endothelium + thin smooth muscle + connective tissue. Include a valve (shown as a flap preventing backflow).
  - Color scheme: internal walls (endothelium) in light gray; smooth muscle in dark red; connective tissue in tan.

- Bottom half: Graph showing pressure (y-axis, 0–140 mm Hg) vs. vessel type (x-axis).
  - Curve starts at 120 mm Hg in the aorta.
  - Steep drop across arterioles (to ~50 mm Hg) — this is where most resistance occurs.
  - Gradual drop through capillaries (to ~30 mm Hg at venous end).
  - Further drop in veins (to ~5 mm Hg).
  - Shade the region "high pressure (risk of rupture)" at the top and "low pressure (slow flow)" at the bottom.

**Purpose:** Students should understand that vessel wall structure is optimized for function. Thick-walled arteries handle high pressure. Thin-walled capillaries allow exchange. Veins have valves to prevent backflow.

**Student should notice:**
- Capillaries have the largest total cross-sectional area (why blood slows there despite thin individual walls).
- The arterioles are the "resistance vessels"—most pressure is lost there.
- The left-side pressure drop (aorta → capillaries) is needed to slow blood so exchange can happen.
- Veins are low-pressure; muscle contraction and valves move blood back to the heart.

**Annotations:**
- "Arteries are thick and elastic to handle the heart's pressure surge."
- "Capillaries are thin—just one layer of cells—to allow oxygen and glucose to diffuse across."
- "Veins are thin-walled and have one-way valves. Skeletal muscle contraction pushes blood back to the heart."
- "Most pressure is lost across arterioles. This is why drugs that relax arteriolar smooth muscle lower blood pressure."

## FIGURE 4: Capillary Exchange and the Starling Principle

**Concept tie:** Core Concept 3—pressure-driven filtration and the balance of hydrostatic and osmotic pressures.

**What it shows:**
- A single capillary in cross-section, with three zones along its length: arterial end, middle, venous end.
- Inside the capillary: red blood cells, oxygen, glucose, other small molecules.
- Outside (interstitial space): tissue cells, water, waste products (CO₂, metabolic byproducts).
- At the arterial end:
  - Arrow pointing *outward* from capillary (hydrostatic pressure = 35 mm Hg).
  - Smaller arrow pointing inward (osmotic pressure = 25 mm Hg).
  - Net: fluid and small molecules leak out (oxygen and glucose go to tissues).
- At the venous end:
  - Arrows reversed: hydrostatic pressure is low (15 mm Hg), osmotic pressure dominates (25 mm Hg).
  - Net: fluid leaks back in (taking waste with it).
- Pressure values labeled at each zone.
- Lymphatic capillary shown nearby, collecting excess interstitial fluid.

**Purpose:** Students should visualize the Starling principle—that pressure gradients *control* what flows in and out of capillaries. This is how exchange is regulated.

**Student should notice:**
- Fluid and dissolved molecules can cross the thin capillary wall; large proteins stay inside.
- At the arterial end, hydrostatic pressure dominates → net outflow.
- At the venous end, osmotic pressure dominates → net inflow.
- The result is a pressure gradient that ensures exchange happens where needed and prevents excessive fluid loss.
- If blood pressure rises (hypertension), the arterial-end outflow increases, causing edema.

**Annotations:**
- "Hydrostatic pressure (the push from the heart) forces oxygen and glucose into the tissue."
- "Osmotic pressure (from proteins left inside the capillary) pulls fluid back in, collecting waste for transport."
- "At the arterial end, outflow exceeds inflow. At the venous end, inflow exceeds outflow. The net result: exchange of gases and nutrients."
- "About 85% of fluid is reabsorbed in the veins; 15% drains to lymphatic vessels for return to the heart."

## FIGURE 5: The Cardiac Cycle and Electrical Coordination

**Concept tie:** Core Concept 2—the cardiac cycle (systole and diastole) and electrical coordination via the sinoatrial and atrioventricular nodes.

**What it shows:**
- Left side: Three panels showing the heart at different phases of the cardiac cycle.
  - Panel A (Diastole, Atrial Filling): Atria are relaxed and filling with blood from the veins. Ventricles are also relaxed. Atrioventricular valves (tricuspid and mitral) are open. Semilunar valves are closed.
  - Panel B (Atrial Systole): Atria contract, pushing blood into ventricles. Atrioventricular valves remain open. Ventricles still relaxed.
  - Panel C (Ventricular Systole): Atria relax. Ventricles contract, pushing blood into the pulmonary artery and aorta. Atrioventricular valves snap shut (producing the "lub" sound). Semilunar valves open.
  - Panel D (Diastole, Ventricular Relaxation): Ventricles relax. Semilunar valves snap shut (producing the "dup" sound). Atria begin filling again.

- Right side: An electrical diagram showing the conduction pathway.
  - Sinoatrial (SA) node in the right atrial wall: site of spontaneous electrical discharge.
  - Electrical signal spreading through both atria (shown as waves).
  - Atrioventricular (AV) node: signal pauses here (~0.1 second), allowing atria to fully empty.
  - Bundle of His and bundle branches: signal spreads through the interventricular septum.
  - Purkinje fibers: final conduction to the ventricular muscle.
  - At the bottom, a simplified ECG (electrocardiogram) trace showing the P wave (atrial depolarization), QRS complex (ventricular depolarization), and T wave (ventricular repolarization).

- Color coding:
  - Active muscle (contracting): bright red.
  - Relaxed muscle: light pink.
  - Electrical signal: yellow/orange pathway.

**Purpose:** Students should see that the heart's beating is not random; it is driven by electrical signals that coordinate the chambers. The pause at the AV node ensures the atria empty before the ventricles pump.

**Student should notice:**
- The sequence: atria contract → brief pause → ventricles contract.
- The pause is essential; if the ventricles contracted immediately, blood would back up into the atria.
- The "lub-dub" sound corresponds to valve closure (atrioventricular valves → "lub," semilunar valves → "dup").
- The ECG trace is a recording of the electrical events, not the mechanical events (they are synchronized but separate).

**Annotations:**
- "The SA node acts as the heart's pacemaker, generating the electrical impulse that triggers each heartbeat."
- "The pause at the AV node gives the atria time to completely empty into the ventricles."
- "The 'lub-dub' sound is the snap of valves closing. 'Lub' when atrioventricular valves close; 'dup' when semilunar valves close."
- "The ECG (right) records the electrical events. In a normal rhythm, P-QRS-T repeats ~70 times per minute (heart rate)."

## Production Notes

- **Scale and clarity:** All figures should be rendered at high resolution (300 dpi for print, 150 dpi for screen).
- **Color palette:** Use the workshop's standard palette. Red/blue for oxygenated/deoxygenated blood; warm colors (tan, brown) for connective tissue; cool colors (gray, pale blue) for muscle and endothelium.
- **Labels and arrows:** All text should be in the same sans-serif font as the chapter body. Arrow thickness should indicate relative flow magnitude (thick arrows = high flow; thin arrows = low flow or weak signal).
- **Animations (if digital version):** Consider animating Figure 2 (blood path through the heart) and Figure 5 (cardiac cycle). A student should be able to "watch" blood move from the body, through the lungs, and back to the body.
- **Accessibility:** All figures should include alt-text describing the image in plain language. Use hatching or patterns (not just color) to distinguish vessel types in Figure 3, in case the figure is printed in grayscale.

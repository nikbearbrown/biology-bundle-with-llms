## 1. Hero Image: "Three Respiratory Strategies"

**Context**: Open of Concept 2 section

**What to Show**:
- Three panels, side-by-side or sequential
- Panel 1: Cross-section of flatworm (1 mm thick, maybe 5 mm long), semi-transparent, showing that every cell is close to the surface. Label the distance: ~0.5 mm from center to surface.
- Panel 2: Fish gill, cross-sectional view, showing the countercurrent arrangement. Water flowing one direction (arrows pointing left), blood flowing opposite (arrows pointing right). Label: water entering at 8 ppm O₂, blood entering at 2 ppm, exiting at 7.1 ppm. Show concentration gradient persisting along the length.
- Panel 3: Insect tracheal tube, branching network from spiracle to individual cells. Show the spiracles as openings in the body wall. Label diffusion distance from outermost tracheal tip to center: 0.5 cm for a 1 cm diameter insect body.

**Student Should Notice**:
- The flatworm's thinness makes every cell "close" to the environment
- The gill's countercurrent arrangement keeps a gradient along the entire surface
- The insect's direct tubes avoid the bloodstream but only work for small bodies

**Annotations**: 
- Distance scales (mm vs. cm)
- Concentration values (ppm) for water vs. blood in gill
- Arrows showing flow directions (opposing in gill, one-way in trachea)

## 2. Figure: Oxygen Dissociation Curve (S-Shaped)

**Context**: Opening of Concept 3, Hemoglobin section

**What to Show**:
- X-axis: Oxygen partial pressure (0–100 mm Hg), label tissue and lung regions
- Y-axis: Hemoglobin saturation (0–100%)
- Main curve: S-shaped (sigmoid), passing through:
  - ~30% saturation at 20 mm Hg (exercising tissue)
  - ~75% saturation at 40 mm Hg (resting tissue)
  - ~98% saturation at 100 mm Hg (lung)
- Show a linear curve overlay (dashed) to illustrate the difference—linear curve would release much less oxygen at tissue pressures
- Highlight the steep section (around 40 mm Hg) where small pressure changes yield large saturation changes

**Student Should Notice**:
- Why tissues at 40 mm Hg are positioned on the steep part: maximum oxygen-release responsiveness
- Why lungs at 100 mm Hg are on the flat part: saturation achieved quickly, no need for higher oxygen
- Why a linear curve would fail: flat everywhere, no responsiveness

**Annotations**:
- Label "Tissue region" (20–40 mm Hg, steep)
- Label "Lung region" (80–100 mm Hg, flat)
- Arrow showing "Bohr effect shift" (curve shifts right with lower pH, releasing more O₂ at any given pressure)

## 3. Figure: Cross-Section of Alveolus and Capillary

**Context**: Concept 3, describing mammalian lung geometry

**What to Show**:
- Alveolar sac (grape-like structure) with multiple alveoli bulging inward
- Capillary network wrapped around the alveoli, showing red blood cells inside
- Thin barrier (label: ~1 micrometer) between air and blood
- Arrows showing oxygen diffusing from alveolus into blood
- Arrows showing CO₂ diffusing from blood into alveolus
- Label oxygen partial pressures: alveolus ~100 mm Hg, blood entering ~40 mm Hg

**Student Should Notice**:
- The enormous relative surface area created by the grape-bunch structure
- The thinness of the barrier (1 micrometer = 1/10 the thickness of a cell)
- The gradients driving diffusion in both directions

**Annotations**:
- Alveolar wall thickness: 1 μm (note: 10x thinner than flatworm skin shown in Hero Image)
- Oxygen concentration gradient: 100 → 40 mm Hg
- Carbon dioxide concentration gradient: 45 → 40 mm Hg

## 4. Figure: Hemoglobin Cooperativity

**Context**: Concept 3, explaining why the curve is S-shaped

**What to Show**:
- Four hemoglobin subunits (represented as circles or cartoon protein shapes) grouped around a central heme region
- Show four states:
  - State 1: No oxygen bound. Protein in "relaxed" conformation (R state). Label: "Low affinity, difficult to bind first O₂."
  - State 2: One subunit bound to O₂. Protein beginning to shift conformation (T → R transition). Label: "Binding of first O₂ begins conformational change."
  - State 3: Two or three subunits bound. Protein partly shifted. Label: "Binding easier for remaining subunits; cooperative effect."
  - State 4: All four subunits bound. Protein in fully shifted "tense" conformation (T state). Label: "Saturated; all subunits now have high affinity."
- Show the S-shaped curve below, with arrows indicating which states correspond to which parts of the curve.

**Student Should Notice**:
- Why binding is harder at low saturation (protein shape unfavorable) and easier at high saturation (protein shape favorable)
- Why this produces an S-shaped curve, not linear
- How a single amino acid change (as in sickle cell) could disrupt this cooperation

**Annotations**:
- R state (relaxed): lower affinity
- T state (tense): higher affinity
- "Allosteric effect": change in one subunit affects others

## 5. Figure: Bohr Effect Shift

**Context**: Concept 3, worked example on oxygen delivery during exercise

**What to Show**:
- Two oxygen dissociation curves overlaid:
  - Curve 1 (resting): normal curve, saturation at 40 mm Hg = 75%
  - Curve 2 (exercising): shifted to the right (higher pH/lower CO₂), saturation at 40 mm Hg = 50%
- Arrows connecting the two curves showing the rightward shift
- Annotation explaining the shift: "Lower pH, higher CO₂, higher temperature in exercising tissue → hemoglobin affinity decreases → more oxygen is released"

**Student Should Notice**:
- The curve shifts right (not up) with Bohr effect
- At the same tissue oxygen pressure (40 mm Hg), exercising muscle extracts 25% more of hemoglobin's capacity than resting tissue
- This is automatic: no conscious control; hemoglobin reads the local conditions

**Annotations**:
- Resting condition: pH 7.4, CO₂ 40 mm Hg, temperature 37°C
- Exercising condition: pH 7.2 (lower), CO₂ 50 mm Hg (higher), temperature 38–40°C
- "Rightward shift = increased oxygen release at any given pressure"

## 6. Figure: Countercurrent Exchange Diagram

**Context**: Concept 2, worked example on gill efficiency

**What to Show**:
- Two parallel pathways (water and blood) flowing in opposite directions
- Water entering at 8 ppm O₂ (left), exiting at ~2 ppm (right)
- Blood entering at 2 ppm O₂ (right), exiting at 7.1 ppm (left)
- Arrows showing oxygen molecules crossing the thin barrier throughout the length
- Concentration labels at four positions along the length:
  - Entry: water 8 ppm, blood 2 ppm (gradient = 6 ppm)
  - 1/3 along: water 6 ppm, blood 4 ppm (gradient = 2 ppm)
  - 2/3 along: water 4 ppm, blood 6 ppm (gradient = 2 ppm, still reversed!)
  - Exit: water 2 ppm, blood 7.1 ppm (gradient collapsed but blood is much richer)

**Student Should Notice**:
- The gradient never fully collapses; it is always driving oxygen from water into blood
- The exiting blood (7.1 ppm) is almost as rich as the incoming water (8 ppm)
- If flow were parallel (both left to right), blood would equilibrate with incoming water (8 ppm) and stop extracting

**Annotations**:
- "Countercurrent flow: opposing directions maintain gradient"
- "Extraction efficiency: 85%" (labeled at exit)
- Comparison box showing parallel flow: "Blood equilibrates at ~4–5 ppm; extraction ~50%"

## 7. Figure: Insect Size Limit (Scaling Argument)

**Context**: Concept 2, worked example on insect tracheal scaling

**What to Show**:
- Three insect silhouettes, increasing in size
- Insect 1 (small): 2 mm body diameter. Tracheal tubes shown branching throughout. Label: "Spiracle-to-center distance: 1 mm. Diffusion adequate."
- Insect 2 (medium): 1 cm body diameter. Tracheal tubes shown, but center still reachable. Label: "Spiracle-to-center distance: 5 mm. Diffusion slower but manageable."
- Insect 3 (large, hypothetical): 10 cm body diameter. Tracheal tubes shown, but they don't reach the center. Label: "Spiracle-to-center distance: 5 cm. Diffusion flux drops to 1/10. Metabolic demand grows 125x (volume cubed). Insect cannot sustain central cells."
- Show a small graph beside: metabolic demand (volume, cubic) vs. tracheal delivery capacity (area, linear) as functions of body size.

**Student Should Notice**:
- Diffusion distance grows linearly with body size
- Flux through a given surface drops inversely with distance
- Metabolic demand grows as volume (cubic with body size)
- At some size, demand exceeds delivery; the animal suffocates

**Annotations**:
- Fick's law: $J \propto \frac{1}{d}$
- Metabolic demand: $\propto \text{volume} = \text{diameter}^3$
- "This is why the largest insects are mouse-sized, not dog-sized"

## General Design Notes for All Images

- Use consistent color coding: oxygen in blue, CO₂ in red, blood in burgundy, air in light gray
- Include scale bars or labels for all distances (micrometers, millimeters, centimeters)
- Use arrows for flow directions; use gradients or heatmaps for concentration (high = dark, low = light)
- Avoid cartoon-style oversimplification; show enough anatomical detail that students recognize the structures
- Every image should have a title that names the concept, not the mechanism (e.g., "Cooperative Binding in Hemoglobin" not "Why the Curve is S-Shaped")

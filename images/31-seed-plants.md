# Images: Seed Plants (Chapter 31)

## Images Referenced in Chapter

[FIGURE: Tropical palms on sandy shore with water in background — contrast with fern habitat in stream corridor]  
**Caption:** Environmental niches. Palms are independent of standing water; ferns are tethered to moisture.

[FIGURE: Seed cross-section showing embryo, endosperm, and seed coat layers]  
**Caption:** Anatomy of a seed. Three structures: embryo (baby plant), endosperm (food), coat (protection).

[FIGURE: Pollen grain under electron microscope — ornate sculptured surface]  
**Caption:** Pollen grain (male gametophyte). The exine coat prevents desiccation and is species-specific in shape.

[FIGURE: Comparison table: fern spore lifecycle vs seed plant lifecycle]  
**Caption:** Trade-offs. Ferns produce cheap, numerous spores; seed plants produce expensive, fewer seeds with better protection.

[FIGURE: Pine cone dissection showing male cones (pollen sacs) and female cones (ovule-bearing scales)]  
**Caption:** Gymnosperm reproduction. Male cones produce pollen; female cones contain ovules that develop into seeds.

[FIGURE: Pine pollen grain with two nuclei visible inside]  
**Caption:** Inside the pollen grain: a pollen tube cell and cells that will produce sperm. In gymnosperms, sperm may retain flagella.

[FIGURE: Pollen tube growing down pine style, sperm nucleus fusing with egg]  
**Caption:** Fertilization in gymnosperms. The pollen tube grows toward the egg. Sperm fuses with egg to form zygote.

[FIGURE: Pine seed on cone scale — embryo, endosperm, seed coat visible]  
**Caption:** Gymnosperm seed structure. Seeds remain on the cone, protected and dormant, until conditions are right.

[FIGURE: Geological timeline: 350 MYA (first seed plants) → 300 MYA (true gymnosperms) → 125 MYA (angiosperms) → 100 MYA (rapid diversification) → present]  
**Caption:** Seed plant evolution. Gymnosperms dominated for 200 million years; angiosperms rose to dominance in under 50 million years.

[FIGURE: Four gymnosperm groups: conifer (pine branches with needles), cycad (palm-like crown), ginkgo (fan-shaped leaves), gnetophyte (shrubby form)]  
**Caption:** Living gymnosperm diversity. Four groups survive today; conifers are by far the largest.

[FIGURE: Complete flower diagram labeling sepals, petals, stamens (anther, filament), carpels (stigma, style, ovary)]  
**Caption:** Flower anatomy. Male parts (stamens) produce pollen; female parts (carpels) contain ovules.

[FIGURE: Flower cross-section showing pollen tube growing from stigma down through style into ovule chamber]  
**Caption:** Pollen tube pathway. The tube delivers sperm through the style into the ovule.

[FIGURE: Double fertilization diagram: two sperm nuclei entering embryo sac, one fusing with egg (forming zygote), one fusing with polar nuclei (forming endosperm)]  
**Caption:** Double fertilization (angiosperms only). One sperm → zygote (embryo). Second sperm + polar nuclei → endosperm (food).

[FIGURE: Comparison of monocot vs eudicot embryos, leaves, vascular tissue, roots, flowers]  
**Caption:** Monocots vs eudicots. Architectural differences visible from seed to mature plant.

[FIGURE: Flower pollinator specificity: hummingbird with red tubular flower, bee with ultraviolet-patterned flower, moth with night-blooming white fragrant flower]  
**Caption:** Pollinator syndromes. Flower characteristics match pollinator sensory and behavioral preferences.

[FIGURE: Nectar guide pattern visible to bees (ultraviolet reflectance) showing path to pollen sacs]  
**Caption:** Ultraviolet guides. Bees see patterns invisible to humans, which direct them to pollen and stigma.

[FIGURE: Fruit diversity: tomato, pepper, bean pod, apple, berry, spiky cocklebur, winged maple seed, dandelion parachute]  
**Caption:** Fruits take many forms. Each type reflects a dispersal strategy: animal, wind, water, or mechanical.

[FIGURE: Acacia tree with ant nest in hollow thorn, ants on leaf nectaries]  
**Caption:** Acacia-ant mutualism. The tree provides shelter and food; ants defend against herbivores and pathogens.

[FIGURE: Plant defense mechanisms: alkaloid toxins in seeds, thorns and spines, waxy leaf coating]  
**Caption:** Herbivore defenses. Plants evolved multiple strategies; herbivores evolved countermeasures.

[FIGURE: Coevolution diagram: plant fitness increases with pollinator specificity, pollinator fitness increases with flower specificity, both ratchet upward]  
**Caption:** Coevolution loop. Bidirectional selection produces increasing specificity and interdependence over time.

[FIGURE: Human uses of seed plants: wheat field, cotton plants, foxglove flowers, opium poppies, cocoa pods, ginkgo trees in urban setting]  
**Caption:** Seed plants sustain human civilization. Food, materials, medicine, and ornament all depend on plant diversity.

[FIGURE: Tropical deforestation: cleared forest with stumps, remaining canopy in distance]  
**Caption:** Biodiversity loss. Deforestation threatens plant species before they are catalogued and their uses discovered.

## Image Specifications

All images should be:
- Clear line drawings or well-lit photographs
- Labeled with anatomical terms in plain language
- Captioned to connect to the narrative flow of the chapter
- Scale-indicated where relevant (magnification for microscopy images)
- Showing color variation where biologically significant (flower pigmentation, fruit colors)

## Notes

- The opening scene (tropical shore + stream fern habitat) is narrative, not scientific; include both to establish contrast
- Comparative anatomy (monocot vs eudicot) works well as a summary table or side-by-side figure
- Coevolution is abstract; the figure should show selection pressure arrows going both directions
- Deforestation image should convey scale (large cleared area, remaining biodiversity) without being sensationalized

# Image and Figure Guide: Biological Macromolecules

## Figures referenced in chapter (markup format)

[FIGURE: Amylase breaking an alpha glycosidic bond with water insertion]
- Caption: Hydrolysis of starch. Water (H₂O) is inserted at the bond between glucose monomers, splitting the glycosidic linkage. This is the reverse of dehydration synthesis.

[FIGURE: Alpha vs. beta glucose orientations in starch vs. cellulose]
- Caption: The same glucose monomer can be oriented two ways (alpha or beta) in a polymer chain. Alpha bonds (as in starch) create a chain that coils. Beta bonds (as in cellulose) create a rigid, extended structure.

[FIGURE: Glycogen branching structure]
- Caption: Glycogen is more heavily branched than starch's amylopectin component. The additional branch points allow faster glucose mobilization in animal cells.

[FIGURE: Chitin exoskeleton structure in arthropod]
- Caption: Chitin provides the rigid exoskeleton of insects and crustaceans. The modified glucose building blocks (N-acetyl-glucosamine) create a tough, lightweight polymer.

[FIGURE: Fatty acid saturation: straight chains vs. kinked chains]
- Caption: Left: saturated fatty acid with all single bonds. Right: unsaturated fatty acid with double bonds creating kinks. The kinks prevent tight packing, keeping unsaturated fats liquid.

[FIGURE: Phospholipid structure with hydrophilic head and hydrophobic tails]
- Caption: A phospholipid consists of a polar, water-loving head (phosphate group) and nonpolar, water-fearing tails (two fatty acids). This amphipathic nature drives self-assembly into bilayers.

[FIGURE: Phospholipid bilayer orientation in water]
- Caption: In an aqueous environment, phospholipids spontaneously form a bilayer with hydrophilic heads facing outward and hydrophobic tails facing inward. This minimizes contact between water and the nonpolar fatty acid chains.

[FIGURE: Amino acid structure showing alpha carbon with four groups]
- Caption: All amino acids share the same backbone structure: amino group (NH₂), carboxyl group (COOH), hydrogen atom, and R group (variable side chain). The diversity of proteins arises from the 20 different R groups.

[FIGURE: Six example amino acids showing chemical properties of R groups]
- Caption: Clockwise from top: glycine (simple, nonpolar), serine (polar), lysine (charged positive), glutamic acid (charged negative), valine (nonpolar, hydrophobic), proline (R group bonded to amino group, rigid).

[FIGURE: Peptide bond formation between two amino acids]
- Caption: A dehydration synthesis reaction between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next releases water and forms a covalent peptide bond.

[FIGURE: Alpha-helix structure with hydrogen bonding between backbone atoms]
- Caption: In an alpha-helix, the polypeptide backbone twists into a spiral shape, stabilized by hydrogen bonds between the carbonyl oxygen of one amino acid and the amino hydrogen four positions down the chain.

[FIGURE: Beta-pleated sheet structure with antiparallel strands]
- Caption: In a beta-pleated sheet, the polypeptide backbone folds into a zig-zag accordion pattern, with adjacent strands running in opposite directions. Hydrogen bonds form between strands.

[FIGURE: Tertiary structure example: enzyme active site with hydrophobic interior]
- Caption: The three-dimensional shape of a protein is determined by interactions between R groups. Hydrophobic amino acids bury themselves in the interior; hydrophilic amino acids arrange on the surface. The active site (where enzyme binds substrate) has precise geometry.

[FIGURE: Hemoglobin quaternary structure with four subunits]
- Caption: Hemoglobin consists of four polypeptide subunits (two alpha, two beta). The subunits do not covalently bond but are held together by weak interactions. Oxygen binding to one subunit facilitates binding to the others (cooperative binding).

[FIGURE: Red blood cells normal vs. sickled]
- Caption: Left: normal disc-shaped red blood cell. Right: sickled cell from hemoglobin polymerization. The distortion occurs when deoxyhemoglobin molecules with the sickle cell mutation stick together, forming rigid fibers.

[FIGURE: Protein denaturation and refolding (reversible) vs. egg albumin (irreversible)]
- Caption: Left: reversible denaturation—the protein unfolds but refolds when conditions normalize. Right: irreversible denaturation (frying an egg)—heat denatures the protein, exposing hydrophobic interior, which then aggregates with other denatured molecules.

## Specifications for illustration style

- Diagrams should show molecular detail (bonds, functional groups) but also use color coding for clarity: hydrophobic in orange, hydrophilic in blue, charged in red (positive) or purple (negative)
- Structural diagrams should include scale references where relevant (e.g., single amino acid is ~0.3 nm; hemoglobin is ~5.5 nm)
- Biological images (cell, exoskeleton, blood cells) should be drawn from life or high-quality photographs, not stylized icons
- Process diagrams (synthesis, folding) should use arrows and progressive panels to show movement and change
- Color palette: high contrast; accessible to colorblind readers (avoid red-green as sole distinction)

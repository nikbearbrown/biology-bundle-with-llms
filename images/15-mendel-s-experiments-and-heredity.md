# Images & Figures — Mendel's Experiments and Heredity

## Figure List & Descriptions

### [FIGURE: Monastery Garden at St. Thomas, Brünn]
Historical image or artistic rendering of the Augustinian Abbey's garden where Mendel conducted his experiments. Shows rows of pea plants, monastery buildings in background, 1850s setting. Purpose: Establish setting and era. Humanize Mendel's work by situating it in a real place.

### [FIGURE: Monohybrid Cross — Flower Color]
Three-generation diagram:
- P: One plant with violet flowers (VV) × one with white flowers (vv)
- F1: All plants with violet flowers (Vv)
- F2: 3 violet (1 VV + 2 Vv) : 1 white (vv)
Includes genotypes and phenotypes at each level. Purpose: Show the pattern Mendel observed and the 3:1 ratio mechanism.

### [FIGURE: 2×2 Punnett Square — Monohybrid]
Grid showing:
- Gametes across top: V, v
- Gametes down side: V, v
- Four boxes: VV, Vv, Vv, vv
- Summary: 1 VV : 2 Vv : 1 vv (genotypic), 3 dominant : 1 recessive (phenotypic)
Purpose: Teach the Punnett square method and make random combination explicit.

### [FIGURE: 4×4 Punnett Square — Dihybrid Cross]
Grid showing:
- Gametes across top: YR, Yr, yR, yr
- Gametes down side: YR, Yr, yR, yr
- Sixteen boxes showing all combinations
- Phenotypic summary: 9 yellow round : 3 yellow wrinkled : 3 green round : 1 green wrinkled
Purpose: Demonstrate independent assortment and the 9:3:3:1 ratio from first principles.

### [FIGURE: Mendel's Seven Traits — Data Table]
Table with rows:
- Characteristic (flower color, flower position, plant height, seed texture, seed color, pod shape, pod color)
- P Contrasting Traits (violet vs. white, axial vs. terminal, etc.)
- F1 Phenotype (100% dominant)
- F2 Counts (actual numbers from Mendel's experiments)
- F2 Ratio (approximate 3:1 for each)
Purpose: Show consistency across traits. Demonstrate experimental scale (thousands of plants). Establish that the pattern repeats.

### [FIGURE: Dominance Hierarchy — Genotype and Phenotype]
Three boxes in a row:
- Left: VV (homozygous dominant) — phenotype: violet
- Middle: Vv (heterozygous) — phenotype: violet
- Right: vv (homozygous recessive) — phenotype: white
Annotation: "Heterozygotes and homozygous dominants are phenotypically identical."
Purpose: Clarify the distinction between genotype and phenotype. Show why dominance masks recessiveness.

### [FIGURE: Gamete Formation and Segregation]
Diagram showing:
- One cell with two copies of a gene (V and v)
- Meiosis occurring (simplified)
- Two gametes emerging, one with V, one with v
- Each marked with "only one copy"
Purpose: Visualize segregation. Show that paired factors separate during gamete formation.

### [FIGURE: Test Cross Outcomes]
Two side-by-side crosses:
Left side: Unknown plant (R_) × homozygous recessive (rr)
- If unknown is RR: all offspring Rr (all round)
- If unknown is Rr: half offspring Rr (round), half rr (wrinkled)
Right side: Summary — "1:1 ratio reveals heterozygosity; all dominant reveals homozygosity"
Purpose: Show how test cross makes invisible genotypes visible through offspring ratios.

### [FIGURE: Historical Timeline]
Timeline from 1856 to 1900+:
- 1856: Mendel begins pea experiments
- 1865: Mendel presents to local society
- 1866: Mendel publishes in Verhandlungen
- 1868: Mendel becomes abbot; ceases experiments
- 1884: Mendel dies; work remains unknown
- 1900: Three botanists independently rediscover paper
- 1902+: Chromosome theory of inheritance aligns with Mendel's laws
Purpose: Illustrate the gap between discovery and recognition. Show how timing and context enable ideas.

### [FIGURE: Blending Theory vs. Segregation]
Two diagrams side by side:
Left (Blending): Red parent + white parent → pink offspring (intermediate)
Right (Segregation): Red parent (RR) + white parent (rr) → F1 red (Rr) → F2 shows both red and white (3:1), no pink
Purpose: Contrast the old and new theories. Make clear why segregation accounts for reappearance of traits.

### [FIGURE: Incomplete Dominance — Snapdragon Flowers]
Three boxes:
- Red flower (C^R C^R)
- Pink flower (C^R C^W) — intermediate
- White flower (C^W C^W)
Annotation: "The heterozygote shows a third phenotype, not a blend of the parents."
Purpose: Show where Mendelian patterns break down. Introduce incomplete dominance as an extension.

### [FIGURE: Sex-Linked Inheritance — Drosophila Eye Color]
Grid showing:
- Females (XX): X^W X^W (red), X^W X^w (red), X^w X^w (white)
- Males (XY): X^W Y (red), X^w Y (white)
Annotation: "Males express the allele they carry; females need two recessive copies."
Purpose: Show how sex chromosomes create different patterns. Explain why sex-linked traits appear more in males.

### [FIGURE: Epistasis — Pigmentation in Mice]
Table showing genotypes at two loci and phenotypes:
- A_C_ (agouti)
- aa C_ (solid color)
- __cc (albino, regardless of A locus)
Annotation: "The C gene masks the A gene effect. This is epistasis."
Purpose: Demonstrate gene interaction. Show how Mendelian ratios can depart from 9:3:3:1.

## Figure Captions & Learning Notes

Each figure should include:
1. Title (what is being shown)
2. Legend (what each symbol, color, or label means)
3. Key insight (what the learner should notice)
4. Connection to text (which section does this figure illustrate?)

Example caption for Punnett square:
"**2×2 Punnett Square for Monohybrid Cross.** Each parent contributes one allele (V or v) to the offspring. The four boxes show all possible combinations. Two combinations produce heterozygotes (Vv), which are phenotypically identical to the homozygous dominant (VV). Result: genotypic ratio 1:2:1, phenotypic ratio 3:1."

## Callouts & Embedded Diagrams

Where applicable, include mini-diagrams in the text to break up long explanations:
- Small icon of two copies separating (for segregation description)
- Small 2×2 grid (for quick Punnett square reference)
- Small timeline tick mark (for historical moments)
- Small cross symbol (for crossing notation: P × P → F1 → F2)

## Art Notes

- Style: Clear, educational, not ornamental
- Color use: Consistent (e.g., always use the same color for dominant alleles across figures)
- Accessibility: All figures should be understandable in black and white if color is unavailable
- Simplicity: No unnecessary details; each figure has one main message
- Scale and proportion: Realistic for biological accuracy where it matters; stylized otherwise

## Source Attribution

Any historical images (Mendel's portrait, monastery garden) should cite their source and date. Modern diagrams are instructional and do not require attribution unless based on a specific textbook or publication.

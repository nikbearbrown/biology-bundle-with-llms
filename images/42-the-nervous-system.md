# Images Guide: The Nervous System

## Descriptions for illustrations

### Figure 1: Neuron anatomy
- **Diagram:** A multipolar neuron with labeled soma (cell body containing nucleus), multiple dendrites branching from soma, axon hillock, axon extending from soma, and axon terminal with synaptic vesicles.
- **Purpose:** Show students the three basic jobs of a neuron: receiving (dendrites), integrating (soma and axon hillock), transmitting (axon).
- **Detail to emphasize:** The size scale is crucial—dendrites might be 100 μm long, soma ~50 μm, axon can be 1 meter long. This is not a proportional drawing.

### Figure 2: Glial cell types
- **Diagram:** Cross-section of brain showing astrocytes (star-shaped, touching both neurons and blood capillaries), microglia (small, branched, eating debris), oligodendrocytes (wrapping myelin around multiple axons), radial glia (vertical scaffolds for migrating neurons).
- **Purpose:** Establish that "support" does not mean passive. Glia actively buffer, insulate, scavenge, scaffold.
- **Detail to emphasize:** One oligodendrocyte can myelinate multiple axons. One Schwann cell (PNS equivalent) myelinates only one.

### Figure 3: Resting membrane potential and ion distribution
- **Diagram:** Neuron membrane at rest with Na+ concentrated outside, K+ concentrated inside. Sodium-potassium pump (shown expelling 3 Na+ for every 2 K+ in). Leak channels with more K+ leaking out than Na+ leaking in. Result: inside negative relative to outside.
- **Table:** Ion concentrations (Na+ 145 mM outside, 12 inside; K+ 4 mM outside, 155 inside; Cl− 120 mM outside, 4 inside). Voltage bar showing -70 mV.
- **Purpose:** Show students that the resting potential is not accidental; it results from two active mechanisms: the pump (costs ATP) and selective permeability.
- **Detail to emphasize:** The pump does the work, but the leak channels determine the actual resting potential. Without unequal leak, the pump alone would drive the membrane to extreme potentials.

### Figure 4: Action potential phases
- **Graph:** Voltage over time showing resting potential (-70 mV) → threshold (-55 mV) → peak (+40 mV) → repolarization to resting → brief hyperpolarization (-85 mV) → return to rest.
- **Overlay:** Voltage-gated sodium channels (opening at threshold, closing just after peak, showing inactivation state), voltage-gated potassium channels (opening after sodium closes, staying open slightly too long).
- **Purpose:** Visual proof that the action potential is not a single event but a sequence of channel openings and closings.
- **Detail to emphasize:** The inactivation gate of sodium channels closes even when the voltage is still depolarized. This is not the same as the gate opening in reverse; it's a separate mechanical lock.

### Figure 5: Saltatory conduction
- **Diagram:** Myelinated axon showing myelin-wrapped (insulated) segments and nodes of Ranvier (gaps where membrane is exposed). Depolarization shown jumping from node to node.
- **Comparison:** Unmyelinated axon for contrast, showing continuous wave-like propagation.
- **Voltage trace:** A voltage electrode placed at different points along both axons, showing rapid jump in myelinated vs. gradual decay in unmyelinated.
- **Purpose:** Establish that insulation is the key to speed; without it, signal degrades over distance.
- **Detail to emphasize:** The nodes are not tiny. They're about 1 μm long. Ion channels are concentrated there because that's the only place where signal regeneration can occur.

### Figure 6: Chemical synapse
- **Diagram (side view):** Presynaptic terminal filled with synaptic vesicles, presynaptic membrane with voltage-gated calcium channels, synaptic cleft (~20 nm), postsynaptic membrane with receptors, postsynaptic spine.
- **Sequence:** (a) Action potential arrives, depolarizing terminal. (b) Ca2+ channels open, calcium enters. (c) Calcium triggers vesicle fusion. (d) Neurotransmitter released into cleft. (e) Neurotransmitter binds postsynaptic receptors. (f) Ion channels open on postsynaptic side.
- **Purpose:** Show that a synapse is a *structure* with multiple moving parts, not just a magical signal transfer.
- **Detail to emphasize:** The cleft is tiny (20 nm) but finite. Neurotransmitter must diffuse across it. This delay (~1 ms) is slower than electrical conduction but allows signal modulation.

### Figure 7: Neurotransmitter signaling
- **Diagram:** Two scenarios: (A) Glutamate (excitatory) binds to glutamate receptor on postsynaptic membrane, opening Na+ channel. Na+ enters, depolarizing (EPSP). (B) GABA (inhibitory) binds to GABA receptor, opening Cl− channel. Cl− enters, hyperpolarizing (IPSP).
- **Purpose:** Establish that the *same mechanism* (a neurotransmitter binding a receptor and opening an ion channel) can have opposite electrical effects depending on which ion channel opens.
- **Detail to emphasize:** GABA doesn't directly hyperpolarize. It allows Cl− in, which enters because the cell is positive inside and chloride is negative. The effect is hyperpolarization.

### Figure 8: Signal integration at the axon hillock
- **Diagram:** Neuron soma and axon hillock receiving multiple synaptic inputs. Some inputs are excitatory (+), some inhibitory (−). Arrows show inputs converging at axon hillock. A voltage trace shows how subthreshold EPSPs sum to reach threshold.
- **Purpose:** Show that a single neuron is a decision-maker, not a relay. Its output depends on the pattern and timing of inputs.
- **Detail to emphasize:** The axon hillock sums signals from all dendrites in real time. If the total exceeds threshold, the neuron fires; below, it remains silent.

### Figure 9: Central and peripheral nervous system organization
- **Diagram:** Brain and spinal cord (CNS) in the center. Peripheral nerves extending outward. Label major regions: cortex, cerebellum, brainstem, spinal cord. Show autonomic (sympathetic and parasympathetic) and sensory-somatic divisions of PNS.
- **Purpose:** Anatomical reference. Students should see where structures are and how they connect.
- **Detail to emphasize:** The spinal cord is not just a relay. It contains motor neurons, interneurons, and circuits for reflexes. The brainstem maintains homeostasis. The cortex plans.

### Figure 10: Brain regions and their primary functions
- **Diagram:** Lateral view of human brain showing four lobes (frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital) and deeper structures (thalamus, hypothalamus, hippocampus, amygdala, cerebellum, brainstem).
- **Color coding:** Different regions in different colors, each with a brief label of primary function (e.g., frontal = motor cortex + planning + attention; temporal = sound + memory).
- **Purpose:** Establish that the brain is not a homogeneous mass. Different regions have different jobs.
- **Detail to emphasize:** Damage to a region causes predictable deficits. This mapping (brain structure → function) is the evidence for localization.

### Figure 11: Sympathetic vs. parasympathetic nervous system
- **Diagram:** Two parallel flow charts. Sympathetic: spinal cord → preganglionic neuron → ganglion near spinal cord → postganglionic neuron → target organ (heart, lungs, digestion, eyes). Parasympathetic: brainstem and sacral spinal cord → long preganglionic neuron → ganglion near target organ → short postganglionic neuron → target.
- **Effects table:** Heart rate (sympathetic: increases; parasympathetic: decreases). Digestion (sympathetic: inhibited; parasympathetic: stimulated). Pupils (sympathetic: dilate; parasympathetic: constrict).
- **Purpose:** Show that opposing systems have anatomically distinct origins and neurotransmitters.
- **Detail to emphasize:** The preganglionic neuron always releases acetylcholine (both systems). The postganglionic is where they differ: sympathetic releases norepinephrine; parasympathetic usually releases acetylcholine.

### Figure 12: Spinal cord cross-section
- **Diagram:** Transverse section showing gray matter (butterfly-shaped center, containing neuron and glial bodies, dorsal sensory regions, ventral motor regions) and white matter (myelinated axons, surrounding the gray matter).
- **Labels:** Dorsal (back, carries sensory info up), ventral (front, carries motor commands down), dorsal root (sensory neurons entering), ventral root (motor neurons exiting).
- **Purpose:** Show students the structural organization that underlies functional separation of sensory and motor information.
- **Detail to emphasize:** A reflex arc can synapse at this level, without reaching the brain. Sensory → interneuron → motor, all within the spinal cord.

### Figure 13: Long-term potentiation (LTP) mechanism
- **Diagram (before and after):** Before: NMDA receptors at synapse (blocked by Mg2+), normal number of AMPA receptors. After: Mg2+ expelled by strong depolarization, Ca2+ enters through NMDA, new AMPA receptors inserted into postsynaptic membrane.
- **Result:** Next time glutamate is released, more AMPA receptors are available, so more current enters, larger EPSP.
- **Purpose:** Show that synapses can be strengthened by a specific molecular mechanism, not magic.
- **Detail to emphasize:** LTP requires *coincidence*: glutamate present AND postsynaptic depolarization at the same time. This is Hebb's principle ("neurons that fire together wire together") at the molecular level.

### Figure 14: Neurodegenerative disease comparison
- **Table/diagram:** Alzheimer's (hippocampus affected most, amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles, memory loss), Parkinson's (substantia nigra dopamine neurons lost, tremor and rigidity), contrasted with normal brain regions and architecture.
- **Purpose:** Show that different diseases affect different regions, leading to different symptoms.
- **Detail to emphasize:** We know the *where* (which neurons die) but not fully the *why* (what causes the death). This uncertainty constrains treatment.

## Production notes

- **Complexity level:** Figures should be clear enough for first-year biology but detailed enough to show *structure*, not just cartoons. Students should see synaptic vesicles, ion channels as membrane proteins, myelin wrapping pattern.

- **Scale and proportion:** Include scale bars or explicit size notes. A synapse is tiny (20 nm cleft). A neuron soma is ~50 μm. An axon can be 1 m. These differences are crucial for understanding signal speed.

- **Color:** Use consistent color coding across figures. Sodium = one color, potassium = another. Glutamate = excitatory color, GABA = inhibitory color. This reduces cognitive load.

- **Animations:** Saltatory conduction (Figure 5) and action potential phases (Figure 4) are excellent candidates for animation showing depolarization wave and channel state changes over time.

- **Accessibility:** All figures should have text-based descriptions (alt text) for students using screen readers. Avoid relying solely on color to distinguish structures.

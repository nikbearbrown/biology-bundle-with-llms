**Cold Open / Section 1: The Prokaryotic Body Plan**

1. **[FIGURE: Carl Woese at ribosomal RNA sequencer, 1977]** — Black-and-white or color photograph, lab setting. Establishes personage and moment. Caption: "Carl Woese sequencing ribosomal RNA revealed three branches of life, not two."

2. **[FIGURE: Prokaryotic cell anatomy diagram]** — Cross-section showing: plasma membrane, cytoplasm, circular DNA (nucleoid, not nucleus), ribosomes. Label additions: flagella, pili, capsule, plasmid. Simplify; avoid clutter. Caption: "Prokaryotic cell: minimal architecture, maximum versatility."

3. **[FIGURE: Gram staining chemistry → visible result]** — Three panels: (a) Gram-positive bacterium (thick peptidoglycan wall) absorbs crystal violet, stains blue; (b) Gram-negative bacterium (thin wall + outer envelope) resists stain, appears pink/red; (c) Archaean (no peptidoglycan) does not respond. Chemistry → color. Caption: "One stain reveals peptidoglycan: Gram-positive (blue), Gram-negative (red), Archaea (unstained)."

**Section 2: Extremophiles**

4. **[FIGURE: Dead Sea ecosystem]** — Aerial or close photograph of Dead Sea showing stratified water colors (deep blue to reddish mats at shallow edges). Show halophilic bacterial mats in color. Caption: "Halophilic bacteria in Dead Sea mats concentrate pigments (red/orange) for UV protection in hypersaline, acidic conditions."

5. **[FIGURE: Extremophile temperature and pH ranges]** — Chart or visual spectrum showing temperature scale (-15°C to 122°C) and pH scale (3 to 9) with labeled organisms at their optima. Thermophile, hyperthermophile, psychrophile on temperature axis; acidophile, alkaliphile on pH. Caption: "Extremophiles mark the boundaries of known life: temperature and pH do not have universal optima."

**Section 3: Metabolism**

6. **[FIGURE: Metabolic matrix — Energy source × Carbon source]** — 2×2 or 2×3 grid (not a table; visual layout). Photoautotroph (sun + CO2), chemolithoautotroph (chemicals + CO2), chemoorganoheterotroph (organics + organics), etc. Show example organisms in each. Caption: "Prokaryotes partition Earth's energy sources: eight combinations of energy and carbon sources."

7. **[FIGURE: Nitrogen fixation equation with legume nodule cross-section]** — Left side: reaction equation. Right side: legume root nodule showing *Rhizobium* bacteria inside oxygen-free environment, leghemoglobin protecting nitrogenase, plant providing photosynthates. Arrows showing nitrogen fixation → ammonia → plant use. Caption: "Nitrogenase converts atmospheric nitrogen to ammonia (16 ATP per N2 molecule), enabling plants to grow without chemical fertilizer."

**Section 5: Early Life & Biofilms**

8. **[FIGURE: Stromatolites — layered sedimentary structures]** — Photograph of fossilized stromatolite (cross-section showing laminations) and modern stromatolite (Anza-Borrego or similar). Caption: "Stromatolites: mineral-precipitated evidence that prokaryotes built Earth's earliest ecosystems 3.5 billion years ago."

9. **[FIGURE: Microbial mat vertical cross-section]** — Labeled layers: top layer (photosynthetic cyanobacteria, light color), middle layers (mixed bacteria using different metabolic pathways, variable colors), bottom (chemosynthetic bacteria near hydrothermal vent or reduced minerals, dark). Extracellular matrix holding all together. Caption: "Microbial mats: prokaryotic communities partitioning energy sources vertically, each layer metabolizing what layers above and below provide."

10. **[FIGURE: Biofilm resistance to antibiotics]** — Split image: left shows planktonic (free-floating) bacteria killed by antibiotic concentration X. Right shows biofilm with matrix protecting interior cells; same concentration X fails to penetrate; requires 1,000X to kill biofilm. Caption: "Biofilm matrix excludes antibiotics and disinfectants: same bacteria 1,000 times more resistant when in community."

**Section 6: Pathogens**

11. **[FIGURE: Black Death timeline and spread]** — Map of Europe showing Silk Road trade routes with dates (1346 arrival in Mediterranean, 1361 peak). Color gradient showing spread speed (darker = more deaths). Include plague ship and flea-on-rat detail. Caption: "Black Death (1346–1361): Yersinia pestis traveled trade routes, killing ~25% of world population through flea-borne transmission and pneumonic spread."

12. **[FIGURE: MRSA age distribution]** — Side-by-side bar charts: (left) hospitalized MRSA patients, average age 68; (right) community-associated MRSA, average age 23. Visual contrast showing shift to younger population. Caption: "Community-associated MRSA targets younger, healthier people in close quarters—evidence that the pathogen evolves in real time."

**Section 7: Partners**

13. **[FIGURE: Fecal transplant restoring microbiome]** — Three panels: (a) healthy gut with diverse bacterial community (many colored dots representing different species); (b) after antibiotics, *Clostridium difficile* blooms (oversized single color); (c) after fecal transplant, diversity restored, *C. difficile* returns to rare status. Caption: "Removing competitors allows invasive species to bloom: fecal transplant restores ecological balance, eliminating dangerous *C. difficile* overgrowth."

14. **[FIGURE: Oil-degrading bacteria at work]** — Photograph of oil spill (or artistic rendering) with microscopic inset showing *Alcanivorax borkumensis* bacteria with surfactant molecules breaking oil into smaller molecules. Caption: "Oil-degrading bacteria like *Alcanivorax borkumensis* produce surfactants that solubilize petroleum, enabling cleanup after spills like Exxon Valdez and BP Gulf spill."

**Section 4: Three Domains (Woese diagram)**

15. **[FIGURE: Three-domain tree of life]** — Woese-style phylogenetic tree with three equal trunks: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya, all emerging from last universal common ancestor (LUCA). Labeled with representative organisms (E. coli for Bacteria; methanogens for Archaea; humans for Eukarya). Caption: "Carl Woese's three domains: prokaryotes are two separate lineages, equally ancient and equally distinct from eukaryotes."

## Figure Quality Specifications

- **Color photography**: Use actual organisms or habitats where vivid and clear (Dead Sea, stromatolites, bacterial mats)
- **Diagrams**: Simplified, labeled clearly, no unnecessary detail
- **Reactions/equations**: Show on page; single mechanism per figure
- **Scale indicators**: Include size scale or context where relevant (e.g., microscopic bacteria next to oil droplet)
- **Captions**: One to two sentences; explain what mechanism or principle the figure illustrates

# Images and Figures: Plant Form and Physiology

## Figure 1: Cross-Section of Tree Trunk (Annual Rings and Tissue Layers)

**Location in chapter:** Secondary growth section, after "Annual Rings" subsection.

**Description:** Concentric circular cross-section of a tree trunk showing multiple annual rings. Labels point to:
- **Cork (bark)**: Outermost layer, dead, waxy, protective
- **Cork cambium**: Thin layer producing cork
- **Phloem**: Just inside cork, thin layer, contains sieve-tube elements and companion cells
- **Vascular cambium**: Single-cell-wide ring between phloem and xylem, dividing actively
- **Sapwood (secondary xylem)**: Several rings of younger xylem, still conducting water, lighter colored
- **Heartwood (primary xylem)**: Center rings, older xylem, clogged with resins/minerals, darker colored, non-conducting
- **Pith**: Center core, parenchyma tissue

**Annual ring detail (inset):** Zoom showing one year's growth with lighter earlywood (spring growth, large cells, thin walls) and darker latewood (fall growth, smaller cells, thick walls). Boundary between latewood of one year and earlywood of next year is the visible "ring."

**Scale note:** This should represent a tree 20-30 years old, showing enough rings to illustrate pattern without overwhelming detail.

## Figure 2: Cohesion-Tension Pull in Xylem Vessels

**Location in chapter:** Water transport section, deep-dive mechanism on xylem flow.

**Description:** Vertical cross-section view of a xylem vessel showing:

**At top (leaf surface):**
- Leaf mesophyll cell with exposed cell wall to intercellular air space
- Water film on cell wall surface (shown as thin blue layer)
- Evaporation arrow pointing upward from water film
- Thin film getting thinner (shown as narrowing layer)

**In the middle (xylem vessel):**
- Vertical xylem vessel tube shown in cross-section
- Water molecules inside connected to each other by hydrogen bonds (shown as dotted lines between H2O symbols)
- Water molecules adhering to cell wall (shown as H2O symbols touching vessel wall)
- Downward-pointing arrow labeled "Tension pull" spanning the entire vessel

**At bottom (root):**
- Root hair cell
- Water in soil (shown as blue droplets)
- Osmotic gradient arrow showing water entering root hair
- Root cells with high solute concentration (negative water potential)

**Legend/callout boxes:**
- "Cohesion: water molecules stick to each other"
- "Adhesion: water sticks to cell wall"
- "Transpiration pull: evaporation creates tension at leaf surface"
- "This tension is transmitted downward, pulling water column"

## Figure 3: Phototropism and Gravitropism (Dual Mechanism)

**Location in chapter:** Plant responses section, covering light and gravity sensing.

**Description:** Two side-by-side seedling diagrams.

**Left side (Phototropism):**
- Seedling under one-directional light source (light rays from left side)
- Light rays hitting shoot tip
- Phototropin receptors in tip cells (shown as orange protein icons on cell membranes, more active on lit side)
- Auxin molecules (shown as red dots) accumulating on shaded (right) side of tip
- Auxin diffusing down the shaded side cells
- Cells on shaded side shown as elongated (taller)
- Cells on lit side shown as shorter
- Tip of seedling bending toward light source (curved arrow)
- Label: "Blue light → phototropin activation (asymmetric) → auxin redistribution to shaded side → differential elongation → bending toward light"

**Right side (Gravitropism):**
- Seedling laid on its side in dark
- Root tip shown at end of root
- Root cap cells with amyloplasts (shown as heavy blue circles inside cells)
- Amyloplasts settling downward due to gravity (downward-pointing arrow)
- Amyloplasts contacting endoplasmic reticulum (ER shown as network near bottom)
- Calcium ion release (Ca2+ symbols emerging from ER)
- Auxin accumulation on lower side of root (shown as red dots concentrated at bottom)
- Cells on lower (bottom) side of root shown as shorter (auxin inhibits elongation in roots)
- Cells on upper side shown as longer
- Root tip bending downward (curved arrow)
- Label: "Gravity → amyloplast sedimentation → Ca2+ signaling → auxin redistribution to lower side → differential growth → bending downward"

**Key contrast callout:** In shoots, auxin promotes elongation. In roots, auxin inhibits elongation. Same hormone, opposite effects.

## Figure 4: Pressure-Flow Model of Phloem Transport

**Location in chapter:** Sugar flow section, showing source-to-sink transport.

**Description:** Two cells or tissue regions connected by phloem sieve-tube.

**On the left (Source leaf):**
- Photosynthesizing leaf cells producing sucrose (shown as small sugar molecules)
- Sucrose being actively transported (requires ATP, shown as energy arrow) into sieve-tube element (STE)
- High concentration of solutes inside STE (shown as dense red dots)
- Water entering from adjacent xylem (arrow from xylem vessel into STE)
- Pressure building inside STE (shown as outward-pointing arrows on cell walls)
- Companion cell adjacent to STE (shown as cell with nucleus and mitochondria)

**In the middle (Phloem sieve-tube):**
- Connected STEs with sieve plates (perforations) between them
- Pressure gradient shown as color gradient (darker/more concentrated at source, lighter at sink)
- Sap flow direction (downward arrow labeled "bulk flow")

**On the right (Sink root):**
- Growing root cells with few solutes
- Sucrose unloaded from STE into sink cells (shown as sugar molecules moving from tube to cells)
- Water diffusing back into xylem vessel (osmosis, arrow returning water)
- Root cell using sugar for growth and respiration

**Callout boxes:**
- "Source: High sugar concentration → high osmotic pressure → water enters → high turgor pressure"
- "Sink: Low sugar concentration (used for growth) → low turgor pressure"
- "Pressure gradient drives bulk flow from source to sink"

## Figure 6: Water Potential Components

**Location in chapter:** Water potential section, showing the formula.

**Description:** Vertical stacked bar chart or visual representation showing how the four components add up to total water potential:

- **Ψs (solute potential)**: Shown as negative bar (red), label "-0.5 to -1.0 MPa in plant cells"
- **Ψp (pressure potential)**: Shown as positive bar (green), label "0.6-0.8 MPa typical, up to 1.5 MPa"
- **Ψg (gravity potential)**: Shown as small negative bar (blue), label "-0.1 MPa per meter height"
- **Ψm (matric potential)**: Shown as small bar (gray), label "negligible in well-watered tissues, -2 MPa in dry seeds"
- **Total Ψ (water potential)**: Shown as sum of components at bottom

Example calculation shown: If Ψs = -0.8, Ψp = +0.6, Ψg = 0 (ground level), Ψm = 0, then Ψtotal = -0.2 MPa.

## Figure 7: Phytochrome System (Pr/Pfr Interconversion)

**Location in chapter:** Red light responses section.

**Description:** Two state diagram showing phytochrome interconversion:

**State 1 (Pr - Red light absorbing, inactive):**
- Phytochrome protein shown as curved protein shape
- Chromophore (light-absorbing tetrapyrrole ring) shown attached to protein in one configuration
- Label: "Pr (red light absorbing, inactive)"
- Arrow labeled "Red light (667 nm)" pointing to State 2

**State 2 (Pfr - Far-red light absorbing, active):**
- Same protein shown with chromophore in different conformation
- Protein shape slightly changed (indicating conformational change)
- Label: "Pfr (far-red light absorbing, active)"
- Arrow labeled "Far-red light (730 nm)" pointing back to State 1
- Curved arrow labeled "Dark reversion (slow, hours)" also pointing to State 1
- Callout: "Pfr activates gene expression and developmental responses"

**Bottom note:** "In full sunlight (red-rich), phytochrome stays as Pfr (active). In shade (red-depleted), phytochrome shifts toward Pr (inactive). This allows plants to sense shade and shift growth strategy."

## General Notes on Figures

- All figures should use consistent color coding: **blue for water, red for sugars/hormones, green for growth/positive, yellow/orange for light receptors**.
- Arrows should indicate direction of flow, force, or causation. Curved arrows show bending/growth.
- Magnifications and scale should be indicated (μm for cellular level, meters for tree height).
- No figures include external labels in caption; all labeling should be integrated into the figure itself or clearly indicated.
- Figures support the mechanism-first approach: they show how things work, not just what things are.

## Concept 1: The Chordate Body Plan and Vertebrate Spine

### Figure 1A: Chordate Body Plan (Cross-section)
**Description:** Side-view longitudinal section of a generalized chordate. Label: notochord (center), dorsal hollow nerve cord (above), pharyngeal gill slits (with water flow arrows), digestive tube (below), post-anal tail (extending past anus).
**Student focus:** The three-axis organization: notochord runs length-wise; nerve cord sits dorsally; pharyngeal slits indicate water flow through pharynx. These are the diagnostic features *every* chordate has.
**Placement:** Concept 1, after "The Scene."

### Figure 1B: Notochord to Spine Transition
**Description:** Three panels side by side:
- Left: Modern amphioxus cross-section with notochord, no vertebrae.
- Middle: Early vertebrate (early Ordovician) cross-section showing small cartilaginous elements around notochord (proto-vertebrae), vertebral arch beginning to form.
- Right: Modern vertebrate cross-section with full vertebral column, neural arch enclosing spinal cord, intervertebral disc between vertebrae.
**Student focus:** The progression from uniform support (notochord) to segmented protection (vertebral column). Notice the neural arch—the protective cage.
**Placement:** Concept 1, in "The Mechanism" section.

### Figure 1C: Vertebral Column Detail
**Description:** Lateral and anterior views of two stacked vertebrae with labels:
- Vertebral body (centrum)
- Vertebral arch
- Neural spine (dorsal process)
- Transverse process
- Intervertebral disc (between bodies)
- Spinal cord (in neural arch)
**Student focus:** The vertebra is not just a single bone; it's a complex structure with a body for weight-bearing and an arch for neural protection.
**Placement:** Concept 1, in worked example section.

## Concept 2: Jaws and Active Predation

### Figure 2A: Gill Arch to Jaw Evolution
**Description:** Three panels showing evolution of mandibular arch:
- Left: Jawless fish (agnathan) with pharyngeal arches (branchial arches 1, 2, 3...) supporting gills. Arch 1 (mandibular arch) is simple support, not hinged.
- Middle: Early gnathostome (placoderm) with same arches but arch 1 modified—two halves have hinged, teeth-bearing plates forming upper/lower jaw.
- Right: Modern fish (e.g., shark) with sophisticated jawed mouth, teeth, muscles labeled (adductor mandibulae, levator mandibulae).
**Student focus:** The mandibular arch *transforms*, not replaces. Gills remain; the first gill arch becomes the jaw.
**Placement:** Concept 2, in "The Mechanism" section.

### Figure 2B: Jaw Mechanics—Lever Arms
**Description:** Two jaw skulls in dorsal view:
- Left: Short-jaw skull (placoderm). Jaw joint positioned far back under eye. Short jaw lever arm from joint to teeth. Label: "high mechanical advantage, crushing force."
- Right: Long-jaw skull (modern fish). Jaw joint more forward. Long jaw lever arm from joint to teeth. Label: "lower mechanical advantage, speed."
**Student focus:** Position of the jaw joint determines the leverage and therefore the bite force or speed trade-off.
**Placement:** Concept 2, in worked example section.

### Figure 2C: Teeth Evolution
**Description:** Microscopic cross-sections and close-ups:
- Scale on fish skin (keratin structure)
- Tooth-scale (intermediate: keratin + mineral)
- True tooth (dentine + enamel)
**Student focus:** Teeth are modified scales, not new structures.
**Placement:** Concept 2, optional detail figure (can be moved to pantry if space is tight).

## Concept 3: Tetrapod Transition

### Figure 3A: Lobe Fin to Limb Skeleton
**Description:** Skeletal comparison, three rows:
- Row 1 (Eusthenopteron, sarcopterygian fish): Lobe fin with humerus, ulna, radius, then radiating bones. Label: "one-two-many pattern."
- Row 2 (Tiktaalik): Fin with same bones but reorganized, joints modified, orientation shifted under body.
- Row 3 (Modern tetrapod, e.g., salamander): Limb with humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, digits. Same "one-two-many" pattern.
**Student focus:** The skeletal *pattern* is inherited; the function changes. Fin → limb via reorientation and joint modification.
**Placement:** Concept 3, in "The Mechanism" section.

### Figure 3B: Tiktaalik Reconstruction
**Description:** Dorsal view of Tiktaalik fossil and reconstruction showing:
- Eyes on top of head (not sides)
- Neck region (gap between head and body, gill supports regressing)
- Forelimbs with visible bones, oriented under body (not out to side like fish fins)
- Tail (still large, paddle-like)
- Gills and lung outline visible
**Student focus:** This animal *straddles* two worlds. Every feature is intermediate.
**Placement:** Concept 3, in "The Scene" or "Worked Example."

### Figure 3C: Tetrapod Life Cycle—Amphibian vs. Direct Development
**Description:** Two parallel timelines:
- **Amphibian (Frog):** Adult frog → water → eggs laid → tadpoles (aquatic, gills, tail) → metamorphosis → froglets → adult.
- **Amniote (Turtle, covered in Concept 4):** Adult turtle → land → eggs laid in nest → embryos develop inside shell (amniotic fluid visible) → hatchlings emerge → juvenile turtles.
**Student focus:** Amphibians have a larval stage; amniotes go directly from embryo to juvenile.
**Placement:** Concept 3 or early Concept 4, in worked example.

## Concept 4: Amniotic Egg

### Figure 4A: Amniotic Egg Structure
**Description:** Cross-section of a shelled amniotic egg with labels for all four membranes:
- Yolk sac (with arrows showing nutrient transport to embryo via blood vessels)
- Amnion (surrounding embryo in fluid-filled cavity)
- Chorion (next layer out, facilitating gas exchange)
- Allantois (storage sac, connected to embryo via blood vessels)
- Albumin (egg white, outside chorion)
- Shell (hard or leathery, with pores for gas exchange)
**Student focus:** This is a *self-contained world*. The embryo never needs to exchange anything with the environment except gases.
**Placement:** Concept 4 opening, after "The Mechanism" introduction.

### Figure 4B: Amniote vs. Amphibian Reproduction
**Description:** Two-column comparison diagram:
- **Left (Amphibian):** Adult → breeding pool → soft shell-less eggs in water → tadpole (aquatic larva, gills) → metamorphosis → adult.
- **Right (Amniote):** Adult → land anywhere → eggs with shells, laid on land → embryo in amniotic egg (no larval stage) → hatchling → juvenile.
**Student focus:** The amniotic egg eliminated the need for water and the larval stage.
**Placement:** Concept 4, in worked example section.

## Integration/Synthesis: Radiation Diagram

### Figure 5: Tetrapod Radiation from Amniotic Common Ancestor
**Description:** A branching diagram showing:
- Base: Amniote (340 Ma)
- Branch 1: Synapsids → Cynodonts → Mammals (with icons: hair, heterodont teeth, mammary glands)
- Branch 2: Sauropsids splits into:
  - Lepidosaurs: Lizards, snakes, tuataras
  - Archosaurs: Crocodiles, dinosaurs (including birds with feathers, hollow bones)
- Branch 3: Anapsids → Turtles (with reduced temporal fenestrae)
**Student focus:** All modern tetrapod diversity radiates from the amniote common ancestor. The amniotic egg was the key innovation that allowed this diversification.
**Placement:** Synthesis section.

## Notes on Figure Sourcing

- Fossil reconstructions (Tiktaalik, Acanthostega, placoderms): verify against primary paleontological sources and modern museum references.
- Anatomical diagrams (notochord, vertebrae, jaw mechanics): standard anatomy and paleobiology textbook quality.
- Embryological figures (amniotic egg, metamorphosis): embryology texts and standard biology pedagogy.
- Phylogenetic tree (tetrapod radiation): phylogenetic consensus from modern molecular and paleontological evidence.

All figures should be accurate to current scientific understanding and avoid outdated or speculative reconstructions. Emphasize that fossils are incomplete; show what *we know* and label uncertainties.

# Image Briefs: Ch. 32 Introduction to Animal Diversity

## Figure 1: Germ-Layer Differentiation During Gastrulation

**What:** Cross-sectional diagram of a developing embryo during and after gastrulation, showing the three germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm) and their tissue derivatives.

**What the student should notice:**
- The three layers emerge from cell migration and rearrangement during gastrulation.
- Each layer gives rise to specific tissue types (ectoderm → skin, nervous system; endoderm → digestive tract; mesoderm → muscle, bone, connective tissue).
- The mesoderm is sandwiched between ectoderm and endoderm, which constrains its role.

**Caption:** "During gastrulation, a two-layer structure (ectoderm + endoderm) rearranges to form three layers. The mesoderm, arising between the outer and inner layers, gives rise to muscle, skeleton, and organs like heart and kidney. Animals with only two germ layers (diploblasts) cannot build these mesodermal tissues."

## Figure 2: The Animal Phylogenetic Tree (Body-Plan Axes)

**What:** A branching tree showing how body-plan decisions partition animal diversity. Starts with a common ancestor, then branches on: (1) presence/absence of true differentiated tissues, (2) radial vs. bilateral symmetry, (3) diploblast vs. triploblast, (4) coelom type, (5) protostome vs. deuterostome.

**What the student should notice:**
- Each branching point represents a decision made early in animal evolution that constrains all descendant forms.
- Radial animals (cnidarians, ctenophores) are at one branch; bilaterians at another.
- Within bilaterians, protostomes (arthropods, mollusks, annelids) and deuterostomes (echinoderms, chordates) are deeply separated.
- Coelom presence/type further subdivides triploblasts.

**Caption:** "The animal tree branches on a series of embryonic decisions. Once an ancestor commits to radial symmetry, its descendants cannot easily evolve directional hunting. Once an ancestor commits to being a protostome, its cell-division pattern and mouth origin are locked in. These decisions, made hundreds of millions of years ago, still constrain modern animal diversity."

---

## Figure 3: Symmetry Types—Radial, Bilateral, Asymmetrical

**What:** Three simple diagrams: (1) radial symmetry (anemone-like, with tentacles radiating from center), (2) bilateral symmetry (fish-like, with head, tail, left and right sides), (3) asymmetrical (sponge-like, no obvious planes of symmetry).

**What the student should notice:**
- Radial symmetry allows equal sensing in all directions—optimal for passive feeders.
- Bilateral symmetry concentrates sensors at the head—optimal for directed hunting.
- Asymmetry is rare and often represents loss of symmetry (not a primitive state).

**Caption:** "Body symmetry correlates with lifestyle. Radial animals (jellyfish, anemones) typically wait for food; bilateral animals (fish, insects) typically hunt. The sponge has no consistent symmetry because it is a passive filter-feeder with a simple body plan."

---

## Figure 4: Coelom Types—Acoelomate, Pseudocoelomate, Eucoelomate

**What:** Three side-by-side cross-sections of animal bodies, showing: (1) acoelomate (solid tissue between gut and body wall), (2) pseudocoelomate (cavity between gut and body wall, partly lined by mesoderm, partly by endoderm), (3) eucoelomate (cavity fully lined by mesoderm, with organs inside).

**What the student should notice:**
- The coelom is a luxury: it provides space for organs to grow and move.
- Acoelomates (flatworms) pack solid tissue everywhere—less flexibility.
- Pseudocoelomates (roundworms) have a partial solution—some organ isolation but not complete.
- Eucoelomates (mollusks, arthropods, vertebrates) have full organ isolation and can build large, complex bodies.

**Caption:** "The body cavity (coelom) is lined by tissue derived from mesoderm in true coelomates. In acoelomates, there is no cavity—organs are packed in solid tissue. In pseudocoelomates, the cavity is only partially mesodermal. This difference constrains organ development and body size."

---

## Figure 5: Protostome vs. Deuterostome Embryogenesis

**What:** Two parallel developmental sequences side by side: (1) protostome (spiral cleavage, early cell-fate determination, blastopore becomes mouth), (2) deuterostome (radial cleavage, indeterminate cell fate, blastopore becomes anus).

**What the student should notice:**
- In protostomes, the cleavage pattern spirals—cells rotate relative to each other.
- In deuterostomes, the cleavage pattern stays aligned—cells divide in parallel planes.
- Cell fate is locked in early for protostomes (remove a cell, and you get a malformed embryo).
- Cell fate is plastic longer for deuterostomes (remove a cell, and neighbors compensate).

**Caption:** "These two embryonic pathways diverged hundreds of millions of years ago and remain consistent across their respective clades. Protostome development is rapid and deterministic. Deuterostome development is more flexible, allowing indeterminate cells (like embryonic stem cells) to remain plastic longer."

## Figure 6: Cambrian Diversity Timeline

**What:** A vertical timeline spanning 700 Mya to 400 Mya, showing fossil occurrences: pre-Cambrian simple life (Ediacaran, Cryogenian), then Cambrian phyla (trilobites, anomalocarids, early chordates, mollusks, echinoderms, arthropods), then Ordovician onward.

**What the student should notice:**
- Most major animal phyla appear within ~30 million years (Cambrian).
- Before the Cambrian, animals are rare and simple in the fossil record.
- After the Cambrian, animal diversity continues to increase but does not spike as dramatically.
- The gap between pre-Cambrian and Cambrian is striking.

**Caption:** "The Cambrian explosion (541–511 Mya) marks the rapid appearance of most major animal phyla in the fossil record. Earlier fossils (Ediacaran, Cryogenian) show simpler life forms. The cause of this explosion remains debated: atmospheric oxygen, ecological competition, genetic innovations, or preservational artifact all play a role."

## Figure 7: Hox Gene Collinearity and Body-Plan Control

**What:** A diagram showing *Hox* gene clusters (as a series of colored boxes) and how their position on the chromosome correlates with their function along the anterior-posterior axis of the body. Show a simple animal (like a fruit fly) with *Hox* genes labeled to anterior structures, middle structures, and posterior structures.

**What the student should notice:**
- *Hox* genes are arranged in a cluster where gene order on the chromosome matches body-region order.
- Anterior genes (like *Hox 1*) control head and front structures.
- Posterior genes (like *Hox 13*) control tail and rear structures.
- Gene duplication events allow more *Hox* genes, enabling more body-region complexity.

**Caption:** "The order of genes on the *Hox* cluster mirrors the anterior-posterior axis of the body. This *collinearity* is one of the most remarkable discoveries in developmental genetics. Gene duplication events during animal evolution allowed vertebrates to evolve four *Hox* clusters, enabling more complex body plans than invertebrates with one cluster."

## Figure 8: Pre-Cambrian and Cambrian Fossils

**What:** A gallery of fossil images (or reconstructions): Ediacaran soft-bodied organisms, sponge-like *Coronacollina acula* (560 Mya), and Cambrian fauna (trilobite, *Anomalocaris*, early echinoderm-like forms, early chordate).

**What the student should notice:**
- Pre-Cambrian fossils are simple and rare.
- Cambrian fossils are diverse and show complex structures (eyes, exoskeletons, appendages).
- *Anomalocaris* is a large, complex predator, implying an arms race was already underway.
- Trilobites appear fully formed with no clear ancestors.

**Caption:** "Cambrian fossils document a rapid diversification of animal body plans. Notable are trilobites (with compound eyes), *Anomalocaris* (a large predator), and early representatives of modern phyla. The earlier Ediacaran fauna were simpler and often lack clear descendants among Cambrian animals."

## Figure 9: The Molecular Reorganization of the Animal Tree

**What:** Before/after phylogenetic trees: (1) morphological tree (showing traditional groupings based on shape), (2) molecular tree (showing rDNA/DNA-based groupings). Highlight major changes: lophophorates move from deuterostomes to protostomes; arthropods move closer to nematodes (Ecdysozoa); ctenophores move to the base of the tree.

**What the student should notice:**
- Morphological similarity can mislead—flatness evolved multiple times (flatworms and acoels).
- Genetic data reveals kinship that morphology hides.
- The animal tree is not "settled"—as new data accumulate, branches shift.

**Caption:** "Molecular data (DNA and RNA sequences) have reorganized our understanding of animal relationships. Organisms once grouped by appearance are now separated; distantly related forms are now grouped together. This revision shows the power of molecular evidence and the importance of testing evolutionary hypotheses."

## Production Notes

- **Style:** Attenborough × Feynman. Simple, clear line drawings or diagrams. No unnecessary decoration. Labels in plain English; jargon defined in captions.
- **Color:** Use a consistent palette. Suggest: cool colors (blues) for water/ancient time, warm colors (reds) for Cambrian/Cambrian fauna, neutral tones for diagram elements.
- **Accessibility:** Ensure all diagrams are colorblind-friendly (no red-green only).

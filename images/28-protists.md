# Image Briefs — Chapter 28: Protists

## Hero Image (Chapter Opening)

**Brief:** Microscopic scene at ~400x magnification of pond water with diverse protists in motion. The composition should show several cell types simultaneously: a slipper-shaped ciliate (like Paramecium) with visible cilia, an amoeba with pseudopodia extending, a photosynthetic protist (green-colored, single cell), and a parasitic protist (if possible, show size scale). The water medium should be evident. Style: scientific illustration with color, not photographic.

**What the student should notice:** "Not all of these are obviously alive or obviously classified the same way. Some move with appendages, some change shape, some are green, some are not. Yet all are single eukaryotic cells."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Embodies the chapter-opening puzzle: diversity within a category defined by what it's NOT.

**Alternatives if commissioning artist:** (1) Annotated electron microscopy montage of protist cell types with labels; (2) Historical drawing (van Leeuwenhoek style) with modern accuracy added; (3) Split-panel showing pond water at macro scale (top) and the same water at microscopic scale (bottom) revealing the protist city.

## Concept 1: Paraphyly of "Protista"

**Brief:** Phylogenetic tree diagram (cladogram) showing the six supergroups of eukaryotes and the position of plants, animals, and fungi within or adjacent to protist branches. The tree should make visually clear that:
- Plants nest inside green algae (Archaeplastida)
- Animals nest inside Opisthokonta (specifically, descended from choanoflagellate-like ancestors)
- Fungi are also in Opisthokonta

A highlighted circle labeled "Protista (traditional definition)" should encompass protist branches but NOT include all descendants of their common ancestor; the circle should visibly exclude animals/plants/fungi, making the paraphyly obvious.

**What the student should notice:** "The circle labeled 'Protista' either includes too much (if it includes animals/plants/fungi) or too little (if it excludes them). There's no way to circle only protists and have them be a clade."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Visceral understanding of paraphyly.

**Alternatives:** (1) Two phylogenetic trees side-by-side: one showing traditional kingdom Protista (with circle), one showing modern supergroup classification (without artificial boundaries); (2) Venn diagram showing the problem.

## Concept 2: Endosymbiotic Membranes

**Brief:** Three-panel diagram sequence showing (1) primary endosymbiosis (bacterium engulfed, 2-membrane result), (2) secondary endosymbiosis (eukaryotic cell containing chloroplast engulfed, 4-membrane result), and (3) cross-section of the multi-membrane secondary chloroplast showing the nucleomorph (remnant nucleus of engulfed cell).

Each panel should show:
- The larger host cell (with nucleus visible)
- The engulfed cell(s)
- Membranes counted and labeled (original bacterium cell membrane, engulfing vesicle membrane, engulfed eukaryote's cell membrane, outer vesicle membrane)
- Resulting organelle with correct membrane count

**What the student should notice:** "Each engulfment event adds membranes. The number of membranes tells you the evolutionary history."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Membrane count as molecular evidence of endosymbiotic events. Foundation for Worked Example 1 in the chapter.

**Alternatives:** (1) Animation sequence (if motion graphics available); (2) Split photomicrograph showing TEM (transmission electron microscopy) of actual 2-membrane and 4-membrane chloroplasts side-by-side.

## Concept 3: Phytoplankton Bloom Satellite Image

**Brief:** Satellite image (from NASA or NOAA) showing an actual phytoplankton bloom — colors should indicate high concentrations of photosynthetic protists (usually blues, greens, or turquoise water). Caption should note location, date, and species composition if available.

**What the student should notice:** "This is millions of tons of single-celled protists. Visible from space. Each cell is microscopic, but collectively, they color the ocean."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Scale shift moment for Concept 3. Connects cellular biology to planetary visibility. Supports the "25% of Earth's photosynthesis" statistic with visual evidence.

**Alternatives:** (1) Time-series of the same bloom over a week (showing rapid growth); (2) Global map of chlorophyll-a concentrations (derived from satellite data) with regions of high protist activity highlighted; (3) Historical comparison (pre-industrial vs. modern-day bloom sizes/locations).

## Synthesis: Eukaryotic Complexity Pathways

**Brief:** Comparison diagram with two paths:

**Path 1 (Multicellular):** Starting point = eukaryotic cell with nucleus + mitochondria. Arrow leads to "Specialization → Tissues → Organs → (Examples: plants, animals, fungi)". Final product has multiple cell types but simpler individual cells.

**Path 2 (Protist):** Starting point = eukaryotic cell with nucleus + mitochondria. Arrows for endosymbiotic events → secondary endosymbiosis → metabolic flexibility → (Examples: diatoms, dinoflagellates, Euglena). Final product is a single cell of extraordinary intricacy.

Both paths should converge at a final box: "Both are complex. Different strategies. Neither is primitive."

**What the student should notice:** "Complexity can be built within a cell or among cells. Both solutions work."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Chapter-level integration. Shows that the two strategies are equally valid, just different. Supports the moral arrival at chapter end.

**Alternatives:** (1) Complexity comparison on two axes (cellular complexity vs. organismal complexity); (2) Historical timeline showing when each strategy emerged in evolutionary history.

## Parasitic Protists: Life Cycle (Malaria Example)

**Brief:** Circular life cycle diagram showing *Plasmodium* transmission:
- Mosquito ingests infected blood (gametocytes visible in blood cells)
- Sexual reproduction in mosquito gut
- Sporozoites migrate to mosquito salivary glands
- Mosquito transmits sporozoites to human
- Sporozoites travel to liver (exoerythrocytic stage)
- Released merozoites infect red blood cells (erythrocytic stage)
- Red blood cell lysis → parasites released → fever episode
- Some parasites become gametocytes (cycle repeats)

Color code: one color for mosquito stage, different color for human stage.

**What the student should notice:** "This is not simple infection. It requires two host species, sexual reproduction in the mosquito, and precise timing. This is specialization."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Demonstrates the complexity of parasitic life cycles. Supports the point that parasitism is not a crude strategy but a refined one.

**Alternatives:** (1) Side-by-side comparison of *Plasmodium* vs. *Trypanosoma* life cycles (showing different structures of two-host requirement); (2) Detailed histological cross-section of liver cells infected with *Plasmodium* sporozoites.

## Ecological Impact: Carbon Pump

**Brief:** Vertical ocean column diagram showing:
- Sunlit euphotic zone (top) with phytoplankton (protists) photosynthesizing
- Zooplankton consuming phytoplankton (food chain arrows)
- Some phytoplankton cells sinking directly (larger arrows)
- Decomposition happening at intermediate depths
- Deep ocean/abyssal zone (bottom) with carbon-rich sediment accumulating
- A vertical "carbon pump" arrow drawn from surface to depth

Color gradient: blue at surface (light), darker blue to black at depth (darkness).

**What the student should notice:** "Not all photosynthetic carbon becomes food. Some sinks directly, removing CO₂ from the atmosphere for hundreds of years."

**Pedagogical purpose:** Ecological mechanism. Supports "25% of global photosynthesis" and "biological carbon pump" claims. Makes abstract climate concept concrete.

**Alternatives:** (1) 3D ocean perspective (bird's eye view of bloom + side cross-section); (2) Animated version showing particles sinking over time; (3) Comparison diagram showing carbon pump in different ocean regions (equatorial vs. polar).

## Notes on Style and Accuracy

- All diagrams should avoid anthropomorphization. Protists are not "cute" or "trying to" anything.
- Phylogenetic trees should follow modern supergroup classification (Adl et al., 2019) unless historical context is the point.
- Satellite imagery should cite source (NASA, NOAA, ESA) and date.
- TEM/EM images of chloroplasts should show actual membrane counts if possible (educational authenticity).
- Do not oversimplify immune evasion in parasites — show the actual complexity (antigenic variation, for malaria).

## Figure 1: Glycolysis Overview — Two Phases

**Location in chapter:** Concept 1 section, after the mechanism explanation.

**Concept it serves:** The two-phase structure of glycolysis (energy investment in steps 1–3, energy extraction in steps 6–10).

**Visual intent:** 
Show glucose (six-carbon ring) on the left entering the investment phase. Depict the two ATP molecules being consumed, the addition of phosphate groups, and the splitting of the sugar into two three-carbon molecules. Then show the extraction phase where the three-carbon molecules are oxidized, NADH is formed, phosphates are transferred to ADP (forming ATP), and pyruvate emerges.

Use color to distinguish:
- Glucose and intermediates: one color (say, blue)
- Phosphate groups: another color (say, orange)
- Electrons (or NADH carriers): a third (say, purple)
- ATP molecules: distinct highlight (say, green)

**Key insight students should notice:** The two phases are asymmetrical—investment costs 2 ATP upfront, but extraction produces 4 ATP, for a net gain of 2. The pathway is front-loaded with a loss that is recouped later.

**Alternative titles:**
- "Glycolysis: Spending to Earn"
- "The Two Faces of Glycolysis"
- "Investment and Payoff in the First Glucose Breakdown"

## Figure 2: The Citric Acid Cycle — The Carbon Furnace

**Location in chapter:** Concept 2 section, after the mechanism explanation.

**Concept it serves:** The complete oxidation of the acetyl group and the cyclic regeneration of oxaloacetate.

**Visual intent:**
Show the cycle as a circle or loop (emphasizing its circular nature). Place oxaloacetate (4-carbon) at the "top" and show:
- Acetyl-CoA (2-carbon) entering and condensing with oxaloacetate to form citrate (6-carbon)
- The path through the eight steps, with key intermediates labeled: isocitrate, α-ketoglutarate, succinyl-CoA, succinate, fumarate, malate
- Arrows indicating where NADH is produced (three times: isocitrate→α-ketoglutarate, α-ketoglutarate→succinyl-CoA, malate→oxaloacetate)
- Arrows indicating where FADH₂ is produced (succinate→fumarate)
- Arrows indicating where ATP/GTP is produced (succinyl-CoA→succinate)
- Arrows indicating where CO₂ is released (two times)
- Oxaloacetate regenerated at the end, ready for the next turn

Use color to distinguish:
- The carbon skeleton: one color (e.g., black or dark blue)
- The two carbons from the acetyl group: a bright color (e.g., red) so students can track them
- NADH-producing steps: one color (purple)
- FADH₂-producing steps: another color (orange)
- ATP-producing step: green

**Key insight students should notice:** The cycle regenerates the starting molecule, so it can turn again. The two carbons from the acetyl group are completely oxidized and released as CO₂. The four-carbon oxaloacetate is always regenerated, making the cycle "self-sustaining."

**Alternative titles:**
- "The Krebs Cycle: Burning Away the Carbons"
- "Complete Oxidation in Eight Steps"
- "The Citric Acid Cycle: Carbon In, CO₂ Out"

## Figure 3: The Electron Transport Chain — Stepwise Energy Extraction

**Location in chapter:** Concept 3 section, after the mechanism explanation.

**Concept it serves:** The flow of electrons through four complexes, the pumping of protons at three sites, and the formation of the proton gradient.

**Visual intent:**
Show the inner mitochondrial membrane as a horizontal bar. Place the four complexes (I, II, III, IV) embedded in the membrane. Show:
- NADH and FADH₂ arriving from the matrix with their high-energy electrons
- Electrons moving from Complex I through ubiquinone (Q) to Complex III, and from Complex II through Q to Complex III
- Electrons moving from Complex III through cytochrome c to Complex IV
- At Complexes I, III, and IV: show protons being pumped from the matrix (bottom) to the intermembrane space (top), creating an accumulation of H+ ions on the outer side
- At Complex IV: show electrons meeting oxygen and protons, forming water
- Below the membrane: show the proton gradient (high concentration of H+ on the outside, low on the inside)
- Show ATP synthase straddling the membrane, with protons flowing through it and ATP being made

Use color to distinguish:
- High-energy electrons: bright (e.g., yellow)
- Low-energy electrons: dim (e.g., gray)
- Protons being pumped: direction arrows in red
- Proton gradient: a gradient from dark (high H+) on the outside to light (low H+) inside
- ATP synthase: a distinct machine-like shape, separate from the complexes

**Key insight students should notice:** Electrons lose energy as they move through the chain. That energy is captured by pumping protons. The gradient is the "battery" that drives ATP synthesis.

**Alternative titles:**
- "Electrons Falling, Protons Pumping"
- "The Electron Transport Chain: Energy Extraction in Motion"
- "Building the Proton Gradient, Step by Step"

## Figure 4: ATP Synthase — The Molecular Motor

**Location in chapter:** Concept 3 section, in the worked example or immediately following.

**Concept it serves:** How the proton gradient is converted into ATP through the mechanical rotation of ATP synthase.

**Visual intent:**
Show a close-up of ATP synthase straddling the inner mitochondrial membrane. Depict:
- The F0 subunit (membrane-embedded rotor) anchored to the membrane with the c-ring subunits forming the rotor that turns
- The F1 subunit (catalytic head) sitting on top, with three catalytic sites
- Protons flowing down the gradient, through the rotor, driving it to turn
- As the rotor turns, the three catalytic sites cycle through conformational changes: one site binds ADP and phosphate, another catalyzes the condensation to form ATP, a third releases ATP
- A "before" and "after" or sequence of states showing how rotation drives the catalytic cycle

Use color to distinguish:
- The rotor (F0): one color (e.g., blue)
- The catalytic head (F1): another color (e.g., green)
- Protons flowing through: arrows in red
- ATP being produced and released: green highlight

**Key insight students should notice:** The turbine-like rotor actually rotates. This is not metaphorical; the rotation is real and has been directly observed using single-molecule imaging. Each proton flowing through contributes to the rotation, and the rotation drives ATP synthesis through mechanical and chemical coupling.

**Alternative titles:**
- "ATP Synthase: Proton Flow Meets Rotation"
- "The Tiniest Motor in Life"
- "How a Gradient Becomes ATP: The Mechanics of ATP Synthase"

## Figure 5: Complete Energy Accounting from One Glucose

**Location in chapter:** Integration/Synthesis section.

**Concept it serves:** The big picture: where all the ATP and electron carriers come from, and what happens to them in the electron transport chain.

**Visual intent:**
Create a summary flow diagram showing:
- One glucose molecule on the left
- Arrow into glycolysis, yielding 2 pyruvate, 2 ATP (net), and 2 NADH
- Two pyruvate molecules entering mitochondrion
- Arrow into pyruvate dehydrogenase, yielding 2 acetyl-CoA and 2 NADH
- Two acetyl-CoA molecules entering the citric acid cycle (shown as a loop)
- First turn of the cycle: 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 ATP
- Second turn of the cycle: 3 NADH + 1 FADH₂ + 1 ATP
- Total accounting box: 10 NADH, 2 FADH₂, 4 ATP (direct)
- Arrow showing NADH and FADH₂ entering the electron transport chain
- Calculation box showing ATP produced from chemiosmosis (roughly 28–30 more ATP)
- Final total: approximately 32–34 ATP per glucose

Use color to distinguish:
- The three pathways: glycolysis (one color), mitochondrial pathways (another), electron transport (a third)
- Direct ATP production: green boxes
- Electron carriers (NADH, FADH₂): purple arrows
- Final ATP from chemiosmosis: light green or highlighted

**Key insight students should notice:** Most ATP (roughly 28–30 out of 32) comes from the electron transport chain. Direct synthesis (substrate-level phosphorylation) contributes only 4 ATP. The electron carriers are the real currency of energy extraction.

**Alternative titles:**
- "From Glucose to ATP: The Complete Accounting"
- "Where Does the Energy in Food Go?"
- "The ATP Budget: Direct vs. Chemiosmotic"

## Figure 6: Proton Gradient as an Electrochemical Battery

**Location in chapter:** Concept 3, in the worked example section.

**Concept it serves:** The proton gradient is more than just a concentration gradient; it is an electrochemical gradient with both concentration and electrical components.

**Visual intent:**
Show the inner mitochondrial membrane with a concentration gradient displayed (high H+ on the outside, low H+ on the inside) and an electrical gradient (negative charge inside, positive outside). Show:
- The physical difference in the number of H+ ions on each side (depicted as dots or small spheres, denser on one side)
- The electrical charge distribution (+ symbols on the outside, – symbols inside)
- Arrows showing the two driving forces: the concentration difference "pushing" protons inward, and the electrical difference "pulling" positive charges inward
- Together, these create the electrochemical gradient
- Show ATP synthase in the membrane as the "channel" through which protons flow when allowed

Use color to distinguish:
- High concentration side: darker color for H+ ions
- Low concentration side: lighter color
- Electrical charges: red for + and blue for –
- The combined gradient: show it as a sort of "downhill" slope that protons want to tumble down

**Key insight students should notice:** The gradient has two components (concentration and electrical), and both drive protons in the same direction. This is why even a small difference in pH (one unit = tenfold difference in proton concentration) is powerful—it combines with the membrane potential to create a strong driving force.

**Alternative titles:**
- "The Gradient: Chemistry and Electricity Combined"
- "Proton Gradient as a Dual-Force Battery"
- "What Powers ATP Synthase: The Complete Picture"

## Figure 7: Feedback Inhibition in Glycolysis (Optional)

**Location in chapter:** Could be in misconceptions section or omitted if space is tight.

**Concept it serves:** How cells regulate glycolysis in response to energy status; the principle that pathways are not "greedy" but responsive.

**Visual intent:**
Show the glycolytic pathway with the three rate-limiting enzymes highlighted (hexokinase, phosphofructokinase, pyruvate kinase). Show:
- High ATP and NADH levels inhibiting phosphofructokinase (with inhibitory arrows in red)
- High citrate (from the citric acid cycle) also inhibiting phosphofructokinase
- Low ATP and high ADP levels activating phosphofructokinase (with activating arrows in green)
- The effect: when energy is abundant (high ATP), glycolysis slows; when energy is scarce (high ADP), glycolysis speeds up

Use color to distinguish:
- Activating signals: green
- Inhibiting signals: red
- The rate-limiting enzymes: darker shade or outlined

**Key insight students should notice:** Pathways are self-regulating. The cell does not need a conscious decision to slow down glycolysis when ATP is abundant—the enzymes themselves respond to the energy state.

**Alternative titles:**
- "Glycolysis Responds to What the Cell Needs"
- "ATP Talks Back: Feedback Inhibition"
- "Why Pathways Don't Run Away"

**Location in chapter:** Synthesis section or exercises.

**Concept it serves:** Why aerobic respiration is so much more successful than anaerobic (fermentation). Visual contrast between the two strategies.

**Visual intent:**
Create a side-by-side comparison:

**Left side (Anaerobic – Glycolysis only):**

- Total ATP: 2

- One glucose enters

**Visual contrast:**

## Production Notes

- **Diagram style:** Clear, labeled, pedagogical. Not photorealistic (they are abstractions, not photographs).
- **Color scheme:** Consistent across all figures (same color for NADH, for example, across multiple figures).
- **Labels:** Every structure, molecule, and process is labeled clearly. No ambiguity.
- **Arrows:** Direction of electron flow, proton flow, and chemical transformation is always indicated.
- **Legends:** Each figure includes a brief legend explaining the color code and main structures.
- **Captions:** Each figure will have a caption (written separately in the chapter) that explains what the student should notice.

## Omitted Figures

**Individual enzyme structures:** The chapter does not show the detailed 3D structure of enzymes like hexokinase, isocitrate dehydrogenase, or ATP synthase at atomic resolution. These are pedagogically rich but would distract from the pathway logic. If an advanced student wants to explore enzyme structure, that belongs in a dedicated structural biochemistry unit.

**Protein complexes in atomic detail:** Complex I, III, and IV are large, multi-subunit machines. Their atomic structures are known, but rendering them accurately would be overwhelming. The conceptual view (electron flow, proton pumping) is sufficient for the learning objectives.

**Evolutionary tree of respiration:** The chapter hints that glycolysis is ancient and was later augmented by the citric acid cycle and electron transport chain. A figure showing the evolutionary timeline of these pathways could be powerful but is held for a later chapter on metabolic evolution.

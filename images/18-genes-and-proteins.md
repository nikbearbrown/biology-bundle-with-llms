# Image briefs — Chapter 18: Genes and Proteins

Specifications for figures to be created. Each figure has pedagogical purpose tied to a concept.

---

## Figure 1: The Central Dogma — Information Flow

**Caption:** One DNA gene produces many mRNA copies, each translated by multiple ribosomes simultaneously. The two-step design allows parallelism and protects the original DNA.

**Content:**
- Left: A DNA double helix, labeled "DNA template strand" and "DNA coding strand."
- Arrow labeled "Transcription" pointing to: Multiple mRNA molecules, each shown as a single line, emerging from the DNA.
- Right: For each mRNA, multiple ribosomes (shown as round/oval shapes) translating simultaneously. Protein chains emerging from each ribosome.
- Labels: "Transcription," "Translation," "mRNA (temporary copy)," "Many ribosomes, one mRNA," "Many proteins from one gene."

**Student should notice:**
- DNA is untouched; the copy is mRNA.
- mRNA is the intermediate that allows parallelism.
- One gene can produce many proteins in parallel.

**Tied to:** Core Concept 1 (The Central Dogma).

## Figure 2: Transcription — RNA Polymerase Reads the Template Strand

**Caption:** RNA polymerase binds to the promoter (in this case, the -10 and -35 boxes in prokaryotes) and reads the template strand 3' to 5', synthesizing mRNA 5' to 3'.

**Content:**
- Top: DNA double helix, showing the promoter region with -35 box and -10 box labeled. The template strand is highlighted.
- Middle: RNA polymerase (as a large enzyme complex) bound to the promoter. The sigma factor is shown as a component of the holoenzyme.
- A "transcription bubble" where the DNA is unwound, the polymerase is reading the template strand (3' to 5' direction shown with arrow), and mRNA is being synthesized (5' to 3' direction shown with arrow).
- Bottom: The emerging mRNA transcript, base-paired to the template strand inside the bubble.
- Labels: "Promoter (-35, -10)," "RNA polymerase core enzyme + sigma factor," "Sigma factor confers specificity," "Template strand (read 3' to 5')," "mRNA synthesized 5' to 3'," "Direction of transcription."

**Student should notice:**
- The promoter is recognized before transcription starts.
- The template strand is the one being read, not the coding strand.
- The direction of reading is 3' to 5'; the direction of synthesis is 5' to 3'.

**Tied to:** Core Concept 2 (Transcription).

## Figure 3: The Genetic Code — Codon Degeneracy

**Caption:** The 64 codons map to 20 amino acids. Most amino acids have multiple codons. Codons differing only in the third position often specify the same amino acid, providing error protection.

**Content:**
- A partial codon table (or "genetic code wheel") showing the structure. For example:
  - GC__ codons (GCU, GCC, GCA, GCG) all code for alanine.
  - UU_ codons (UUU, UUC for phenylalanine) and UUA, UUG (for leucine).
  - GA_ codons (GAU, GAC for aspartate; GAA, GAG for glutamate).
- Color-coding: Same amino acids are the same color. Highlight that changes in the third position often do not change the amino acid.
- Inset showing: A point mutation in the third position (e.g., GCC → GCU) results in the same amino acid (alanine), a "silent mutation."
- Labels: "Third position wobble," "Silent mutation," "Degenerate but not ambiguous," "64 codons → 20 amino acids."

**Student should notice:**
- The genetic code has structure; it is not random.
- Degeneracy is concentrated in the third codon position.
- Chemically similar amino acids have similar codons.

**Tied to:** Core Concept 3 (Translation and Genetic Code).

## Figure 4: Translation — The Ribosome Reading mRNA

**Caption:** The ribosome has three tRNA binding sites (A, P, E). As the ribosome moves along the mRNA, tRNAs enter the A site, base-pair with the codon, form a peptide bond, and then move to the P and E sites. The ribosome advances one codon at a time.

**Content:**
- A ribosome (shown as a large structure, with small and large subunits indicated) sitting on an mRNA strand.
- The mRNA is threaded through the ribosome, with three codons visible in the ribosome binding sites.
- The A site (aminoacyl): showing a tRNA entering with the correct anticodon pairing with the codon. The amino acid carried by this tRNA is at the top of the tRNA structure.
- The P site (peptidyl): showing a tRNA with the previous amino acid, bonded to the amino acid carried by the A-site tRNA (shown as a black line = peptide bond).
- The E site (exit): showing an empty tRNA about to be released.
- Arrows showing the direction of movement: tRNA from A → P → E.
- Labels: "mRNA codon," "tRNA anticodon," "Base pairing," "Peptide bond," "A site (incoming)," "P site (carrying chain)," "E site (exit)," "Ribosome advances 3 nucleotides," "5' to 3' direction."

**Student should notice:**
- The anticodon-codon pairing is the recognition mechanism.
- Peptide bonds form between amino acids, not between nucleotides and amino acids.
- The ribosome's motion is in steps, one codon at a time.

**Tied to:** Core Concept 3 (Translation).

## Figure 5: Frameshift Mutation — Reading Frame Shift

**Caption:** A frameshift mutation (insertion or deletion of a nucleotide) shifts the reading frame, changing all downstream amino acids until a stop codon is encountered.

**Content:**
- Top row: Normal mRNA with codons marked in boxes. Translation shown above: AUG / GCC / GAU / AAA / UAA (Met / Ala / Asp / Lys / STOP).
- Middle row: After insertion of a single U after the start codon (AUG U). The new reading frame is shifted: AUG U GC / CGA / UUA / AAU / AA? The boxes are offset, showing the shift. Translation shown: Met / Val / Leu / Asn / ?
- Bottom row: The effect is highlighted: every amino acid downstream is wrong, and the protein is likely terminated at a different stop codon or is truncated.
- Labels: "Normal reading frame," "Insertion of U," "Shifted reading frame," "All downstream amino acids changed," "Frameshift mutation."

**Student should notice:**
- Frameshift mutations have catastrophic effects.
- The reading frame is read in triplets, and any break in the pattern ruins the downstream sequence.
- This is why the triplet codon structure is so important.

**Tied to:** Core Concept 3 and Exercises (Frameshift Mutations).

## Figure 6: Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic Transcription and Translation

**Caption:** In prokaryotes, transcription and translation occur simultaneously in the cytoplasm. In eukaryotes, they are separated in space and time: transcription in the nucleus, translation in the cytoplasm, with mRNA processing in between.

**Content:**
- Left side (Prokaryote):
  - A bacterial cell with a circular DNA chromosome in the nucleoid region.
  - Transcription occurring on the DNA (RNA polymerase + DNA visible).
  - Ribosomes already attaching to and translating the mRNA that is still being transcribed (no break between transcription and translation).
  - Label: "Simultaneous transcription and translation."
  
- Right side (Eukaryote):
  - A eukaryotic cell with a nucleus (large circle) and cytoplasm.
  - Inside nucleus: DNA on a chromosome (packaged with histones), RNA polymerase II transcribing, and the nascent mRNA being processed (5' cap, 3' poly-A tail, splicing shown).
  - mRNA exiting the nucleus through nuclear pores.
  - In cytoplasm: Ribosomes translating the mature mRNA.
  - Labels: "Transcription (nucleus)," "mRNA processing (5' cap, 3' tail, splicing)," "Nuclear export," "Translation (cytoplasm)."

**Student should notice:**
- The spatial separation allows for mRNA processing and regulation.
- Prokaryotes couple transcription and translation for speed.
- Eukaryotes trade speed for control.

**Tied to:** Core Concepts 2 and 3, and the Integration section (Parallelism and Trade-offs).

## Figure 7: tRNA Structure — Adapter Molecule

**Caption:** A transfer RNA (tRNA) has an amino acid binding site at one end and an anticodon at the other end. The tRNA acts as an adapter, matching amino acid to mRNA codon.

**Content:**
- A cloverleaf (2D) representation of tRNA showing:
  - The acceptor stem (at top) with the CCA sequence where the amino acid is attached.
  - The anticodon loop (at bottom) with the three-nucleotide anticodon.
  - The D loop and TψC loop (providing structure and stability).
  - An arrow showing the anticodon base-pairing with a mRNA codon.
  - The amino acid attached to the acceptor stem (shown as a ball with the amino acid name, e.g., "Ala").
  
- Alternatively, show the 3D L-shaped structure of tRNA:
  - The two ends are at opposite ends of the molecule.
  - The anticodon is at one end, the amino acid-binding site at the other.
  - The spatial separation allows the ribosome to hold both the codon (mRNA) and the amino acid at the right positions.

**Student should notice:**
- The tRNA is a bridge between the language of nucleotides and the language of amino acids.
- The anticodon is specific; each tRNA carries the correct amino acid for its anticodon.
- The L-shaped 3D structure is functional: the two ends are far apart, allowing the ribosome to read the codon and transfer the amino acid.

**Tied to:** Core Concept 3 (Translation).

## Summary of pedagogical purpose

Each figure has a learning objective:

1. **Central Dogma** — Understand the flow of information and why the system is designed with two steps.
2. **Transcription** — Understand how RNA polymerase reads the template strand and builds mRNA.
3. **Genetic Code** — Understand codon structure, degeneracy, and error protection.
4. **Translation** — Understand how the ribosome reads codons and builds proteins.
5. **Frameshift Mutation** — Understand why the triplet reading frame is critical.
6. **Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic** — Understand the trade-off between speed and regulation.
7. **tRNA** — Understand the adapter molecule and how codons are matched to amino acids.

## Implementation notes

- Figures should use consistent color coding (e.g., mRNA in one color, protein in another, DNA in a third).
- Arrows should show direction of synthesis/reading (5' to 3', 3' to 5').
- Labels should use the technical terms introduced in the chapter.
- Each figure should be self-contained but also reference the others (e.g., "Figure 1 shows the overall process; Figure 2 zooms in on transcription; Figure 4 zooms in on translation").

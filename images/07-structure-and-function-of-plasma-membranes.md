# Image Briefs: 07 The Plasma Membrane

## Figure 1: Fluid Mosaic Model — Cross-Section of the Plasma Membrane

**Purpose:** Show the student the actual architecture of the membrane: bilayer structure, embedded and peripheral proteins, carbohydrates on the exterior.

**What to show:**
- Phospholipid bilayer with hydrophilic heads (ball-shaped) facing outward (both sides).
- Hydrophobic fatty acid tails pointing inward, creating the core.
- Several integral proteins spanning the bilayer; some single-pass, one multi-pass.
- Peripheral proteins attached to the inner and outer surfaces.
- Carbohydrate chains (glycoproteins, glycolipids) extending from proteins and lipids on the outer surface only.
- Label each component and use colors to distinguish hydrophilic (e.g., blue) from hydrophobic (e.g., yellow/orange).

**What the student should notice:**
- The membrane is not a simple sheet; it is a complex assembly of many different molecule types.
- Proteins are embedded in the bilayer at various angles and depths.
- The outside is carbohydrate-rich (glycocalyx); the inside is not.
- The bilayer itself is thin (5–10 nm) but the overall membrane, including protein, can be thicker.

**Dimensions and style:** Standard textbook cross-section diagram. Labels with arrows. Color-code hydrophilic vs. hydrophobic. Show scale (nm).

## Figure 2: Diffusion Across the Membrane — Three Pathways

**Purpose:** Show the student the difference between simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and osmosis, all in one visual.

**What to show:**
- Panel A: Small uncharged molecule (O2) passing directly through the lipid bilayer via simple diffusion. Use arrows to show the movement; higher concentration on left, lower on right.
- Panel B: Glucose molecule (larger, charged) binding to a carrier protein. Show the carrier changing shape, moving the glucose across. Illustrate that glucose cannot cross alone; the carrier is essential.
- Panel C: Water molecules moving through an aquaporin channel (or across the bilayer directly). Show net flow from high water concentration (low solute) to low water concentration (high solute).
- Use distinct colors for each molecule type and for the different transport routes.

**What the student should notice:**
- Simple diffusion is fast but works only for small, uncharged molecules.
- Facilitated diffusion requires a specific protein and is selective.
- Water can move both through channels and directly through the bilayer.
- The membrane itself is the barrier; proteins are the gates.

**Dimensions and style:** Three side-by-side panels, each showing one molecule type and one transport method. Time arrows show direction of net movement.

## Figure 3: The Sodium-Potassium Pump — Seven-Step Cycle

**Purpose:** Show the student the mechanistic steps of the Na+/K+ ATPase, the most important active transport mechanism in animal cells.

**What to show:**
- A circular diagram with seven positions around the circle, each showing a step.
- **Step 1:** Pump is oriented inward; three Na+ ions bind from inside the cell. Label the high-affinity state.
- **Step 2:** ATP approaches; phosphate group attaches to the pump (phosphorylation). Show the energy input.
- **Step 3:** Pump changes conformation and reorients to face outward. Na+ ions dissociate on the outside. Show the shape change explicitly.
- **Step 4:** Two K+ ions bind from outside. Label the new, outward-facing conformation.
- **Step 5:** Phosphate detaches. Show the energy release.
- **Step 6:** Pump reorients inward. K+ ions dissociate inside the cell.
- **Step 7:** Pump returns to the original inward-facing state, ready to bind Na+ again.
- Use color to distinguish Na+ (one color, e.g., red), K+ (another color, e.g., blue), ATP/ADP/phosphate (distinct from both), and the pump protein (gray or neutral).

**What the student should notice:**
- The pump undergoes dramatic conformational changes.
- Energy from ATP hydrolysis drives the shape change.
- The asymmetry: 3 Na+ exit, only 2 K+ enter. This is not a mistake; it is the design.
- The process is cyclic; the pump resets after each round.

**Dimensions and style:** Circular layout, seven equal positions, arrows showing clockwise progression. Insets for close-ups of key steps (phosphorylation, ion release). Use consistent colors throughout to track ions.

## Figure 4: Electrochemical Gradients in a Resting Neuron (Optional Reference)

**Purpose:** Show the student the real concentration differences and electrical potential that the Na+/K+ pump maintains.

**What to show:**
- A neuron soma (cell body) with inside and outside compartments clearly separated.
- Label concentrations for sodium (12 mM inside, 145 mM outside), potassium (155 mM inside, 5 mM outside), chloride (typical values).
- Show the electrical gradient: −70 mV inside relative to outside.
- Use + and − symbols to indicate charge distribution.
- Draw arrows showing the tendencies: sodium wants to flow in (both concentration and electrical); potassium wants to flow out (concentration gradient) but is pulled in (electrical gradient); chloride equilibrates.

**What the student should notice:**
- The concentration gradients are real and dramatic (10–30 fold for ions).
- The electrical gradient (−70 mV) is large and significant.
- Different ions have different driving forces; the pump must work harder for some ions than others.

**Dimensions and style:** Simple cell diagram with concentrations labeled inside and out. Voltage bar showing the −70 mV. Arrows indicating driving forces.

## Implementation Notes

- **Figure 1** is essential; it is the core concept image and should appear early in Concept 1.
- **Figure 2** should appear early in Concept 2 to concretely show the three transport mechanisms side-by-side.
- **Figure 3** is the pedagogical centerpiece of Concept 3; it warrants a full, detailed diagram with step-by-step clarity.
- **Figure 4** is optional but helpful for students who want to understand real numbers and why gradients matter.
- All figures should use high contrast and clear labeling to remain readable at chapter-width in the PDF or online.
- Consider providing animated versions of Figures 2 and 3 for digital learning environments, with narration walking through each step.

---

## Style Guidelines

- Use consistent colors across all figures (e.g., always red for Na+, always blue for K+).
- Label every component on first appearance; define "hydrophilic," "hydrophobic," "integral protein," "peripheral protein," etc., in the figure captions.
- Include scale bars or dimension labels (nm, mV) to ground the student in real physical units.
- Avoid "cartoon" simplification that obscures actual mechanism; show the bilayer thickness, the protein complexity, the ion specificity.
- Arrows and directional cues should be clear; use bold lines for motion, dashed lines for potential flow.

# Images & Diagrams: Chapter 06 — Cell Structure

Descriptions and placement guides for figures referenced in the chapter. These are integration points where visual representation would anchor the written explanation.

## Opening Visual: Cork Tissue Under Hooke's Microscope

**Type:** Historical illustration or recreation

**Caption:** Hooke's 1665 drawing of cork cells. The rectangular compartments he saw gave the term "cell" to biology.

**Placement:** Top of chapter, after the opening narrative (after the second paragraph).

**Purpose:** Grounds the chapter in historical observation. Shows the simplicity of what Hooke saw and why the name stuck.

**Lesson:** Not all scientific breakthroughs are about complexity. Sometimes they are about noticing that there *is* a pattern.

## Figure 1: Scale Comparison — Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells

**Type:** Relative size diagram

**Elements:**
- Pinhead (2000 μm)
- Red blood cell (8 μm)
- Typical eukaryotic cell (10–100 μm)
- Typical prokaryotic cell (0.1–5 μm)
- Ribosome (20 nm)
- Protein molecule (5 nm)

**Visual approach:** Logarithmic scale, vertical line with labeled objects.

**Caption:** Size differences are not trivial. A prokaryotic cell is orders of magnitude smaller than a eukaryotic cell. At prokaryotic scale, diffusion is fast; at eukaryotic scale, it is slow. This geometry drives the need for compartmentalization.

**Placement:** After the section "What a Cell Actually Is," in the subsection on size.

**Purpose:** Convey the *quantitative* difference before diving into why it matters.

## Figure 2: Surface-Area-to-Volume Ratio

**Type:** Mathematical/geometric illustration

**Elements:**
- Three cubes of increasing size.
- For each: label surface area (r²), label volume (r³), calculate ratio.
- Example: small cube (1 μm): SA = 6 μm², V = 1 μm³, ratio = 6:1.
- Large cube (10 μm): SA = 600 μm², V = 1000 μm³, ratio = 0.6:1.

**Visual approach:** Simple geometry, numbers clear.

**Caption:** As a cell grows, its surface area-to-volume ratio declines. This is not opinion; it is mathematics. At some size, the membrane cannot move material fast enough to sustain the interior. The cell must either stay small, divide, or compartmentalize.

**Placement:** After "Why Does Nature Build in Two Fundamentally Different Ways?" section, before discussion of compartmentalization.

**Purpose:** Make the constraint *visible*. Students who see the numbers understand the constraint better than students who only hear it.

## Figure 3: Prokaryotic Cell Cross-Section

**Type:** Labeled diagram

**Elements:**
- Plasma membrane (lipid bilayer)
- Cytoplasm (gel-like)
- Nucleoid (DNA, not membrane-bound)
- Ribosomes (scattered)
- Cell wall (if bacteria: peptidoglycan)
- Flagella or pili (if present)

**Visual approach:** Simple, clean, no clutter.

**Caption:** A prokaryotic cell is a single room. DNA floats in the nucleoid. Ribosomes drift freely. All reactions occur in shared cytoplasm. This simplicity is also the limitation: the cell cannot grow large without starving its interior.

**Placement:** In section "The Two Designs: Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic," prokaryotic subsection.

**Purpose:** Establish the baseline. Show students what *simple* looks like, so the complexity of eukaryotic design becomes meaningful by contrast.

## Figure 4: Endosymbiotic Theory Timeline

**Type:** Schematic sequence diagram

**Panels:**
1. Early prokaryote (ancestor of eukaryotes).
2. Aerobic bacterium (future mitochondrion) approaching.
3. Ingestion event.
4. Internalized bacterium; still intact; still has own DNA.
5. Over millions of years: genetic transfer to host nucleus; loss of genes in organellar genome.
6. Modern eukaryotic cell with mitochondria.

**Visual approach:** Simplified, emphasis on the key transition points.

**Caption:** Endosymbiosis was not a discovery by observation; it was an inference from the structure of mitochondria and chloroplasts. Both retain DNA and ribosomes. Both have double membranes. Both are the size of bacteria. The evidence points to a single evolutionary event ~1.5 billion years ago.

**Placement:** In section "The Two Designs," after definition of eukaryotes, as the explanation for *why* eukaryotes are different.

**Purpose:** Answer the question: "Why do mitochondria have their own DNA?" Embed the answer in the historical narrative.

## Figure 5: Nuclear Envelope and Nuclear Pores

**Type:** Cross-sectional diagram

**Elements:**
- Double membrane (outer and inner)
- Pore complexes (protein channels)
- Nucleoplasm (inside): chromatin, nucleolus
- Cytoplasm (outside)
- Arrows showing RNA exiting, proteins entering

**Visual approach:** Clean cross-section; emphasize the pores as active gates, not passive holes.

**Caption:** The nuclear envelope is not a wall; it is a checkpoint. Pores actively transport molecules, allowing exit of RNA and entry of proteins while blocking unfolded proteins and large structures. This regulation allows the cell to separate DNA reading from protein synthesis.

**Placement:** In section "The Nuclear Envelope: The First Wall," as primary diagram.

**Purpose:** Show that compartmentalization is not isolation but *control*. The boundary is permeable but regulated.

## Figure 6: Endomembrane System Pathway

**Type:** Flow diagram with compartments

**Compartments:**
- Rough ER (with ribosomes shown as dots)
- Smooth ER (no ribosomes)
- Golgi apparatus (stack of sacs)
- Transport vesicles (small spheres with arrows)
- Lysosome
- Secretory vesicle fusing with plasma membrane

**Flow:**
- Protein synthesis in rough ER lumen.
- Vesicle budding to Golgi.
- Modification in Golgi.
- Vesicle budding to destination (membrane or secretion or lysosome).

**Caption:** The endomembrane system is an assembly line. Proteins are made in the rough ER, ferried to the Golgi for modification and addressing, and routed to their final destination. Lysosomes receive degraded materials for recycling. The system requires constant energy but enables precision.

**Placement:** In section "The Endomembrane System," as the organizing diagram for the entire subsection.

**Purpose:** Show the interconnectedness. Students must see that ER, Golgi, and lysosomes are not independent; they are one system.

## Figure 7: Mitochondrial Structure and Cristae

**Type:** 3D diagram with cross-section inset

**Elements:**
- Outer membrane (smooth)
- Inner membrane (highly folded into cristae)
- Matrix
- Cristae as folded inpocketings
- Inset showing electron transport chain proteins on cristae surface

**Visual approach:** Emphasize the folds. The cristae are the functional innovation.

**Caption:** Mitochondrial energy production happens on the inner membrane. The folds (cristae) increase surface area dramatically, providing more real estate for the electron transport chain. More surface = more ATP. Form follows function.

**Placement:** In section "The Powerhouse and the Solar Panel," in the mitochondria subsection.

**Purpose:** Show that mitochondrial efficiency is tied to a structural feature. Students should see that biology achieves high efficiency through geometry.

---

## Figure 8: Chloroplast Structure and Thylakoid Organization

**Type:** 3D diagram with inset of thylakoid stacking

**Elements:**
- Outer membrane
- Inner membrane
- Thylakoid membrane (shown as flattened sac)
- Grana (stacks of thylakoids)
- Stroma (space between grana)
- Chlorophyll molecules in thylakoid membrane (shown as light-absorbing pigments)

**Visual approach:** Emphasize the layering and the difference between thylakoid space and stroma.

**Caption:** Photosynthesis happens in two locations. Light reactions occur in and around the thylakoid membrane; the stacked grana increase light capture. The Calvin cycle occurs in the stroma. This separation allows the cell to run two different reactions sequentially but in the same organelle.

**Placement:** In section "The Powerhouse and the Solar Panel," in the chloroplast subsection.

**Purpose:** Show that chloroplasts are as precisely organized as mitochondria, optimized for light capture and energy conversion.

## Figure 9: Plant Cell vs. Animal Cell Side-by-Side

**Type:** Comparative diagram

**Left panel (plant cell):**
- Cell wall (outside)
- Plasma membrane
- Large central vacuole
- Nucleus
- Chloroplasts (shown as oval with grana)
- Mitochondria
- Small vesicles (not large vacuoles)
- Cytoskeleton

**Right panel (animal cell):**
- Plasma membrane (no cell wall)
- Small vesicles (no large central vacuole)
- Nucleus
- Mitochondria (no chloroplasts)
- Lysosomes
- Centrosome (with centrioles)
- Cytoskeleton

**Shared elements:** Color-coded to show what is the same.

**Differences:** Labeled and explained.

**Caption:** Plant and animal cells differ in three striking ways: plants have a cell wall and large central vacuole; animals do not. Plants have chloroplasts; animals do not (rare exceptions). These differences reflect different evolutionary strategies: plants are stationary and self-sufficient in energy; animals are mobile and dependent on external energy.

**Placement:** In section "Form Following Function: The Difference Between Animal and Plant Cells."

**Purpose:** Make the differences concrete and memorable. Students should see these diagrams and immediately connect structure to lifestyle.

## Figure 10: Cytoskeleton Three-Component System

**Type:** Comparative diagram

**Three rows:**

**Row 1: Microfilaments (actin)**
- Diameter: 7 nm
- Structure: two intertwined helical strands
- Color: red
- Function labels: cell movement, muscle contraction
- Example: white blood cell extending pseudopodium

**Row 2: Intermediate Filaments**
- Diameter: 8–10 nm
- Structure: fibrous proteins wound together
- Color: green
- Function labels: tension bearing, structural support, anchoring
- Example: keratin in hair and skin

**Row 3: Microtubules**
- Diameter: 25 nm
- Structure: hollow tube of tubulin dimers
- Color: blue
- Function labels: vesicle transport, cell division, cilia/flagella
- Example: vesicles moving along microtubule track

**Comparative scale:** All three shown to scale relative to each other.

**Caption:** The cytoskeleton has three components, each with different diameter and function. Microfilaments enable movement. Intermediate filaments provide structural support. Microtubules serve as tracks for transport and form the basis of cell division and cell movement. Together, they scaffold the cell and enable the dynamic processes that life requires.

**Placement:** In section "Scaling and Support: The Cytoskeleton," as the main organizing diagram.

**Purpose:** Show that the cytoskeleton is not one thing but three, each with its own job. This prevents students from treating it as a monolithic structure.

## Figure 11: Flagella and Cilia Structure

**Type:** Cross-sectional diagram

**Elements:**
- 9 + 2 array (9 outer microtubule doublets arranged in a ring, 1 central pair)
- Nexus links between adjacent doublets (dynein arms)
- Radial spokes connecting outer doublets to central pair
- Base (basal body)

**Inset 1:** Full length of flagellum (long, tapers)

**Inset 2:** Full length of cilium (short, multiple per cell)

**Caption:** Eukaryotic flagella and cilia share a common internal structure (9 + 2 array) but differ in length and number. Flagella move the entire cell; cilia move the cell or sweep fluid across the cell surface. Dynein arms cause adjacent doublets to slide past each other, creating the beating motion.

**Placement:** In section "Scaling and Support: The Cytoskeleton," subsection on flagella and cilia.

**Purpose:** Show that eukaryotic flagella/cilia are structurally and functionally different from prokaryotic flagella (which rotate). This is evidence of the eukaryotic origin from separate prokaryotic cells.

## Closing Visual: The Eukaryotic Cell as a City

**Type:** Annotated illustration (metaphorical)

**Elements:**
- Nucleus as "government center" (DNA as archives, RNA as policy documents)
- ER and Golgi as "factories and shipping warehouses"
- Mitochondria as "power plants"
- Lysosomes as "sanitation departments"
- Plasma membrane as "border"
- Vesicles as "delivery trucks"
- Cytoskeleton as "roads and infrastructure"

**Caption:** A eukaryotic cell is like a city: compartmentalized, specialized, interconnected, and organized. Each organelle has a function. Each region has distinct chemistry. Communication happens through defined channels. Understanding a cell is understanding a system in miniature.

**Placement:** End of chapter summary, before "Connections Forward."

**Purpose:** Provide a visual metaphor that students can carry away. The city analogy is imperfect but useful for making compartmentalization intuitive.

## Figure Rationale

All figures are designed to serve the narrative. None are decorative. Each figure answers a specific question or illustrates a specific concept that words alone leave ambiguous:

- Scale comparisons (Figures 1, 2) make the geometry *visible*.
- Pathway diagrams (Figures 6, 10) show the interconnectedness of the system.
- Comparative diagrams (Figure 9) highlight differences that explain biological diversity.
- Historical figures (Hooke) ground modern understanding in the history of observation.

The goal is not to replace words with pictures but to use pictures to do what pictures do best: show spatial relationships, relative sizes, and dynamic processes that words must describe in sequence.

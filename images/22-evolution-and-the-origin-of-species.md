### FIGURE 3: Polyploidy Speciation Mechanism
**Location:** Section 3 (Sympatric Speciation), subsection Polyploidy
**Type:** Flow diagram, chromosome tracking
**Content:**
- Row 1: Normal diploid parent (2n = 6, shown as 6 chromosomes colored by pair)
- Row 2: Meiosis error → polyploid gamete (2n instead of n)
- Row 3: Polyploid gamete fuses with normal gamete → triploid offspring (3n)
- Row 4: Triploid attempted meiosis (chromosomes can't pair evenly; meiosis stalls; sterile)
- Row 5: Two polyploid gametes fuse → tetraploid offspring (4n)
- Row 6: Tetraploid meiosis (chromosomes pair as two bivalents; meiosis completes; fertile)
- Row 7: Tetraploid × diploid → triploid (sterile; reproductive isolation)
- Caption: "Polyploidy produces instant reproductive isolation. Triploid offspring are sterile (odd number of chromosome sets cannot pair evenly during meiosis). Tetraploid offspring are fertile with other tetraploids but cannot produce fertile offspring with the original diploid population."

- Arrow: "Boundary: Fertilization"
- Caption: "Reproductive isolating mechanisms prevent gene flow. Prezygotic barriers prevent fertilization; postzygotic barriers reduce hybrid fitness or fertility."

### FIGURE 5: Hybrid Zone Outcomes
**Location:** Section 5 (Hybrid Zones)
**Type:** Three parallel diagrams, time-series
**Content:**
- Three panels (left to right):

  **Panel A: Reinforcement**
  - Time 0: Two species at boundary, hybrids forming
  - Time 1: Hybrids less fit (health/fertility symbols diminished)
  - Time 2: Prezygotic barriers strengthen (mating calls diverge more; behavioral/visual isolation increases)
  - Result: Species separate further; hybrid zone narrows

  **Panel B: Fusion**
  - Time 0: Two species at boundary, hybrids forming
  - Time 1: Hybrids as fit as purebreds (equal fitness symbols)
  - Time 2: Gene flow continues; two species interbreed freely
  - Result: One species; hybrid zone disappears (species merge)

  **Panel C: Stability**
  - Time 0: Two species at boundary, hybrids forming
  - Time 1: Hybrid fitness intermediate (moderate symbols)
  - Time 2: Hybrid zone persists indefinitely; two species coexist
  - Result: Boundary remains; species maintain distinct identities with gene flow at edge

- Caption: "Hybrid zone outcomes depend on hybrid fitness. Low fitness drives reinforcement (strengthened isolation). High fitness drives fusion (species merge). Intermediate fitness allows stability (coexistence with ongoing hybridization)."

### FIGURE 6: Allopatric Speciation via Geographic Isolation
**Location:** Section 3, subsection "How Separation Happens"
**Type:** Sequential map/diagram
**Content:**
- Panel 1 (ancestral): Single population of spotted owl across continuous forest (continuous green)
- Panel 2 (geography changes): Mountains rise or climate shifts; forests fragment into north and south regions
- Panel 3 (isolation): Geographic barrier (mountains, unsuitable habitat) blocks gene flow
- Panel 4 (divergence): North population evolves (cooler climate traits); south population evolves (warmer climate traits; genetic changes indicated by color shift)
- Panel 5 (present day): Two species, distinct morphologies; if they meet, reproductive barriers prevent mating
- Arrows show: Gene flow → separation → independent evolution → divergence
- Caption: "Allopatric speciation. Geographic separation stops gene flow. Each population evolves independently, accumulating different mutations and adaptations. After sufficient divergence, reproductive barriers evolve, creating separate species even if populations reunite."

### FIGURE 7: Sympatric Speciation via Ecological Specialization (Cichlid Fish Example)
**Location:** Section 3, subsection "Sympatric Speciation Via Ecological Specialization"
**Type:** Population diagram with resource use overlay
**Content:**
- Initial state: Single cichlid population in lake, color variation present
- Resource availability diagram: Bottom (algae), middle (small fish), surface (insects at different depths)
- Time progression:
  - Generation 1: Genetic variation for color and feeding preference present
  - Generation 2-10: Some fish specialize on algae (red coloration); others on insects (blue coloration); assortative mating begins (red with red, blue with blue)
  - Generation 20+: Reproductive barriers evolve; two species now occupy same lake but different ecological niches
- Females shown with mating preference icons (color-based mate choice)
- Caption: "Sympatric speciation via ecological specialization. Assortative mating (mating with similar individuals) reduces gene flow. Divergent selection on different food sources drives genetic divergence. Reproductive barriers (here, visual/behavioral isolation via color preference) evolve without geographic separation."

### FIGURE 8: Speciation Rates—Gradualism vs. Punctuated Equilibrium
**Location:** Section 6 (Rates of Speciation)
**Type:** Two side-by-side graphs
**Content:**
- **Left graph (Gradualism):**
  - X-axis: Time
  - Y-axis: Morphological trait (e.g., size)
  - Single lineage shown as smooth, continuous curve from ancestor to descendant
  - Fossil record (points along curve) shows many intermediate forms
  - Caption: "Gradual change over time. Small steps accumulate to large change. Fossil record should show many intermediates."

- **Right graph (Punctuated Equilibrium):**
  - X-axis: Time
  - Y-axis: Morphological trait
  - Lineage shown as horizontal line (stasis) → sharp vertical step (rapid change) → horizontal line (stasis)
  - Fossil record (points) shows stasis periods, then gaps, then new form
  - Caption: "Rapid change in small, isolated populations; then long stasis. Fossil record shows steps, not smooth curve."

- Overall caption: "Two models of speciation rate. Gradualism: slow, steady change. Punctuated equilibrium: rapid change in small populations, then stasis. Evidence supports both mechanisms in different contexts."

## Image Creation Notes

- **All figures should be publication-quality (300 dpi if printed).**
- **Use consistent color schemes** (e.g., red for larger/faster evolution, blue for slower; warm colors for prezygotic, cool for postzygotic).
- **Label axes clearly** with units and scales.
- **Include legends** where appropriate.
- **Ensure accessibility:** Figures should be legible to someone with color blindness (consider colorblind-friendly palettes).

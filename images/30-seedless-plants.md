# Images and Figures: Seedless Plants (Ch 30)

## In-Chapter Figures

All figures marked `[FIGURE: ...]` in the chapter text below.

### Figure 1: The Carboniferous Landscape (Ch Opening)
- **Placement**: After opening paragraph, as narrative context.
- **Content**: Artistic reconstruction of Carboniferous swamp forest showing scale: giant club mosses (lycophytes, 20–40 meters), tree ferns (monilophytes), horsetails with jointed stems. Relative size comparison to modern trees. Murky water, thick vegetation, fossil deposits visible in lower layers.
- **Purpose**: Visual anchor for "300 million years ago"; establishes that seedless plants can be large and successful (not just modern mosses).
- **Source/Note**: Standard paleobotany illustration. See *Smithsonian Paleobiology of Plants* or equivalent.

### Figure 2: Cuticle and Stoma Cross-Section
- **Placement**: During "Cuticle and Stomata" section.
- **Content**: Magnified cross-section of leaf showing: outer cuticle (waxy layer, hydrophobic, appears as thin shiny band), beneath it one to several layers of epidermal cells, then spongy mesophyll cells. Stoma shown in open and closed position side-by-side: guard cells swollen (open stoma, gas can enter) vs. flaccid (closed stoma, water conserved). Arrows showing CO₂ entering, water vapor escaping.
- **Purpose**: Makes visible how cuticle prevents drying but blocks gas; shows mechanical action of stomata (turgor pressure in guard cells controls opening).
- **Label key**: cuticle, epidermis, guard cells, stoma (pore), mesophyll.

### Figure 3: Xylem and Phloem Anatomy
- **Placement**: During "Vascular Tissue" section.
- **Content**: Cross-section of stem or root showing:
  - Xylem: hollow, dead cells (no nucleus visible), arranged in radiating pattern from center, cell walls heavily thickened and reinforced (show with darker shading to indicate lignin).
  - Phloem: living cells (nucleus visible), sieve tubes (cells with pores connecting adjacent cells via plasmodesmata), companion cells alongside sieve tubes.
  - Arrows showing water/minerals moving upward (xylem) and sugars moving downward (phloem).
- **Purpose**: Clarifies difference in anatomy and function. Lignin shading shows why xylem is structural; living phloem shows why it requires energy input.
- **Label key**: xylem, phloem, tracheid, sieve tube, companion cell, plasmodesmata, lignin-reinforced wall.

### Figure 4: Alternation of Generations (General Pattern)
- **Placement**: During "Alternation of Generations" section, before the deep explanation.
- **Content**: Circular or cyclical diagram showing two multicellular forms:
  - Top: Gametophyte (n, haploid), shown as distinct multicellular organism, with gametangia (male and female structures indicated).
  - Mitosis arrow from gametophyte to gametes (sperm with flagella shown, egg inside archegonium).
  - Fertilization arrow showing fusion of sperm and egg to form zygote.
  - Zygote grows by mitosis into Sporophyte (2n, diploid), shown as second distinct multicellular organism.
  - Meiosis arrow from sporophyte to spores (haploid).
  - Spore grows by mitosis into new gametophyte (cycle completes).
- **Purpose**: Shows the full cycle visually; makes clear that mitosis builds bodies (gametophyte and sporophyte), while meiosis reduces ploidy (in sporophyte) and mitosis restores it (in gametophyte and during gametangia).
- **Label key**: gametophyte, sporophyte, mitosis, meiosis, spore, gamete, fertilization, n, 2n.

### Figure 5: Moss Life Cycle (Detailed Example)
- **Placement**: In or near "Four Groups, One Pattern" section, as concrete instantiation of alternation of generations.
- **Content**: Life cycle of moss showing:
  - Left side: Green cushion (gametophyte) with stalks (sporophytes) growing out of it. Sporangia (spore capsules) at tips of stalks.
  - Spores released by meiosis from sporophyte.
  - Spore grows into green gametophyte (protonema → leafy gametophyte).
  - Gametangia (antheridia producing sperm with flagella, archegonia producing eggs) on gametophyte.
  - Sperm swim through water film to fertilize egg in archegonium.
  - Fertilized egg (zygote) develops into new sporophyte, which grows out of gametophyte body.
  - Cycle completes.
- **Purpose**: Shows the dominance of gametophyte in mosses; makes visible the water-dependence of sperm swimming.
- **Label key**: gametophyte (n), sporophyte (2n), spore (n), antheridium, archegonium, sperm, egg, water film.

### Figure 6: Fern Life Cycle (Detailed Example)
- **Placement**: In "Four Groups, One Pattern" section, as contrast to moss (sporophyte is dominant here).
- **Content**: Life cycle of fern showing:
  - Left/top: Large fern plant (sporophyte, the familiar frond-bearing plant). Underside of frond shows sori (clusters of sporangia).
  - Meiosis in sporangia produces haploid spores.
  - Spore lands on moist soil and grows by mitosis into gametophyte (tiny heart-shaped, 5–10 mm, usually green, thin).
  - Gametophyte is hard to see; include size scale (next to fingernail or coin for size comparison).
  - Gametangia on underside of gametophyte: antheridia producing flagellated sperm, archegonia holding eggs.
  - Sperm swim through water film (droplet, moist soil) to fertilize egg.
  - Zygote develops into new sporophyte fern, which can grow very large (meters tall).
- **Purpose**: Shows the switch to sporophyte dominance in vascular plants; emphasizes that gametophyte remains present but tiny.
- **Label key**: sporophyte (2n), sorus, sporangium, spore (n), gametophyte (n), antheridium, archegonium, sperm, egg, zygote.

### Figure 7: Sporopollenin Wall Structure
- **Placement**: During "Spores with Armor" section.
- **Content**: Magnified (electron microscopy style) cross-section of spore showing:
  - Inner cell cytoplasm and nucleus.
  - Spore wall (sporopollenin layer) as thick, textured, yellow-orange band.
  - Texture shown as interwoven chains (representing fatty acid and carotenoid polymers).
  - Arrows showing what the wall resists: UV light, desiccation, chemical degradation, microbial attack.
  - Comparison image: fossilized pollen grain from ancient deposit, showing same wall structure intact after millions of years.
- **Purpose**: Makes visible why sporopollenin is so important for dispersal; shows chemical toughness in a way that explains fossil preservation.
- **Label key**: sporopollenin, exine (if using technical term; explain in caption), cell wall, UV resistance.

### Figure 8: Antheridium and Archegonium (Gametangia)
- **Placement**: During "Water Dependence" section.
- **Content**: Two side-by-side magnified views of gametophyte structures:
  - Left: Antheridium (male gametangium), showing chamber filled with sperm cells, flagellated sperm being released into surrounding water.
  - Right: Archegonium (female gametangium), showing flask-shaped structure, egg cell in base, neck canal cells at opening.
  - Between them: Arrow showing sperm swimming through water, chemically attracted down neck of archegonium toward egg.
  - Alternative panel showing: antheridium and archegonium on same gametophyte (showing that gametophyte can be hermaphroditic).
- **Purpose**: Makes the water-dependence problem concrete and visual; shows the anatomy that makes swimming sperm necessary.
- **Label key**: antheridium, archegonium, sperm, egg, neck canal, flagella, water.

### Figure 9: Plant Adaptations Timeline
- **Placement**: At or near "Evolution of Land Plants" section.
- **Content**: Horizontal timeline from Ordovician (500 mya) to Devonian (400 mya) showing:
  - Ordovician: Simple land plants, appearance of cuticle fossils.
  - Silurian: Early vascular plants (*Cooksonia*), still small.
  - Devonian: Ferns, horsetails, club mosses, first seed plants.
  - For each era, show representative plant silhouettes (small in Ordovician/Silurian, increasingly large and diverse in Devonian).
  - Mark on timeline: emergence of cuticle, vascular tissue, sporangia, seeds.
  - Geological column on left showing periods.
- **Purpose**: Contextualizes the innovations within evolutionary time; shows that vascular tissue came later than cuticle.
- **Label key**: Ordovician, Silurian, Devonian, cuticle, vascular tissue, spore, seed.

### Figure 10: Bryophyte vs. Vascular Plant Comparison
- **Placement**: In "Four Groups, One Pattern" section, as visual summary of structural differences.
- **Content**: Side-by-side anatomical comparison:
  - Left: Moss (bryophyte) - small height (few cm), shown with cross-section revealing uniform cells throughout, no vascular bundles, thin outer cells.
  - Right: Fern (vascular plant) - large height (30 cm to meters), shown with cross-section revealing vascular bundles (xylem and phloem) distributed through stem, thicker outer cells, clear tissues zones.
  - Size scale for each.
  - Arrows pointing out absence vs. presence of xylem, phloem, lignin.
- **Purpose**: Visually explains why vascular tissue allows height. Makes clear that "seedless plants" is not a size category—it includes both tiny mosses and large ferns.
- **Label key**: bryophyte, vascular plant, xylem, phloem, cuticle, epidermis.

## Figure Technical Notes

- **Style**: Illustrations should match the biology-bundle house style (clean line drawings, muted color palette, clarity-first labeling).
- **Scale**: All magnified structures should include scale bars or size comparisons (e.g., "comparable to a fingernail").
- **Arrows**: Use arrows to show movement (water in xylem, sugars in phloem, sperm swimming, meiosis/mitosis transitions).
- **Color coding (optional)**: 
  - Haploid (n) structures in one color family (e.g., green).
  - Diploid (2n) structures in another (e.g., brown/red).
  - Water-dependent processes in blue.
- **Accessibility**: All figures must be clear in grayscale; patterns/textures for distinction, not color alone.

## Suggested Multimedia / Interactive Elements

- **Interactive Alternation of Generations**: Clickable diagram where students can toggle between gametophyte and sporophyte, see ploidy labels, and follow the path of meiosis/mitosis/fertilization.
- **Stoma Animation**: Show guard cells swelling and shrinking as water enters/leaves, opening and closing the stoma; link to environmental triggers (humidity, light, CO₂ concentration).
- **Fern Life Cycle Simulation**: Students "plant" a spore, watch it grow into a gametophyte, apply water to allow fertilization, and watch the sporophyte develop. Introduces life cycle timing and environmental dependence.

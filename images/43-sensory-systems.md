## Figures Referenced in Chapter

### Figure 1: Mechanoreceptor Transduction
**Location in text:** Section Two, subsection "The Receptor Potential: Making Stimuli into Signals"

**What it shows:** 
- Cross-section of mechanoreceptor in skin (detail of single receptor cell)
- Resting state: ion channels closed, normal membrane potential
- Stimulated state: pressure bends dendrites → mechanically gated ion channels open → Na+ flows in → membrane depolarizes
- Scale: zoomed into single synapse/receptor area

**Key elements to label:**
- Dermis/hypodermis boundary
- Receptor cell membrane
- Mechanically gated ion channels (in resting and open states)
- Sodium ions (Na+)
- Direction of deformation force
- Receptor potential (voltage measurement)

**Teaching point:** This is where transduction becomes visible. A physical force (pressure) becomes an electrical signal (depolarization) through ion channel opening.

### Figure 2: Skin Layers and Receptor Distribution
**Location in text:** Section Two, subsection "The Architecture of Somatosensation"

**What it shows:**
- Vertical cross-section of skin from surface to underlying tissue
- Three layers: epidermis (thin), dermis (thick), hypodermis (fatty)
- Merkel's disks and Meissner's corpuscles near the surface
- Ruffini endings and Pacinian corpuscles deeper
- Hair follicles with hair receptors
- Blood vessels, sweat glands

**Key elements to label:**
- Epidermis (location: outermost)
- Dermis (location: middle)
- Hypodermis/subcutaneous tissue (location: deepest)
- Merkel's disks (near epidermis base, slow-adapting, disc-like)
- Meissner's corpuscles (upper dermis, rapidly adapting, oval)
- Ruffini endings (deep dermis, slow-adapting, spindle-like)
- Pacinian corpuscles (deepest, rapidly adapting, onion-shaped)
- Hair follicle with receptor wrapping around base
- Direction of stimulus: pressure from above

**Teaching point:** Receptor type and depth are not random. Rapid adapters are where they sense change; slow adapters sense sustained stimuli. Depth determines what kind of stimulation reaches them.

### Figure 3: Sound Wave Properties
**Location in text:** Section Four, subsection "The Physics of Sound"

**What it shows:**
- Two sine waves side by side
- Left wave: low frequency (long wavelength, low pitch)
- Right wave: high frequency (short wavelength, high pitch)
- Below left wave: amplitude marked (vertical distance from center to peak)
- Label showing frequency (cycles per second, Hz) and amplitude (decibels, dB)

**Key elements to label:**
- Wavelength (distance between peaks)
- Frequency (number of cycles per unit time)
- Amplitude (height of wave)
- Pitch (heard as frequency)
- Volume (heard as amplitude)

**Teaching point:** Sound has two independent dimensions. Frequency and amplitude are orthogonal; you can have low-frequency loud sound and high-frequency quiet sound.

**Key elements to label:**

**Teaching point:** The cochlea is a mechanical frequency analyzer. Different frequencies maximally stimulate different locations, creating a "pitch map."

### Figure 7: Rods and Cones (Photoreceptors)
**Location in text:** Section Five, subsection "Photoreceptors: Rods and Cones"

**What it shows:**
- Rods and cones side-by-side, showing shape difference
- Rod: long, cylindrical, narrow
- Cone: shorter, wider, tapered
- Both showing outer segment (where photopigment lives) and inner segment (cell body)
- Distribution in retina: rods at periphery (outer regions), cones in center (fovea)
- Comparative density map showing cone density highest at fovea, rod density highest at periphery

**Key elements to label:**
- Rod (grayscale, photosensitive, peripheral vision)
- Cone (color vision, bright light, central vision)
- Outer segment (contains photopigment, site of transduction)
- Inner segment (cell body, nucleus)
- Synapse at base (connects to bipolar cells)
- Fovea (high cone density region)
- Periphery (high rod density region)

**Teaching point:** Shape is functional. Long rods have more photopigment; short cones allow higher acuity. Distribution (center vs. periphery) reflects their different roles.

### Figure 8: Rhodopsin Isomerization
**Location in text:** Section Five, subsection "The Mechanism of Light Detection"

**What it shows:**
- Rhodopsin molecule in photoreceptor membrane
- Two conformations: resting (opsin + retinal in bent/cis form) and excited (opsin + retinal in straight/trans form)
- Photon hitting retinal
- Subsequent conformational change
- Coupling to G-protein (transducin) shown below

**Key elements to label:**
- Opsin protein (membrane-spanning helical bundle)
- Retinal ligand (small molecule, bound to opsin)
- Cis form (bent, resting state)
- Trans form (straight, light-activated)
- Photon arrow (energy input)
- Conformational change in opsin
- Intracellular side showing G-protein coupling

**Teaching point:** Photons do not break bonds. They cause an isomerization—a change in shape that is quickly reversed. This reversibility is why vision can happen so fast and why rod is so sensitive.

### Figure 9: Three Cone Types (Color Vision)
**Location in text:** Section Five, subsection "Color Vision: The Three Cone Types"

**What it shows:**
- Graph showing three cone sensitivity curves
- X-axis: wavelength (380–740 nm)
- Y-axis: sensitivity (relative)
- Three curves: S-cone (peak ~420 nm, blue), M-cone (peak ~530 nm, green), L-cone (peak ~560 nm, red)
- Overlay showing how different wavelengths excite different ratios of cone types

**Key elements to label:**
- S-cone curve (short wavelength peak)
- M-cone curve (medium wavelength peak)
- L-cone curve (long wavelength peak)
- Wavelength scale (nm)
- Sensitivity scale (relative)
- Example: red light excites L-cone strongly, M-cone weakly, S-cone not at all
- Example: green light excites M-cone strongly

**Teaching point:** Color is not intrinsic to wavelength. It is the ratio of S, M, L cone activation. The same wavelength can appear different colors in different contexts (color constancy).

### Figure 10: Retinal Circuit (Simplified)
**Location in text:** Section Five, subsection "Retinal Processing and Opponent Colors"

**What it shows:**
- Photoreceptor layer (rods/cones)
- Horizontal cells (laterally connecting)
- Bipolar cells (vertically integrating)
- Amacrine cells (laterally connecting)
- Ganglion cells (output to optic nerve)
- Synaptic connections between layers
- Lateral inhibition pathway (rod → horizontal cell → nearby rod, inhibitory)

**Key elements to label:**
- Photoreceptor (rod or cone)
- Horizontal cell (carries signal laterally, creates lateral inhibition)
- Bipolar cell (integrates photoreceptor and horizontal cell inputs)
- Amacrine cell (carries signal laterally among bipolar/ganglion cells)
- Ganglion cell (output neuron, axons form optic nerve)
- Excitatory synapse (triangle or +)
- Inhibitory synapse (circle or -)

**Teaching point:** The retina is not a simple transmission line. It is a computation device. Lateral inhibition sharpens edges before information even leaves the eye.

## Data Figures and Graphs

### Graph 1: Just-Noticeable Difference (Weber's Law)
**Location in text:** Section One, subsection "Intensity and the Just-Noticeable Difference"

**What it shows:**
- X-axis: original stimulus magnitude (1 lb, 10 lb, 20 lb, 100 lb)
- Y-axis: just-noticeable difference (absolute value in pounds)
- Plot showing linear relationship (JND increases proportionally with stimulus)
- Overlay showing JND as percentage of original (constant ~20%)

**Teaching point:** The relationship is not linear in absolute terms (JND = k × stimulus) which produces a linear relationship on a log-log plot.

---

### Graph 2: Auditory Sensitivity Across Frequencies
**Location in text:** Section Four, subsection "The Physics of Sound"

**What it shows:**
- X-axis: frequency (Hz, log scale, 20–20,000 Hz)
- Y-axis: sound intensity (dB SPL, sound pressure level)
- Curve showing human hearing threshold (quietest sound audible at each frequency)
- Shaded region: human hearing range
- Annotations: "30 Hz" (lowest audible), "20,000 Hz" (highest for young humans), frequencies for other animals (40,000 Hz dogs, 150,000 Hz dolphins)

**Teaching point:** Human hearing is not equally sensitive across frequencies. We are most sensitive in the speech range (1–5 kHz) and less sensitive at very low and very high frequencies.

---

### Graph 3: Cone Density Across Retina
**Location in text:** Section Five, subsection "Photoreceptors: Rods and Cones"

**What it shows:**
- X-axis: distance from fovea (degrees of visual angle, 0–80°)
- Y-axis: receptor density (receptors per mm²)
- Two curves: cone density (high at center, drops rapidly) and rod density (low at center, increases toward periphery)
- Annotation: fovea (0°), blind spot (~15°), peripheral vision (60°+)

**Teaching point:** The retina is not uniformly distributed. Cones dominate the center; rods dominate the periphery. This reflects functional specialization.

## Diagrams and Schematics

### Schematic 1: The Three-Step Sensory Process
**Location in text:** Chapter opening, "How Sensation Works: Three Steps"

**What it shows:**
- Box 1: Stimulus (photon, pressure wave, molecule, heat)
- Arrow: "Reception"
- Box 2: Sensory receptor (activated, membrane deformation/conformational change)
- Arrow: "Transduction"

**Teaching point:** This is the universal architecture. Every sense follows these steps.

### Schematic 2: Tonotopic Organization (Cochlear Map)
**Location in text:** Section Four, subsection "Frequency Analysis: How the Cochlea Works"

**What it shows:**
- Unrolled cochlea with oval window on left, apex on right
- Frequency scale from high (left) to low (right)
- Example: 4 kHz region marked, 1 kHz region marked, 0.1 kHz region marked
- Hair cells at each region responding maximally to that frequency
- Auditory cortex (tonotopic map) showing same frequency organization reflected in brain

**Teaching point:** The nervous system preserves the spatial organization of the cochlea in the auditory cortex. This is called tonotopic organization. Where a neuron is in auditory cortex tells you what frequency it codes for.

---

### Schematic 3: Visual Pathways in Brain
**Location in text:** Section Five, subsection "Higher Processing"

**What it shows:**
- Retina and optic nerve
- Optic chiasma (crossing of nasal fibers)
- Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) of thalamus
- Primary visual cortex (V1)
- Two pathways diverging from V1:
  - Magnocellular pathway → parietal cortex ("where")
  - Parvocellular pathway → temporal cortex ("what")
- Inferior pathway to superior colliculus (eye movement coordination)
- Pathway to suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) (circadian rhythm)

**Teaching point:** Vision is not a single stream. Multiple parallel pathways process different features (motion, color, location) simultaneously.

## Optional Advanced Figures (For Deeper Learning)

### Figure A1: G-Protein Cascade in Phototransduction
**For students wanting mechanism detail:**
- Photon → retinal isomerization → opsin conformational change → activation of transducin (G-protein) → activation of phosphodiesterase → hydrolysis of cGMP → closing of cGMP-gated ion channels → hyperpolarization

**Teaching point:** Transduction uses a signaling cascade to amplify the signal. One photon can lead to hydrolysis of many cGMP molecules.

---

### Figure A2: Hair Cell Mechanoelectric Transduction
**For students wanting mechanism detail:**
- Stereocilia movement → opening of mechanically gated ion channels → Ca2+ entry → depolarization → neurotransmitter release → activation of auditory nerve

**Teaching point:** Hair cells in both cochlea and vestibular system use similar mechanotransduction but respond to different movement patterns.

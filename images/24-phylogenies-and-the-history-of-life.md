# Image Briefs — Chapter 24: Phylogenies and the History of Life

## Figure 1: Bat Wing, Bird Wing, Insect Wing — Homology and Analogy

**Purpose:** Illustrate the difference between homologous structures (bat wing and human arm/bird wing—shared bone structure) and analogous structures (bat/bird wings vs. insect wing—different structure, same function).

**What to show:**
- Anatomical cross-section or diagram of three wing types
- Bat wing: highlight the recognizable mammalian hand (arm bone, two forearm bones, five digits stretched with webbing)
- Bird wing: highlight the modified limb structure (arm bone, fused wrist bones, feather-anchoring bones)
- Insect wing: highlight the chitinous exoskeletal extension (no bones, just cuticle and veins)
- Color-code or label to show which structures are homologous (bat arm = human arm = bird wing bones) and which are analogous (all three wings function for flight)

**Student learning target:** 
"See that bat and human arm bones are recognizably the same, even though the bat's are stretched and webbed? That is homology—shared structure from descent. Now look at the insect wing—it has no bones at all. Both solve flight, but the solutions are completely different. That is analogy—function converged, structure did not."

**Placement:** Concept 1 (Homology vs. Analogy)

## Figure 2: Phylogenetic Tree of Four Vertebrates

**Purpose:** Show a simple cladogram with labeled nodes and branch points, illustrating how to read relationships from a tree structure.

**What to show:**
- Simple tree with four modern species at the tips: salmon, lizard, hawk, dog
- Labeled nodes showing where lineages split
- At the base of the amniote clade (where lizard, hawk, and dog branch off from salmon), label the shared derived characters (four limbs, amniotic egg)
- At the bird branch point, label the derived character (feathers)
- At the mammal branch point, label the derived character (hair)
- Include a "root" label at the base (common ancestor of all vertebrates)

**Alternative or additional panels:**
- Show the same tree with the question: "Are birds or crocodiles more closely related to humans?" (Answer: birds, because the bird-mammal-crocodile-all-reptiles group shares amniotic eggs and four limbs, so birds + mammals are closer to each other within amniotes)
- Show rotation at branch points (same tree, but branches rotated) to illustrate that rotation does not change the information

**Student learning target:**
"The tree shows branching order. Derived characters mark the splits. The further back you trace, the more ancient the common ancestor. Lizards and hawks share a more recent ancestor with the dog than any of them share with the salmon."

**Placement:** Concept 2 (Building Trees)

## Figure 3: Archaeopteryx, Modern Bird, Crocodile — Anatomy Comparison

**Purpose:** Show the transitional anatomy that reveals Archaeopteryx's true kinship (bird, not reptile) through shared derived characters.

**What to show:**
- Skeletal or anatomical illustration of Archaeopteryx (fossil reconstruction)
- Modern bird skeleton (for comparison)
- Modern crocodile skeleton (for comparison)
- Highlight shared derived characters between Archaeopteryx and bird:
  - Feathers (or feather impressions in fossil)
  - Wishbone (furcula)
  - Skeletal anatomy of wings
- Contrast with crocodile, which lacks these features
- Option: show teeth in Archaeopteryx and crocodile but not in modern birds (ancestral character, does not distinguish groups)

**Alternative representation:**
- Side-by-side or overlaid wing structures (Archaeopteryx vs. modern bird), highlighting bone homology
- Fossil image of Archaeopteryx with annotations

**Student learning target:**
"Archaeopteryx looks reptilian—teeth, bony tail, claws. But shared derived characters tell the truth: feathers and wishbone connect it to birds, not crocodiles. Appearance lies; structure tells history."

**Placement:** Concept 2 (Building Trees) / Worked Example

## Figure 4: Dolphin Flipper vs. Tuna Fin — Bone Structure

**Purpose:** Illustrate homology in action: show that dolphin flippers, despite solving the same problem as tuna fins, retain the mammalian hand structure.

**What to show:**
- Dorsal view or skeletal diagram of dolphin flipper
- Label the arm bone (humerus), forearm bones (radius, ulna), wrist bones (carpals), digits (phalanges)
- Highlight the five-digit structure
- Show the same bones in a human hand for comparison (optional side panel)
- Dorsal view of tuna fin
- Label the fin rays (cartilaginous or bony rays radiating from the base)
- Contrast the structure: no arm bone, no discrete digits, no homology to limbs
- Include hydrodynamic overlays (streamlining, fin shape) to show that function converged even though structure remained different

**Student learning target:**
"Dolphin and tuna both evolved for efficient swimming, but dolphin inherited a mammalian hand and repurposed it. The bone structure proves kinship with humans; the function shows adaptation to the ocean."

**Placement:** Concept 1 / Worked Example

## Figure 5: Three-Domain Tree (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya)

**Purpose:** Show the deep structure of life, with the three domains diverging from a common ancestor, and the place of Eukaryota further subdivided into major groups.

**What to show:**
- Simple branching tree with a single root (last universal common ancestor, LUCA)
- Three major branches: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya
- Eukarya further subdivided into: Protists, Fungi, Plants, Animals
- Option: within Animals, show further subdivisions (Sponges, Cnidarians, Worms, Arthropods, Mollusks, Chordates)
- Each branch point could be labeled with the shared derived character (e.g., "Unique rRNA sequences diagnose each domain")

**Variation or addition:**
- Show the same three-domain tree alongside a web-of-life diagram (horizontal lines crossing between domains) to illustrate HGT
- Annotate the web version with "Genes transfer between bacteria, archaea, and early eukaryotes"

**Student learning target:**
"Life branches into three deep divisions revealed by ribosomal RNA. Within Eukarya, we see the familiar kingdoms. But for prokaryotes, the tree gets tangled by horizontal gene transfer."

**Placement:** Concept 3 (Synthesis)

## Figure 6: Molecular Clock Calibration

**Purpose:** Show how mutation rate is calibrated against fossil evidence and then used to estimate divergence times.

**What to show:**
- A pair of modern organisms (e.g., human and chimpanzee) with their DNA sequence difference noted (e.g., ~1% of nuclear DNA)
- A fossil record showing the estimated divergence time (e.g., 6-7 million years ago)
- Calculation: (1% difference) / (2 × 6.5 million years) ≈ 0.077% change per million years
- Show this rate applied to other organism pairs: if species X and Y differ at 2%, their divergence time is roughly 13 million years ago

**Alternative representation:**
- A timeline with organisms placed at estimated divergence points based on molecular clock calculations
- Include comparison with fossil record dates to show how well the clock correlates

**Student learning target:**
"You count the DNA differences and know the divergence time from fossils. Divide the difference by the time, and you have the mutation rate. Then apply that rate to other pairs without fossils to estimate their divergence times."

**Placement:** Concept 3 (Molecular Clocks)

## Figure 7: Gene Transfer Mechanisms in Prokaryotes

**Purpose:** Illustrate the four main mechanisms of horizontal gene transfer in bacteria and archaea.

**What to show:**
- Four panels, one for each mechanism:
  1. **Transformation:** A bacterium taking up naked DNA from the environment (from dead, broken cells)
  2. **Transduction:** A bacteriophage (virus) accidentally packaging DNA from one bacterium and injecting it into another
  3. **Conjugation:** Two bacteria connected by a pilus, with a plasmid being transferred from donor to recipient
  4. **Gene Transfer Agents (GTAs):** Virus-like particles transferring DNA from one bacterium to another

- Each panel should show the DNA movement clearly (arrows, color changes, etc.)
- Label the donor and recipient organisms
- Indicate the result: the recipient now carries new genetic material

**Student learning target:**
"Bacteria share genes between unrelated cells, not just parent to offspring. Horizontal gene transfer happens through four main routes. This is why the prokaryotic tree of life looks more like a web than a tree."

**Placement:** Concept 3 (Horizontal Gene Transfer) / Synthesis

## Figure 8: Homologous Traits Across Vertebrates (Optional Enhancement)

**Purpose:** Show a suite of homologous traits shared by all vertebrates or specific subgroups, illustrating the principle of shared derived characters.

**What to show:**
- Chart or diagram showing vertebrates (fish, amphibian, reptile, bird, mammal)
- Row for each trait: vertebral column, hinged jaws, limbs, amniotic egg, hair, specialized ear bones, mammary glands
- Check marks (✓) for presence, blanks or X for absence
- Shows progression: all vertebrates have vertebral column; only amniotes have amniotic eggs; only mammals have hair and mammary glands

**Student learning target:**
"Each shared trait appears at a specific point in the tree and is inherited by all descendants. Reading this table, you can see which organisms are most closely related by counting shared derived traits."

**Placement:** Concept 2 (optional summary for reference)

## Figure 9: Convergent Evolution — Wings and Eyes

**Purpose:** Show examples of analogous structures that evolved independently in unrelated lineages, supporting the principle that appearance can deceive.

**What to show:**
- Bat wing, bird wing, insect wing (already described in Figure 1, but this could be a summary)
- OR: Camera eyes in humans/squid (both eyes with lenses, very different evolutionary origins)
- OR: Streamlined body shapes in sharks, dolphins, and ichthyosaurs (extinct marine reptiles)
- Show the similarities (function, appearance) alongside the deep differences (structure, evolutionary origin)

**Student learning target:**
"Evolution finds the same solutions to the same problems in different ways. When organisms look similar, it might be homology (shared descent) or analogy (independent evolution). You have to look at structure and ask: how different are the underlying mechanisms?"

**Placement:** Concept 1 (optional enhancement)

## Notes for the Artist

1. **Anatomical accuracy:** Figures 1, 3, and 4 require accurate skeletal anatomy. Consult standard vertebrate paleontology or zoology references for bone names and positions.

2. **Tree orientation:** Phylogenetic trees are conventionally drawn with the root on the left (or bottom) and modern species at the tips (right or top). Maintain this convention for clarity.

3. **Color and labeling:** Use color consistently (e.g., bone in one color, cartilage in another, soft tissue in another). Label every structure that is referenced in the text.

4. **Scale:** Figures showing multiple organisms (e.g., dolphin flipper vs. tuna fin) should note that they are not to scale, or should note relative sizes.

5. **Fossil reconstruction:** Archaeopteryx (Figure 3) is known from beautiful fossil specimens. Use paleontologically accurate reconstructions, not artistic embellishments.

6. **Captions:** Each figure should have a one- to two-sentence caption that names what the figure shows and what the student should notice ("Notice that the dolphin flipper bones are homologous to the human arm...").

### [FIGURE: Arctic Fox in Snow]

**Location:** Opening section, after the hook paragraph.

**Caption:** Arctic fox (Vulpes lagopus) in winter. Despite air temperatures reaching -40°C, the fox's body temperature remains ~37°C due to active thermoregulation (metabolic heat generation + insulation from dense fur).

**Purpose:** Anchor the opening puzzle. Make the problem concrete: how does this animal stay warm?

### [FIGURE: Surface-Area-Volume Scaling]

**Location:** Concept 2, after the sphere calculation.

**Description:** Side-by-side comparison of two spheres (r=1 and r=2), showing:
- Volume change: 4.2 → 33.5 (8x increase)
- Surface area change: 12.6 → 50.4 (4x increase)
- Surface-to-volume ratio: 3 → 1.5 (halved)

Also show a small mammal (mouse) and large mammal (moose) with relative surface areas shaded, illustrating why the mouse loses heat faster per unit mass.

**Purpose:** Make abstract geometry concrete. Show why small animals burn fuel frantically.

---

### [FIGURE: Homeostatic Feedback Loop]

**Location:** Concept 3, after "How Homeostasis Works" section.

**Description:** Diagram of negative feedback loop:
1. Sensor (thermoreceptor) detects environmental change
2. Arrow to control center (hypothalamus)
3. Comparison against set point
4. Arrow to effector (muscles, blood vessels, glands)
5. Response (shivering, vasoconstriction, etc.)
6. Return arrow showing deviation corrected

Overlay: Example pathway for cold response (temperature drops → thermoreceptors → hypothalamus → muscles contract → heat produced → temperature rises → deviation corrected).

**Purpose:** Make the servo loop visible. Show why homeostasis is active, not passive.

---

### [FIGURE: Endotherm vs. Ectotherm Thermoregulation]

**Location:** Concept 4, comparing the two strategies.

**Description:** Two parallel timelines over a 24-hour period:

**Endotherm (mammal):**
- x-axis: time (6am to 6am next day)
- y-axis: body temperature (°C)
- Top line: environmental temperature (varies 5°C to 25°C)
- Bottom line: animal body temperature (constant ~37°C)
- Shaded area between shows the "thermoregulatory burden"

**Ectotherm (lizard):**
- Same axes
- Top line: environmental temperature (varies 5°C to 35°C)
- Bottom line: animal body temperature (tracks environment, 5°C to 35°C)
- Behavior annotations: "shelter" during hot midday, "basking" at dawn

**Purpose:** Visual comparison of the two strategies. Show the energy cost (area of separation) and behavioral adaptation.

# Image and Figure Specifications: Chapter 52 — Ecology and the Biosphere

## Figures required for Attenborough × Feynman conversion

### [FIGURE: Hadley circulation cells and global biome distribution]

**Purpose:** Central mechanism figure. Shows why deserts occur at 30 degrees N/S latitude and rainforests at the equator.

**Content:**
- Three-cell circulation (Hadley, Ferrel, polar) from equator to pole
- Earth side view with latitude markings (0, 30, 60, 90 degrees)
- Arrows showing: warm air rising at equator, moving poleward aloft, descending at 30 degrees, moving toward poles again at 60 degrees
- Moisture content indicated (high at equator, very low at 30 degrees, moderate at 60 degrees)
- Corresponding biome zones aligned to latitude bands: rainforest (0-15°), desert (15-30°), grassland/forest (30-60°), tundra/boreal (60-90°)
- Ocean currents labeled: Gulf Stream, Humboldt Current

**Caption:** "The Hadley circulation redistributes heat and moisture from equator to poles, creating predictable patterns of rainfall and temperature that determine biome location. Deserts occur at 30 degrees N/S where descending dry air warms and removes moisture from the surface."

### [FIGURE: Four scales of ecological organization — the Karner blue butterfly example]

**Purpose:** Organizational figure showing how the four scales nest and interact.

**Content:**
- Four nested boxes or panels, each showing the Karner blue at a different scale
- Organismal: close-up of butterfly, lupine plant, physiological adaptations (wing muscles, egg-laying behavior)
- Population: map of Connecticut showing distribution of butterfly populations, year-to-year abundance graph, population size and genetic diversity
- Community: network diagram showing Karner blue, lupine, ants (*Formica*), parasitic wasps, herbivores, flowering plants, fungi
- Ecosystem: pyramid showing energy flow from sun through lupine (producer) to butterfly (primary consumer) to wasp (secondary consumer), plus nutrient cycling (nitrogen, phosphorus)

**Caption:** "Ecology operates at four nested scales. Understanding the Karner blue butterfly requires integrating organismal adaptations, population dynamics, community interactions, and ecosystem processes."

### [FIGURE: Lake turnover and thermocline dynamics across seasons]

**Purpose:** Worked example of abiotic mechanism regulating nutrient cycling.

**Content:**
- Four seasonal panels (winter, spring, summer, fall) of a temperate lake
- Winter: frozen surface, cold dense water at top, slightly warmer water below ice, 4°C water at bottom, oxygen-depleted zone at bottom
- Spring: ice melting, surface water cooling to 4°C (density maximum), turnover arrows showing bottom water rising to surface
- Summer: thermocline forming, warm epilimnion at surface, cold hypolimnion at bottom, nutrients trapped below thermocline
- Fall: surface water cooling, turnover arrows showing bottom water rising again
- Color gradient indicating temperature (warm=red/orange, cool=blue/purple)
- Legend showing oxygen-rich zones and nutrient-rich zones

**Caption:** "Spring and fall turnovers recycle nutrients from lake bottoms to the surface. This seasonal nutrient cycling is driven by changes in water density as temperature shifts. At 4°C, water is most dense and sinks."

### [FIGURE: Energy flow through trophic levels and the 10% rule]

**Purpose:** Explains why top predators are rare.

**Content:**
- Energy pyramid or trophic pyramid showing:
  - Bottom (widest): Plants (producers) — 100% of solar energy captured (approximately 10,000 calories per square meter per year in a temperate grassland)
  - Second level: Herbivores (primary consumers) — 10% of plant energy (1,000 calories)
  - Third level: Carnivores (secondary consumers) — 10% of herbivore energy (100 calories)
  - Top level (narrowest): Top predators (tertiary consumers) — 10% of carnivore energy (10 calories)
- Arrows showing energy loss at each step: respiration (heat), waste, structural biomass not consumed
- Bar graphs beside each level showing relative abundance (many plants, fewer herbivores, fewer carnivores, very few top predators)

**Caption:** "At each trophic level, only about 10% of energy from the previous level is available for growth. This is why ecosystems support many more plants than herbivores, and many more herbivores than carnivores. Top predators are always rare."

### [FIGURE: Biome distribution by temperature and precipitation]

**Purpose:** Shows how two abiotic factors (temperature and precipitation) determine biome type.

**Content:**
- X-axis: annual precipitation (0 to 250+ cm)
- Y-axis: mean annual temperature (-10 to +30°C)
- Plot points or zones for each biome:
  - Tropical wet forest: high temp, very high precipitation (upper right)
  - Tropical savanna: high temp, moderate precipitation
  - Subtropical desert: high temp, very low precipitation
  - Chaparral: moderate temp, moderate-low precipitation, concentrated in winter
  - Temperate grassland: moderate temp, moderate precipitation
  - Temperate deciduous forest: moderate temp, moderate-high precipitation
  - Boreal forest: low temp, low-moderate precipitation
  - Arctic tundra: very low temp, very low precipitation (lower left)
- Arrows or contours showing transition zones between biomes

**Caption:** "The major biomes can be predicted from two abiotic factors: mean annual temperature and annual precipitation. Similar climates support similar biomes regardless of geographic location."

### [FIGURE: Ocean upwelling and nutrient recycling]

**Purpose:** Parallel to lake turnover; shows nutrient cycling in marine ecosystems.

**Content:**
- Coastal region view showing:
  - Continent on left, ocean on right
  - Wind blowing parallel to coast (out to sea)
  - Surface water pushed offshore
  - Deep, nutrient-rich water rising from below to replace surface water
  - Zone of high primary productivity (phytoplankton bloom) in upwelling region
  - Labeled: Ekman spiral, upwelling zone, nutrient-rich water (high N, P, Si), areas of high fish productivity

**Caption:** "Coastal upwelling brings nutrient-rich deep water to the surface, fueling phytoplankton blooms and supporting major fisheries. Strong winds blow surface water offshore; denser water from below rises to replace it."

### [FIGURE: Tropical rainforest vertical structure]

**Purpose:** Illustrates how a single biome contains multiple habitats at different heights.

**Content:**
- Vertical cross-section of rainforest showing five layers:
  - Forest floor: dark, sparse vegetation, decaying leaf litter, fungi, decomposers
  - Understory: shrubby vegetation, shade-tolerant herbs, low light
  - Middle canopy: smaller trees, intermediate light, abundant fauna (birds, insects)
  - Upper canopy: closed layer of large tree crowns, high light
  - Emergent trees: giant trees rising above the canopy, full sunlight, wind exposure
- Species indicators in each layer (e.g., epiphytes on upper canopy trees, jaguars in understory, harpy eagles in emergent trees, leaf-cutter ants on forest floor)
- Light gradient indicated by color intensity

**Caption:** "Tropical rainforests contain distinct vertical layers, each with its own climate, light availability, and community of organisms. Epiphytes use trees as platforms to reach sunlight without competing with them for ground space."

### [FIGURE: Elevation and latitude biome parallels]

**Purpose:** Shows how climbing a mountain mimics moving toward a pole in terms of biome change.

**Content:**
- Two side-by-side illustrations:
  - Left: cross-section of a high mountain in the tropics, showing biome change from base to peak
    - Base (0 m): tropical rainforest
    - Mid-elevation (1,000-2,000 m): cloud forest
    - Higher (3,000-4,000 m): montane grassland
    - Peak (4,500+ m): alpine tundra
  - Right: North-South transect from equator to pole on flat terrain, showing same biome sequence:
    - Equator: tropical rainforest
    - 15-30° N: savanna/desert
    - 30-60° N: temperate grassland and deciduous forest
    - 60°+ N: boreal forest
    - Far north: arctic tundra
- Temperature and precipitation indicated for each zone in both figures

**Caption:** "Climbing a mountain produces the same sequence of biomes as traveling from the equator toward a pole. Both are driven by temperature decrease; elevation decreases temperature by about 6.5°C per kilometer of altitude gain."

## Figure specifications for production

**Style:** Clean, labeled diagrams with minimal decorative elements. Use consistent color scheme across all figures (blue for water, green for vegetation, orange/red for heat, arrows in dark gray).

**Accessibility:** All figures include alt text and captions that can stand alone. No information is conveyed only through color (use patterns and labels as well).

**Sources for reference:**
- Hadley cell diagram: Standard climatology textbooks, NOAA, NASA Earth Observatory
- Lake turnover: Limnology textbooks (Kalff, Wetzel)
- Trophic pyramid: Ecology textbooks; energy values from Whittaker and Likens
- Biome diagram: Whittaker's climate classification system
- Rainforest layers: Rainforest ecology papers, natural history museum illustrations

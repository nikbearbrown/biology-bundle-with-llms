# Images: Cell Reproduction

## Core Figures (Canonical to Chapter)

1. **[FIGURE: DNA Packaging Hierarchy]**
   - Raw DNA double helix (2 nm diameter)
   - DNA wrapped around histone octamer (nucleosome, 10 nm)
   - Nucleosomes coiled into 30-nm fiber
   - Further condensation into chromatin loops
   - Maximally condensed metaphase chromosome (1 μm)
   - Labels: "~7-fold compaction" per nucleosome, "~50-fold compaction" at 30-nm fiber level
   - Context: Shows how 2 meters of DNA compacts into a 10-μm nucleus

2. **[FIGURE: The Cell Cycle — Timing and Phases]**
   - Circle divided into segments labeled G1, S, G2, M
   - Proportions accurate: ~9 hours G1, ~10 hours S, ~4.5 hours G2, ~0.5 hours M (out of 24-hour cycle)
   - Sister chromatids shown during and after S phase
   - Centrosome duplication during S
   - Label: "Interphase ~90% of cell cycle"

3. **[FIGURE: PMAT — The Five Stages of Mitosis]**
   - Prophase: nuclear envelope dissolving, centrosomes migrating, spindle forming, chromosomes visible
   - Prometaphase: kinetochores assembling, spindle fibers attaching
   - Metaphase: chromosomes aligned at metaphase plate, sister chromatids still connected by cohesin
   - Anaphase: cohesin degrading, sister chromatids separating and being pulled to poles
   - Telophase: nuclear envelopes reforming, spindle disassembling
   - Color code: green (spindle microtubules), red (chromosomes), blue (kinetochores)

4. **[FIGURE: Cytokinesis in Animal and Plant Cells]**
   - Left panel (animal): actin ring forming at equator, cleavage furrow deepening, cell pinching in two
   - Right panel (plant): Golgi vesicles accumulating, phragmoplast forming, cell plate building outward
   - Final stage shows complete division
   - Context: Cell wall as the structural constraint explaining the difference

5. **[FIGURE: The Three Cell-Cycle Checkpoints]**
   - Circle (cell cycle) with three marked checkpoints:
     - G1 checkpoint: "DNA intact? Resources available?" → proceed or exit to G0
     - G2 checkpoint: "Replication complete? DNA intact?" → proceed or repair/apoptosis
     - M checkpoint (spindle checkpoint): "All sister chromatids attached?" → proceed to anaphase or wait
   - Decision tree at each checkpoint showing outcomes
   - Color: green (proceed), red (halt), yellow (repair)

6. **[FIGURE: Cyclin and Cdk Levels Throughout the Cell Cycle]**
   - X-axis: cell cycle phases (G1, S, G2, M)
   - Y-axis: protein concentration
   - Cyclin E peaks in late G1/early S
   - Cyclin A rises in S, peaks in G2
   - Cyclin B rises in G2, peaks in M
   - Cdk levels relatively constant
   - Label: "Cyclin-Cdk complexes drive the cell forward; as phase changes, cyclin is degraded and new cyclin appears"

7. **[FIGURE: p53 Flow Chart — DNA Damage Response]**
   - Box 1: "DNA damage detected"
   - Arrow to Box 2: "p53 activated, p53 levels rise"
   - Branches:
     - Path A: "DNA repair successful" → p53 levels drop → cell cycle resumes
     - Path B: "DNA damage irreparable" → p53 triggers apoptosis → cell dies
   - Context: Explains why p53 mutations are so dangerous (all three functions lost simultaneously)

8. **[FIGURE: Proto-oncogenes vs. Tumor Suppressors — The Two-Hit Hypothesis]**
   - Top row: Proto-oncogene (normal + hyperactive mutant) — one copy sufficient to affect phenotype
   - Middle row: Tumor suppressor (normal + non-functional mutant) — one copy usually okay (backup on homolog)
   - Bottom row: Two tumor suppressor mutations — both brakes lost, cell runs unchecked
   - Labels: "One-hit activation" vs. "Two-hit loss of function"

9. **[FIGURE: Anaphase Failure — Aneuploidy from Spindle Checkpoint Failure]**
   - Left: Normal anaphase — sister chromatids pulled to opposite poles equally
   - Right: Abnormal anaphase — sister chromatid pair not properly attached, unequal segregation
   - Result: One daughter cell with trisomy, one with monosomy
   - Context: Why aneuploidy correlates with spindle checkpoint failure

10. **[FIGURE: Binary Fission in Prokaryotes]**
    - Step 1: Circular chromosome attached to cell membrane at origin
    - Step 2: Bidirectional replication, two copies moving apart as membrane grows
    - Step 3: FtsZ ring assembling at equator
    - Step 4: Septum forming, cell wall growing inward
    - Step 5: Two daughter cells with replicated chromosomes
    - Comparison inset: FtsZ ring (prokaryote) vs. mitotic spindle (eukaryote)

## Supporting Figures (Context/Analogy)

11. **[FIGURE: Chromatin Accessibility in Liver vs. Nerve Cell]**
    - Two cells with identical DNA on same chromosomes
    - Liver cell: chromatin loose around digestive gene loci, tight around neurotransmitter loci
    - Nerve cell: chromatin tight around digestive loci, loose around neurotransmitter loci
    - Label: "Same DNA, different accessibility = different proteins = different cell type"

12. **[FIGURE: Human Papillomavirus and p53 Degradation]**
    - Normal cell: p53 accumulates when DNA is damaged, activates repair genes
    - HPV-infected cell: E6 protein binds p53, marks it for ubiquitination and degradation
    - Result: p53 levels stay low, checkpoint is disabled
    - Timeline arrow: "Years of accumulated mutations → cancer"

13. **[FIGURE: Cell Division Rate Across Cell Types]**
    - Bar chart or timeline showing:
      - Red blood cell precursor: divides every 24 hours (or less in early development)
      - Epithelial cell (gut, skin): divides every 24–48 hours
      - Fibroblast: divides every 24 hours
      - Neuron: remains in G0 indefinitely
      - Cardiac muscle cell: remains in G0 indefinitely
    - Context: Different tissue strategies based on turnover needs

14. **[FIGURE: FtsZ and Tubulin Homology]**
    - Side-by-side: FtsZ protein folding and tubulin protein folding
    - FtsZ polymerizing into ring (prokaryotic cytokinesis)
    - Tubulin polymerizing into microtubules (eukaryotic spindle)
    - Label: "Evolutionarily related proteins, different contexts"

## Diagrams to Avoid or Note

- Do not show the "tornado" or overly stylized spindle — keep it geometric and accurate (microtubules shown as lines/fibers, not as a vague cloud)
- Do not animate metaphase plate alignment without showing that cohesin proteins still hold sister chromatids together
- Do not show anaphase without explicitly showing cohesin degradation as the event that triggers separation
- Cell plate formation should show Golgi vesicles fusing, not magically appearing

## Caption Style

Each figure caption includes:
- **What:** Describe what is shown
- **Why:** Explain why this matters to the chapter's logic
- **Context:** Where this fits in the bigger picture

Example: "**[FIGURE: DNA Packaging Hierarchy]** — Raw DNA would stretch two meters if unwound from a human cell. To fit in a 10-micrometer nucleus, the cell packages DNA in four hierarchical steps: histone wrapping (nucleosomes), 30-nanometer fiber coiling, chromatin loops, and final condensation. The result is a metaphase chromosome, visible under a light microscope, containing the full genetic instructions for that cell compressed by roughly 200,000 times. This packing and unpacking is continuous throughout the cell cycle, allowing genes to be accessible when needed and condensed when cell division approaches."

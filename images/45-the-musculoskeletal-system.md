## Figure 1: Osteon Structure (Compact Bone Microarchitecture)

**Format:** Cross-sectional and longitudinal view of a single osteon

**Elements:**
- Haversian canal in center, containing blood vessel and nerve
- Concentric lamellae (rings of mineral matrix) surrounding the canal
- Osteocytes in lacunae (small chambers) within the matrix, connected by canaliculi (tiny channels)
- Labels: Haversian canal, lamella, osteocyte, lacuna, canaliculus

**Rationale:** Students often have no mental image of where osteocytes live or how they maintain bone tissue. This figure shows them embedded within the solid matrix, still connected via canaliculi. Essential for understanding how remodeling signals propagate through bone.

**Caption:** The osteon is the structural unit of compact bone. Osteocytes live in small chambers (lacunae) within the mineral matrix and communicate through tiny channels (canaliculi). Blood vessels and nerves run through the central Haversian canal, delivering nutrients and signaling information. This architecture allows the bone to be both strong and responsive to mechanical stress.

## Figure 2: Long Bone Structure—Compact vs. Spongy Bone

**Format:** Longitudinal section of a long bone (e.g., femur), showing proximal epiphysis, diaphysis, and distal epiphysis

**Elements:**
- Outer layer of compact bone (dense, organized osteons)
- Interior spongy bone shown as lattice of trabeculae
- Red bone marrow in spaces between trabeculae
- Medullary cavity in the center of the diaphysis
- Growth plate (epiphyseal plate) between epiphysis and diaphysis in a growing bone
- Labels: compact bone, spongy bone, trabecula, red marrow, medullary cavity, epiphyseal plate

**Rationale:** Introduces the two-tissue architecture. The visual contrast between dense outer bone and airy inner lattice explains why long bones are strong yet lightweight. Students should see that the two tissues serve different functions.

**Caption:** Compact bone forms the hard outer shell and resists stress along the length of the bone. Spongy bone fills the interior and the bone ends, where it absorbs and redirects stress coming from many directions. Red bone marrow nestles in the spaces between trabeculae, producing blood cells. This two-tissue arrangement makes bone both strong and light.

## Figure 3A: Sarcomere at Rest

**Format:** Side-by-side thin and thick filaments, showing their arrangement at rest

**Elements:**
- Two Z discs marking sarcomere boundaries
- Thin filaments (red, extending from Z disc) with tropomyosin (shown as blocking strands) covering myosin-binding sites
- Thick filaments (purple, in center) with myosin heads in "cocked" position but not bound
- A band (region of overlap), I band (thin filaments only), H zone (thick filaments only) labeled
- Tropomyosin explicitly shown blocking the binding sites on actin

**Rationale:** Establishes the baseline architecture. Students need to see exactly where myosin heads are positioned and why they cannot bind (tropomyosin is in the way). This makes the calcium-mediated displacement meaningful.

**Caption:** At rest, myosin heads are in the high-energy "cocked" position but tropomyosin blocks the myosin-binding sites on actin. No cross-bridges can form. The muscle is relaxed.

## Figure 3B: Sarcomere During Contraction

**Format:** Same structure as 3A, but with contraction progressed through the power stroke

**Elements:**
- Z discs moved closer together (sarcomere is shorter)
- Thin filaments pulled inward (I band narrower)
- Myosin heads attached to actin (cross-bridges shown)
- H zone (thick filaments only region) visibly narrower
- A band same length as at rest (myosin filaments unchanged)
- Tropomyosin moved aside (binding sites exposed)
- Arrows showing direction of filament sliding

**Rationale:** The visual comparison between rest and contraction state demonstrates that thin and thick filaments do not shorten, but their relative positions change. The unchanged A band length is particularly important for falsifying the "filaments get shorter" misconception.

**Caption:** During the power stroke, myosin heads pull thin filaments toward the center of the sarcomere. The Z discs move closer together, shortening the sarcomere. Note that the A band does not change length—the filaments do not shorten; they slide past each other.

## Figure 4: Cross-Bridge Cycle (Four States)

**Format:** Four panels arranged left to right, showing progression through one cycle

**Panel 1: Cocked State (at rest)**
- Myosin head in high-energy position, angled away from actin
- ADP and phosphate still attached to myosin head
- Tropomyosin blocking binding sites
- Label: "High-energy state, ATP hydrolyzed but not yet released"

**Panel 2: Binding**
- Tropomyosin moved aside (by calcium-troponin)
- Myosin head attached to actin (cross-bridge formed)
- ADP and phosphate still attached
- Label: "Cross-bridge formed, ready for power stroke"

**Panel 3: Power Stroke**
- Myosin head pivoted toward M line (shown with arrow)
- Actin pulled toward center
- ADP and phosphate released
- Label: "Myosin head pivots, pulling actin ~10 nm"

**Panel 4: ATP Binding and Release**
- New ATP molecule binds to myosin head
- Myosin detaches from actin
- ATP hydrolyzed back to high-energy state
- Myosin head re-cocks
- Label: "ATP binding causes release; ATP hydrolysis re-cocks the head"

**Color coding:** ATP = green, ADP+Pi = yellow, myosin head = purple, actin = red, calcium/troponin = orange

**Rationale:** The cycle is the chapter's deepest mechanism. A four-panel figure allows students to trace each state and understand what ATP and calcium do at each step. The arrows showing filament movement are essential.

**Caption:** The cross-bridge cycle repeats as long as ATP and calcium are present. (1) Myosin head is cocked and ready. (2) Calcium-troponin removes tropomyosin, exposing binding sites. Myosin attaches to actin. (3) The power stroke—myosin head pivots, pulling actin toward the center of the sarcomere by about 10 nanometers. (4) ATP binds to myosin, causing it to detach from actin. ATP hydrolysis re-cocks the head for the next cycle. Without ATP or without calcium (no uncovering of sites), the cycle stops.

## Figure 5: Tension vs. Sarcomere Length

**Format:** Graph with sarcomere length (horizontal axis, in micrometers) vs. tension (vertical axis, % of maximum)

**Graph elements:**
- X-axis labeled 1.5 to 3.5 micrometers (approximate sarcomere lengths)
- Curve showing peak tension around 2.0-2.2 micrometers (optimal overlap)
- Tension declining as sarcomere lengthens beyond optimal (filaments separate)
- Tension declining as sarcomere shortens beyond optimal (thin filaments overlap, Z disc collision)
- Annotations showing thin/thick filament overlap at different points

**Insets:** Three diagrams showing filament positions at three points on the curve:
- Left inset (stretched beyond optimal): thin and thick filaments overlap minimally
- Center inset (optimal): maximum overlap
- Right inset (shortened beyond optimal): thin filaments overlapping with each other in center

**Rationale:** This graph captures a functional principle that students should understand: muscle tension is not constant across all lengths. It explains why stretching reduces force (fewer cross-bridges), why shortening too much also reduces force (thin filaments collide), and why the "comfortable" resting length is near the peak.

**Caption:** A sarcomere produces maximum tension when thick and thin filaments overlap optimally. At this length, the most cross-bridges can form. Stretching reduces overlap and tension. Shortening beyond the optimal point causes thin filaments to overlap with each other at the center of the sarcomere, also reducing tension.

## Figure 6: Neuromuscular Junction

**Format:** Detailed view of the synaptic terminal, synaptic cleft, and muscle membrane

**Elements:**
- Motor neuron axon terminal (presynaptic side, top)
- Synaptic vesicles filled with acetylcholine
- Mitochondria providing ATP for vesicle mobilization
- Synaptic cleft (gap between neuron and muscle, ~50 nm)
- Muscle membrane (sarcolemma) with:
  - Acetylcholine receptors (ion channels) concentrated at junctional folds
  - Junctional folds (membrane invaginations)
  - Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) in the synaptic cleft
- Labels: axon terminal, synaptic vesicle, neurotransmitter, synaptic cleft, motor end plate, ACh receptor, junctional fold, acetylcholinesterase

**Color coding:** ACh molecules = bright color (e.g., green), enzymes = darker shades

**Rationale:** Students need to visualize this junction because it is the bottleneck between neural signal and muscle contraction. The presence of the gap and the enzymatic cleanup (AChE) are often overlooked but functionally important.

**Caption:** The neuromuscular junction is where motor neurons communicate with muscles. Acetylcholine (ACh) is released from synaptic vesicles in the neuron terminal, diffuses across the synaptic cleft, and binds receptors on the muscle membrane. Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) then breaks down ACh within milliseconds, preventing unwanted continued stimulation.

## Figure 7: Three Muscle Types—Comparison

**Format:** Three side-by-side images of muscle fiber cross-sections and single cells, one for each muscle type

**Skeletal Muscle:**
- Striated appearance (clear A and I bands)
- Multiple nuclei per cell (peripheral location)
- Unbranched, cylindrical fiber
- Label: "Striated, voluntary, multiple nuclei, fast-twitch"

**Cardiac Muscle:**
- Striated appearance
- Single or two nuclei per cell (central location)
- Branched interconnections
- Intercalated disks clearly visible at cell-cell junctions
- Label: "Striated, involuntary, intercalated disks for electrical coupling"

**Smooth Muscle:**
- No striations (appears smooth)
- Single nucleus per cell (central, elongated position)
- Tapered at both ends
- Spindle-shaped appearance
- Label: "Non-striated, involuntary, sustained contraction"

**Rationale:** A visual comparison is more memorable than a table. The structural differences (striations, branching, disk presence) reflect functional differences (control, speed, endurance).

**Caption:** The body has three muscle types. Skeletal muscle is striated and under voluntary control. Cardiac muscle is also striated but electrically coupled via intercalated disks, allowing synchronized contraction of the heart. Smooth muscle lacks striations and is involuntary, suited for sustained contraction in blood vessels and organs.

## Figure 8: Troponin-Tropomyosin Regulation

**Format:** Two states: "At Rest" and "With Calcium"

**At Rest panel:**
- Tropomyosin strands shown as blocking rods, covering myosin-binding sites on actin
- Troponin molecules bound to tropomyosin (shown in their "resting" conformation)
- No calcium present
- Binding sites inaccessible to myosin heads

**With Calcium panel:**
- Calcium ions bound to troponin subunits
- Troponin conformation change (shown as color shift or shape change)
- Tropomyosin pulled away from the binding sites
- Myosin-binding sites now exposed (shown as uncovered surfaces)
- Myosin head about to attach (shown as arrow or proximity)

**Color coding:** Calcium = yellow, troponin = orange, tropomyosin = red/light color, actin = red, myosin heads = purple

**Rationale:** This figure shows the mechanism by which calcium "gives permission" for contraction. It explains why tropomyosin blocking is not simply a passive physical block but a regulated control point.

**Caption:** Calcium is the on/off switch for muscle contraction. When calcium binds to troponin, it causes a conformational change that pulls tropomyosin aside, exposing the myosin-binding sites on actin. When calcium is reabsorbed, tropomyosin re-blocks the sites and contraction stops.

# Image Briefs: Animal Nutrition and the Digestive System

## Figure 1: The Alimentary Canal — Compartments and pH Environments

**Purpose:** Show the entire digestive tube with labeled regions, pH values at each region, and the primary enzymes/jobs at each region.

**What the student should notice:**
- The tube is continuous from mouth to anus.
- Each region has a different pH (color-code: mouth/esophagus neutral, stomach acidic/red, small intestine alkaline/blue, colon neutral).
- The pH changes are sharp transitions (not gradual).
- Sphincters (shown as ring-like constrictions) separate regions and control passage.
- Size of each region varies (stomach is a bulge; small intestine is long and narrow; colon is wider but shorter).

**Visual style:** Longitudinal cross-section of human torso, showing alimentary canal as a tube with regions highlighted. Labels for mouth, esophagus, stomach, pyloric sphincter, duodenum, jejunum, ileum, ileocecal valve, colon (ascending/transverse/descending/sigmoid), rectum, anus. pH scale on the left margin (0–14), with color bar showing where each region falls.

**Enzymes/jobs to label:**
- Mouth: salivary amylase (starch splitting), mastication
- Stomach: pepsin (protein cutting), HCl secretion, churning
- Duodenum: pancreatic amylase (starch), trypsin/chymotrypsin (protein), pancreatic lipase (fat), bile (fat emulsification)
- Jejunum: brush border enzymes (disaccharides, peptides), absorption
- Ileum: absorption, bile salt recovery
- Colon: water reabsorption, microbial fermentation

## Figure 2: Stomach pH and Pepsin Activation

**Purpose:** Show why the stomach's acidic environment is essential for protein digestion.

**What the student should notice:**
- Pepsinogen is produced as an inactive form (shown as a closed/folded shape).
- HCl cleaves a fragment of pepsinogen, converting it to pepsin (open/active shape).
- Pepsin can now cut proteins (shown as scissors cutting a chain).
- The pH gradient: as chyme moves toward the neutral duodenum, pepsin denatures (shown as the shape warping/unfolding into an inactive form again).

**Visual style:** Diagram showing the stomach lumen. Chief cells on the stomach lining secrete pepsinogen (shown as a closed blob). HCl (shown as H+ and Cl– ions) is secreted by parietal cells. Where HCl meets pepsinogen, activation occurs (pepsinogen opens into pepsin shape). Pepsin then cuts proteins (shown as long chains breaking into shorter fragments). Arrow pointing toward duodenum, with pepsin shown denaturing as pH rises.

**Key labels:**
- Chief cell (pepsinogen production)
- Parietal cell (HCl production)
- Pepsinogen (inactive form)
- Pepsin (active form)
- Protein substrate (long chain)
- Peptides (fragments)
- pH gradient arrow (2 in stomach → 8 in duodenum)

## Figure 3: Villi and Microvilli — Surface Area Amplification

**Purpose:** Show the architecture of villi and microvilli, and how they amplify surface area.

**What the student should notice:**
- The small intestine lining is not smooth; it is covered with finger-like projections (villi).
- Each villus is studded with tiny hair-like extensions (microvilli).
- The microvilli are the actual absorptive surface—where nutrients cross into the bloodstream.
- The multi-level folding (villi + microvilli) dramatically increases surface area compared to a smooth tube.

**Visual style:** Three-panel sequence:
- **Panel A:** Cross-section of smooth tube (hypothetical), showing the epithelial layer. Label: "Smooth tube: ~30 sq ft surface area"
- **Panel B:** Same cross-section, now with villi shown as finger-like projections into the lumen. Label: "With villi: ~100 sq ft surface area"
- **Panel C:** Magnified view of one villus, showing microvilli on the surface of absorptive cells. Blood capillaries inside the villus. Label: "With villi and microvilli: ~250 m² surface area"

**Key labels:**
- Lumen (inside of tube)
- Villi (finger-like projections)
- Microvilli (hair-like extensions on absorptive cells)
- Epithelial cells (absorptive cells)
- Blood capillary (inside villus, receives absorbed nutrients)
- Lymphatic capillary (inside villus, receives lipid-based nutrients)

**Detail to show:** One nutrient molecule (e.g., glucose) approaching the brush border, a transporter protein in the membrane, and the molecule crossing into the cell.

## Figure 4: Bile Salt Emulsification of Fat Globules

**Purpose:** Show why emulsification is necessary and how bile salts do it.

**What the student should notice:**
- Fat naturally forms large globules in water (shown as a huge blob on the left).
- Bile salts are amphipathic molecules: one end likes fat (hydrophobic), one end likes water (hydrophilic).
- Bile salts insert themselves at the oil-water interface, stabilizing small droplets.
- Small droplets have much more total surface area than one large globule.
- Lipase can now work on the increased surface area.

**Visual style:** 
- **Left panel:** Large fat globule in watery environment. Enzymes (lipase) around it, unable to penetrate. Label: "Large globule: lipase can only cut the surface."
- **Center panel:** Bile salts (shown as ← → shaped molecules) insert between water and fat at the interface. The large globule begins to fragment.
- **Right panel:** Many small fat droplets, each surrounded by a shell of bile salts (hydrophobic tails pointing inward, hydrophilic heads pointing outward toward water). Lipase molecules can now access the increased surface area. Label: "Emulsified fat: lipase can work on thousands of droplets."

**Key labels:**
- Fat/lipid (hydrophobic core)
- Water (hydrophilic environment)
- Bile salt (amphipathic, shown as molecule with distinct hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions)
- Lipase (enzyme)
- Micelle (small lipid droplet surrounded by bile salts)

## Figure 5: The Three Phases of Digestion — Hormonal Signals

**Purpose:** Show what triggers each of the three digestive phases and which hormones are involved.

**What the student should notice:**
- Digestion is not continuous; it happens in phases, each triggered by a different stimulus.
- The cephalic phase is purely neural (via the vagus nerve), not hormonal.
- The gastric phase is triggered by food in the stomach (stretch, protein, pH).
- The intestinal phase is triggered by chyme in the duodenum (acid, fat, protein).
- Different hormones are released in each phase: gastrin (gastric), secretin and CCK (intestinal).
- Each hormone has specific targets: gastrin → stomach, secretin → pancreas, CCK → pancreas and gallbladder.

**Visual style:** Three-column timeline showing the three phases:

**Column 1: Cephalic Phase**
- Trigger: Smell, sight, or thought of food
- Neural pathway: Olfactory bulb → hypothalamus → vagus nerve
- Result: Salivation, gastric acid secretion, stomach prepared

**Column 2: Gastric Phase**
- Trigger: Food in stomach (stretch, protein, low pH)
- Hormone: Gastrin (released by G cells in stomach)
- Result: Continued HCl secretion, stomach contractions, enzyme secretion

**Column 3: Intestinal Phase**
- Trigger: Chyme enters duodenum (acid, fat, protein)
- Hormones: Secretin (released by S cells), CCK (released by I cells)
- Result: Bicarbonate from pancreas, enzymes from pancreas, bile from gallbladder, modulation of intestinal motility

**Key labels:**
- Brain (smell, sight, thought)
- Stomach (stretch, protein, pH)
- Duodenum (acid, fat, protein)
- G cell (secretes gastrin)
- S cell (secretes secretin)
- I cell (secretes CCK)
- Pancreas (responds to secretin and CCK)
- Gallbladder (responds to CCK)

## Figure 6: Protein Digestion — Serial Enzyme Action

**Purpose:** Show why protein digestion requires multiple enzymes in sequence, each tuned to different substrate sizes.

**What the student should notice:**
- Proteins start large and folded.
- Pepsin (in stomach) cuts them into medium-sized peptides.
- Pancreatic proteases (in small intestine) cut peptides further into smaller peptides.
- Brush border peptidases (in small intestine) cut small peptides into amino acids.
- Each enzyme is optimized for its substrate size and its pH.
- The final product is single amino acids, which can be transported.

**Visual style:** Flowchart showing protein transformation:
- **Top:** Intact protein (folded blob with color gradient showing internal structure)
- **Arrow 1 (Stomach, pH 2):** Pepsin cuts → medium peptides (colored chains of ~5–10 units)
- **Arrow 2 (Duodenum, pH 8):** Trypsin, chymotrypsin cut → smaller peptides (colored chains of ~2–3 units)
- **Arrow 3 (Jejunum, pH 8):** Carboxypeptidase, dipeptidase cut → free amino acids (individual colored spheres)

**Key labels:**
- Pepsin (note: only works in acid)
- Trypsin, chymotrypsin (note: only works in neutral/alkaline pH)
- Carboxypeptidase, dipeptidase (note: cuts one amino acid at a time from the end)
- Peptide bonds (shown as lines between amino acids, getting broken at each step)

**Detail to include:** Show that pepsin cannot work in the small intestine because it denatures at neutral pH. Show that trypsin cannot be secreted directly into the stomach because the acid would destroy it before it could work.

## Figure 7: The Complete Meal — Carbohydrate, Protein, and Fat Pathways

**Purpose:** Show how a mixed meal (with carbohydrate, protein, and fat) is processed simultaneously through the digestive system.

**What the student should notice:**
- Three different nutrient types follow different pathways but happen in parallel in the small intestine.
- Carbohydrate is cut into glucose and absorbed.
- Protein is cut into amino acids and absorbed.
- Fat is emulsified, cut, reassembled into chylomicrons, and absorbed into the lymphatic system (not directly into the blood).
- The absorptive surface (villi-microvilli) handles all three in parallel.

**Visual style:** Longitudinal cross-section of the small intestine showing:
- **Top (lumen):** Carbohydrate particles (starches, disaccharides), protein fragments (peptides), fat droplets (emulsified by bile).
- **Middle (villi and brush border):** Three parallel sets of enzymatic breakdown and transport.
- **Bottom (capillaries and lymphatic vessels):** Glucose and amino acids entering blood capillaries, chylomicrons entering lymphatic capillary.

**Key labels and color coding:**
- Carbohydrate pathway: shown in one color (e.g., blue) → amylase → maltase → glucose → blood
- Protein pathway: shown in another color (e.g., green) → trypsin → peptidases → amino acids → blood
- Lipid pathway: shown in a third color (e.g., yellow) → bile emulsification → lipase → monoglycerides and fatty acids → recombination → chylomicrons → lymph

## General Visual Notes

- **Color scheme:** Use consistent colors across all figures (e.g., blue for carbohydrate, green for protein, yellow/orange for fat).
- **Labeling:** All technical terms should be labeled on first encounter in each figure.
- **Scale and realism:** Where accurate anatomy is important (e.g., stomach, small intestine, colon), use realistic proportions. Where pedagogical clarity is more important (e.g., enzyme mechanisms, villi structure), exaggerate or simplify as needed.
- **Student audience:** These figures are for undergraduate biology majors with some chemistry background. Assume familiarity with basic molecular structure (atoms, bonds, proteins as chains) but not with enzyme kinetics or advanced biochemistry.
- **Accessibility:** All figures should be accessible to colorblind readers (use patterns or textures in addition to color where needed).

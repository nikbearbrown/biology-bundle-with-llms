## Figure 1: Linear Genetic Map (Drosophila Chromosome 2)

**Caption:** Recombination frequencies between marker genes on *Drosophila* chromosome 2 translate into a linear genetic map. The distance between genes A and B (5 cM) indicates crossing over separates them in 5% of meioses in a heterozygote. Genes farther apart show higher recombination frequencies, proportional to the physical distance between them on the chromosome.

**Type:** Line diagram with labeled positions

**Composition:**
- Horizontal line representing chromosome
- Four genes marked at positions: gene A at 0 map units, gene B at 5 cM from A, gene C at 12 cM from A (so 7 cM from B), gene D at 20 cM from A
- Distance labels between each gene pair
- Legend noting that 1 cM corresponds to 1% recombination frequency

**Data source:** Morgan, T. H. et al. (1915) *The Mechanism of Mendelian Heredity*. Specific map values derived from original crossing-over experiments.

**Pedagogical role:** Concrete instantiation of Sturtevant's abstract insight—recombination frequency becomes a measurable proxy for chromosomal distance.

## Figure 2: Nondisjunction During Meiosis I vs II

**Caption:** Failure of homologous chromosomes to separate (nondisjunction) produces abnormal gametes with extra or missing chromosomes. The timing determines the pattern: nondisjunction in meiosis I creates two n+1 gametes and two n-1 gametes; in meiosis II, one n+1, one n-1, and two normal (n) gametes result.

**Type:** Comparative side-by-side cell diagrams

**Left side (Meiosis I nondisjunction):**
- Normal cell (2n) with two homologous chromosomes shown in different colors (one maternal, one paternal)
- After DNA replication: sister chromatids visible
- Metaphase I: homologs aligned but fail to separate
- Telophase I: both homologs move to one pole, leaving the other pole empty
- After Meiosis II: four gametes shown—two with extra copy (n+1), two with none (n-1)

**Right side (Meiosis II nondisjunction):**
- Same start, but homologs separate normally in meiosis I
- Metaphase II: sister chromatids aligned; in one secondary spermatocyte they fail to separate
- Telophase II: one cell gets both sister chromatids (n+1), one gets none (n-1), two get one each (n)
- Four gametes shown with proportions labeled

**Color coding:** Consistent colors for maternal/paternal chromosomes across both diagrams.

**Data source:** Standard cytology texts; diagram follows convention in Campbell *Biology* or similar introductory texts.

## Figure 3: Incidence of Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21) by Maternal Age

**Caption:** Risk of trisomy 21 rises sharply with maternal age, particularly after age 35. At age 20, the risk is about 1 in 1,500 births; by age 45, it approaches 1 in 30. This age effect correlates with the prolonged arrest of oocytes in meiosis I (decades-long wait from fetal development until ovulation).

**Type:** Bar chart or line graph with shaded confidence interval

**Axes:**
- X-axis: maternal age (20 to 50 years, increments of 5)
- Y-axis: incidence per 1,000 births (or risk ratio 1:number)
- Logarithmic scale on Y recommended to show the exponential relationship clearly

**Data:**
- Age 20: ~0.7 per 1,000 (1:1,500)
- Age 25: ~1.0 per 1,000 (1:1,000)
- Age 30: ~1.5 per 1,000 (1:700)
- Age 35: ~2.8 per 1,000 (1:350)
- Age 40: ~6.7 per 1,000 (1:150)
- Age 45: ~33 per 1,000 (1:30)

**Data source:** CDC, WHO, or peer-reviewed epidemiology study (e.g., Hassold & Hunt, 2001, "To err (meiotically) is human...").

**Key point highlighted:** The sharp inflection after 35 is well-documented and has important clinical implications for prenatal counseling.

## Figure 4: X Inactivation and Barr Body Formation in Females

**Caption:** In early female development, one X chromosome per cell becomes inactivated (randomly chosen, maternal or paternal). The inactivated X condenses into a Barr body. This process equalizes gene dosage between males (one X) and females (two X, but one inactive per cell), explaining why female heterozygotes for X-linked traits often display a patchy phenotype.

**Type:** Cell diagram sequence and genetic pedigree mockup

**Part A (Cell process):**
- Early female embryo: cell nucleus with two X chromosomes visible, both euchromatic (loosely packed)
- Arrow labeled "Random inactivation"
- Later stage: one X remains euchromatic (active), the other condenses into a dark-staining Barr body
- Several cells shown side-by-side with either the maternal or paternal X inactivated randomly

**Part B (Tortoiseshell cat example):**
- Diagram of cat's body showing patchy orange and black fur
- Label indicating orange patches derive from cells that inactivated the X carrying the black allele
- Black patches from cells that inactivated the X carrying the orange allele
- Note: only females are tortoiseshell (or extremely rare XXY males); homozygous females show uniform color

**Data source:** Calico/tortoiseshell cat genetics is well-documented; Barr body observation from classic cytology.

## Figure 5: Inversion Heterozygote During Meiosis I

**Caption:** When one homologous chromosome carries an inversion but the other does not, synapsis during meiosis requires one chromosome to form a loop to maintain point-for-point alignment of genes. Crossing over within the inverted region can produce recombinant gametes with duplications and deletions.

**Type:** Detailed chromosome diagram

**Composition:**
- Two homologous chromosomes drawn as thin lines, showing gene positions as tick marks or letters (A, B, C, D, E, etc.)
- First chromosome: normal order (A—B—C—D—E)
- Second chromosome: inversion (A—D—C—B—E); the CDB segment is flipped
- Synapsis shown with the inverted chromosome forming a loop
- The normal chromosome wraps around it, maintaining alignment of homologous regions
- Recombination shown as a crossover within the loop
- Resulting recombinant chromatids highlighted, showing duplications (extra genes) and deletions (missing genes)

**Pedagogical note:** This diagram is complex; include a caption emphasizing that the unusual topology (loop) is necessary for correct alignment but creates conditions for unequal exchange.

## Figure 6: Philadelphia Chromosome Translocation and BCR-ABL Fusion

**Caption:** In chronic myelogenous leukemia, a reciprocal translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22 brings the ABL gene (normally on chromosome 9) next to the BCR gene (normally on chromosome 22). The fusion creates an abnormal BCR-ABL protein with excessive kinase activity, driving uncontrolled cell division.

**Type:** Ideogram comparison (before and after)

**Before translocation:**
- Chromosome 9 shown with ABL gene marked near the tip
- Chromosome 22 shown with BCR gene marked near the tip
- Both labeled as normal

**After translocation (Philadelphia chromosome):**
- Chromosome 9: shortened, missing the ABL-bearing segment (now labeled 9q-)
- Chromosome 22: lengthened, now carrying the ABL segment next to BCR (labeled 22q+ or Ph chromosome)
- Fusion gene product shown: BCR-ABL with abnormal kinase domain active
- Inset or callout: "Uncontrolled tyrosine kinase activity" driving cell division

**Data source:** Rowley, J. D. (1973). "A new consistent chromosomal abnormality in chronic myelogenous leukemia..." *Nature*, 243, 290-293.

**Clinical note:** Philadelphia chromosome presence is used to diagnose CML and to target therapy (e.g., imatinib/Gleevec).

## Figure 7: (Optional) Chiasmata During Meiosis I

**Caption:** Chiasmata (singular: chiasma) are visible contact points between paired homologous chromosomes during prophase I. At these X-shaped junctions, physical exchange of DNA segments occurs, generating new allele combinations.

**Type:** Photomicrograph or high-fidelity diagram

**Content:**
- Meiotic cell in prophase I or metaphase I
- Bivalent (synapsed homologous pair) clearly visible
- Multiple chiasmata shown as characteristic X-shaped crossing points
- Colors or labels distinguishing the two homologs

**Data source:** Light microscopy image or electron micrograph from a classical cytology source.

**Note:** If actual photomicrograph is used, credit the original source. Many textbooks use *Drosophila* or *Neurospora* oocyte images.

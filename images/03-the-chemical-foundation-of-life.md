## FIGURE 1: Electron Configuration of Carbon

**Description:**
Central nucleus (six protons, six neutrons, shown as separate spheres or a cluster) surrounded by electron orbitals.
- First shell (1s orbital): two electrons, close to nucleus, spherical.
- Second shell: two electrons in 2s orbital (spherical), and four electrons in three 2p orbitals (dumbbell-shaped, oriented along x, y, z axes).
Outer electrons (valence electrons in the second shell) are highlighted.

**Caption:**
"Carbon has six electrons. The first shell (1s) is full with two. The second shell (2s and 2p) has four. Those four outer-shell electrons are the ones that do chemistry. The orbital shapes and spacing determine what bonds carbon can form."

**Caption alternative (more Attenborough-like):**
"The inner two electrons are satisfied. The outer four are restless. This imbalance is carbon's gift to life: enough electrons to bond with others, but not so many that bonding is forced. Flexibility."

## FIGURE 2: Ionic vs. Covalent Bonding — Sodium and Chlorine vs. Hydrogen and Oxygen

**Description:**
Two side-by-side panels.

**Panel A (Ionic Bonding — NaCl):**
Left: Sodium atom (11 protons, one valence electron shown as a dot).
Right: Chlorine atom (17 protons, seven valence electrons).
Arrow showing electron transfer from Na to Cl.
Result: Na⁺ ion (positive charge) and Cl⁻ ion (negative charge), attracted to each other by electrostatic force.

**Panel B (Covalent Bonding — Water):**
Oxygen atom (8 protons, six valence electrons) in the center.
Two hydrogen atoms (one electron each) approaching from above and below.
Electrons shown as dots or in orbitals, indicating sharing (electrons spend time with both nuclei).
Result: H-O-H molecule with shared electron pairs.

**Caption A:**
"Sodium loses its one electron to chlorine because losing is easier than keeping it. Chlorine gains because gaining fills its shell. The resulting ions are opposite and attracted. Salt: held by electrostatic force."

**Caption B:**
"Oxygen needs two more electrons to fill its shell. Hydrogen has one to share. They split the electrons between them. Both are satisfied. The shared electrons hold the molecule together."

## FIGURE 3: Water Molecule Geometry and Polarity

**Description:**
Central panel: Water molecule with O at center, two H atoms at ~104° angle. Electron clouds shown as shaded regions around atoms.
- Large red circle (or shaded region) around oxygen: δ- charge.
- Smaller red circles around hydrogen: δ+ charge.
- Arrows pointing from H toward O, indicating electron attraction.

Side panel: Show the orbital geometry (sp³ hybridization, if appropriate for audience) — four orbitals arranged tetrahedrally, two with bonding pairs (pointing toward H), two with lone pairs (pointing away).

**Caption:**
"Oxygen pulls harder than hydrogen. The shared electrons spend more time near oxygen than hydrogen, creating a charge imbalance. The H-O-H angle is ~104°—not straight, not 90°, but a specific geometry that makes water a polar molecule. This angle is everything."

## FIGURE 4: Hydrogen Bonding in Liquid Water

**Description:**
Cluster of water molecules shown as triangular shapes (O at apex, two H at base, representing the ~104° angle).
- Solid lines between O and H within molecules: covalent bonds.
- Dashed lines between the δ+ hydrogen of one molecule and the δ- oxygen of another: hydrogen bonds.
Each water molecule shown forming four hydrogen bonds (two as donor, two as acceptor).

**Color coding:**
- Oxygen: red or darker color
- Hydrogen: blue or lighter color
- Dashed bonds: gray or lighter

**Caption:**
"Water molecules hydrogen bond with each other. One molecule's partially positive hydrogen attracts another's partially negative oxygen. Each molecule can form up to four hydrogen bonds. This network, constantly breaking and reforming, gives water its unique properties."

## FIGURE 5: Ice vs. Liquid Water — Density Difference

**Description:**
Two panels side by side.

**Panel A (Liquid Water):**
Water molecules shown close together, somewhat randomly oriented, some empty space between them.
Label: "Liquid water at 4°C: density = 1.00 g/cm³"

**Panel B (Solid Ice):**
Water molecules locked in crystalline lattice, tetrahedrally arranged, more space between molecules than in liquid.
Label: "Ice at 0°C: density = 0.917 g/cm³"

**Bottom:** Show ice cube floating on water surface to illustrate the consequence.

**Caption:**
"When water freezes, hydrogen bonds lock the molecules into a lattice. The lattice is more open than the liquid. Ice is less dense. It floats. For most liquids, solid sinks. For water, solid floats. This quirk of geometry is why fish survive under ice in winter."

## FIGURE 7: Bicarbonate Buffer System — Equilibrium and Shifts

**Description:**
Central equilibrium equation:
H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻

Left panel (When acid is added):
- Arrow pointing right in the equation (equilibrium shifts left).
- HCO₃⁻ + H⁺ → H₂CO₃ (bicarbonate absorbs the added proton).
- Label: "pH drops less than without buffer."

Right panel (When base is added):
- Arrow pointing left in the equation (equilibrium shifts right).
- H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ (carbonic acid releases a proton).
- Released H⁺ reacts with added OH⁻ to form water.
- Label: "pH rises less than without buffer."

Bottom panel (Vent mechanism):
- H₂CO₃ → CO₂ + H₂O
- Arrow showing CO₂ being exhaled by lungs.
- Label: "Carbonic acid decomposes. CO₂ is exhaled. Buffer is restored."

**Caption:**
"The buffer works by interconversion. Bicarbonate absorbs added acid. Carbonic acid releases protons to neutralize added base. Carbon dioxide is exhaled, preventing indefinite H⁺ accumulation. This three-part system—chemical, renal, respiratory—maintains blood pH to within 0.3 units."

- "Ions: electron transfer, buffering"
- "Proteins: covalent backbones, hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions"
- "DNA: covalent phosphodiester bonds, hydrogen base pairing"

**Caption:**
"A diatom is a machine built from atoms. Covalent bonds form the backbone (proteins, DNA). Hydrogen bonding holds the structure together and allows specific recognition. Ionic gradients drive signals. Water is the medium. pH buffering maintains the chemical window. Every part depends on every other. The diatom is not a collection of parts—it is an integrated solution to the problem of staying alive."

## FIGURE 9: Scale Oscillation — Electrons to Diatom

**Description:**
Sequence of panels showing progressive zoom-out, or concentric circles showing increasing scale:

1. **Atomic level:** Electron cloud around a nucleus. Scale: ~10⁻¹⁰ m (1 Ångström).
2. **Molecular level:** Water molecule (H-O-H), covalent bonds, hydrogen bonding. Scale: ~10⁻⁹ m (nanometers).
3. **Cellular components:** Proteins, DNA, lipid bilayer. Scale: ~10⁻⁸ m.
4. **Cell level:** Diatom with visible organelles. Scale: ~10⁻⁶ m (micrometers).
5. **Organism in environment:** Diatom in pond water (illustrated, not to scale).

**Caption:**
"Chemistry operates across scales. The behavior of electrons determines the shape of molecules. Molecular shape determines whether water is polar. Polarity determines hydrogen bonding. Hydrogen bonding determines water's heat capacity and solvent power. These properties determine whether a cell can survive. A diatom is the visible consequence of laws written at the atomic scale."

## Notes on Illustration Style

- Follow the Attenborough × Feynman voice: specificity over abstraction. Use actual numbers, actual molecule structures, actual measurements.
- Avoid cartoonish simplification (e.g., electrons as dots orbiting like planets). Use orbital clouds or probability distributions if showing electron structure.
- Use color purposefully. Red/blue for polarity (δ-, δ+), gradients for concentration differences, arrows for directionality.
- Include scale labels on figures involving spatial dimensions.
- Captions should earn their grandeur through specificity: "a 10⁶-fold difference in proton concentration" instead of "huge difference."

## Figures Referenced in Chapter Text

### Figure 1: Peppered Moths Timeline (1848–1895)
- **Purpose:** Show the shift in moth phenotype frequency over 47 years
- **Content:** Two panels or continuous timeline showing light-colored moths → dark-colored moths in Manchester
- **Caption:** "As industrial soot darkened Manchester's trees, peppered moth populations shifted from predominantly light to predominantly dark. This change in allele frequency over time is the signature of directional selection."
- **Notes:** Include original illustration of pale and dark forms; optional: side view on sooty bark vs. lichen
- **Source compatibility:** Standard textbook illustrations widely available

### Figure 2: Hardy-Weinberg Equation and Genotype Frequencies
- **Purpose:** Visualize the parabolic relationship between allele frequency (p) and genotype frequencies
- **Content:** Graph with x-axis = p (or q), y-axis = frequency of genotypes; three curves (p², 2pq, q²)
- **Caption:** "The Hardy-Weinberg equation predicts genotype frequencies given allele frequencies. When p = 0.5, all three genotypes appear at specific frequencies. Deviations from this curve signal that evolution is occurring."
- **Notes:** Geometric interpretation helps intuition. Conic section (parabola) shape is memorable.

### Figure 3: Stabilizing, Directional, and Diversifying Selection
- **Purpose:** Show how three modes of selection change the distribution of phenotypes
- **Content:** Three bell curves showing:
  - Before/after for stabilizing (narrowing distribution)
  - Before/after for directional (shift in mean)
  - Before/after for diversifying (bimodal or widened distribution)
- **Caption:** "Different modes of selection produce different outcomes. Stabilizing selection reduces variation, directional selection shifts the population mean, and diversifying selection increases variation."
- **Notes:** Use color or shading to indicate selected region and resulting population

### Figure 4: Genetic Drift in Small vs. Large Populations
- **Purpose:** Illustrate that drift has stronger effects in small populations
- **Content:** Two parallel "random walks" — one starting with 10 individuals (shows larger fluctuations), one with 1,000 (shows smaller fluctuations)
- **Caption:** "Genetic drift causes random changes in allele frequency. In small populations (left), chance events have large impacts on allele frequencies. In large populations (right), drift is buffered by the large number of individuals."
- **Notes:** Optional animation concept: show multiple replicate simulations

### Figure 5: Founder Effect and Bottleneck Effect
- **Purpose:** Contrast two mechanisms by which small populations get founded
- **Content:** Two scenarios:
  - Founder: original large population → subset colonizes new area (shows different allele representation)
  - Bottleneck: large population → catastrophe → survivors (shows random survivors becoming whole population)
- **Caption:** "The founder effect occurs when a small subset of a population colonizes a new area, carrying alleles not representative of the source. The bottleneck effect occurs when a catastrophe kills most of a population randomly, leaving survivors as the new gene pool."
- **Notes:** Visual: show beads of different colors in a jar, subset drawn out (founder) vs. randomly selected (bottleneck)

### Figure 6: Side-Blotched Lizard Throat Colors and Rock-Paper-Scissors Dynamics
- **Purpose:** Illustrate frequency-dependent selection via rock-paper-scissors cycle
- **Content:** Three lizard head silhouettes (orange, blue, yellow throat colors) with arrows showing dominance pattern (orange → blue → yellow → orange)
- **Caption:** "Male side-blotched lizards come in three throat colors. Each color has a mating advantage over one color and a disadvantage against another, creating a stable cycle. This is frequency-dependent selection: which phenotype thrives depends on the frequency of other phenotypes."
- **Notes:** Optional: time series graph showing population frequencies of the three phenotypes oscillating

### Figure 7: Gene Flow and Clines
- **Purpose:** Show how gene flow creates gradual transitions (clines) between populations
- **Content:** Geographic map with latitude on x-axis and body size (or bloom time) on y-axis, showing smooth gradient
- **Caption:** "Species distributed across a continent often show gradual changes in phenotype (clines) rather than sharp boundaries. This occurs because gene flow between neighboring populations is slow enough to allow local selection but fast enough to prevent isolation."
- **Notes:** Example: body size increasing toward poles (Bergmann's rule) or bloom time changing with altitude

### Figure 8: Hardy-Weinberg Violet Flower Worked Example
- **Purpose:** Visual walkthrough of the calculation
- **Content:** 
  - Left panel: Population of 500 plants, 100 violet (V_), 400 white (vv)
  - Middle panel: Calculation steps (q² = 0.8 → q = √0.8 → p = 1 − q)
  - Right panel: Expected genotype frequencies (p² = ?, 2pq = ?, q² = ?)
- **Caption:** "Working through Hardy-Weinberg: given observed phenotypes, calculate allele frequencies, then predict genotype frequencies."
- **Notes:** Use boxes or flowchart format for clarity

### Figure 9: Peacock Tail and Sexual Selection
- **Purpose:** Illustrate the handicap principle and good genes hypothesis
- **Content:** Peacock with large tail being hunted by predator; inset showing fitness curves
- **Caption:** "A peacock's large tail impairs survival (makes it more visible to predators and slower to escape). Yet peahens prefer males with larger tails. This is explained by the handicap principle: only very healthy males can afford such costly ornaments."
- **Notes:** Optional: diagram showing trade-off between survival fitness and sexual fitness

### Figure 10: Assortative Mating and Inbreeding
- **Purpose:** Show how mate choice (not changing allele frequencies) changes genotype frequencies
- **Content:** Diagram showing random vs. assortative mating with colored individuals (tall + tall, short + short vs. random)
- **Caption:** "Assortative mating increases homozygosity without changing allele frequencies. Individuals mate with phenotypically similar partners, leading to increased inbreeding and potential inbreeding depression."
- **Notes:** Use visualization to show that allele frequency (color distribution) stays same, but genotype (pairing) changes

## Supplementary Visuals (Optional but Pedagogically Useful)

### Appendix A: The Five Conditions of Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium
- **Content:** Checklist-style infographic listing conditions and what happens when violated
- **Purpose:** Reinforces why no natural population is in equilibrium and when deviations matter

### Appendix B: Allele Frequency Change Under Different Mechanisms
- **Content:** Four graphs showing characteristic patterns of allele frequency change over time
  - Natural selection: directional, monotonic
  - Genetic drift: random walk, no direction
  - Gene flow: convergence toward source frequency
  - Mutation: very slow, linear increase
- **Purpose:** Let students predict mechanism from data pattern

### Appendix C: Relative Fitness in Action
- **Content:** Simulation or worked example showing how w (relative fitness coefficient) changes allele frequency
- **Purpose:** Demystifies the math behind selection; shows it is just proportional reproduction

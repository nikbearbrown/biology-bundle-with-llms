## A Note about AI

Biology is a field where the model is most usefully wrong. The textbook content is largely stable — the structure of a cell, the genetic code, the citric acid cycle, the principles of evolution — and the model handles it cleanly. The places where biology is moving (recent genomic discoveries, contested phylogenies, frontier neuroscience, emerging infectious diseases) are exactly where the model's training cutoff and its bias toward summarized averages most damage the picture.

The model has read every introductory biology textbook in print and many of the secondary references. It will produce a competent paragraph on cellular respiration, mitosis, Mendelian genetics, or natural selection. The competence is real. It becomes a problem at two specific places: when the question is about species-level specifics (the gestation period of *Loxodonta cyclotis*, the genome size of *Arabidopsis lyrata*) where the model confabulates plausibly, and when the question is about contested mechanisms (the role of horizontal gene transfer in eukaryotic evolution, the population genetics of recent admixture events) where the model treats living debates as settled.

Where the model genuinely helps: explaining mechanisms (how the sodium-potassium pump moves ions, how a ribosome reads an mRNA, how a meristem produces new cells), drilling you on terminology and taxonomy at the textbook level, producing structured comparisons across organism classes (prokaryote vs. eukaryote, vascular vs. nonvascular plant, ectotherm vs. endotherm), and walking through the experimental logic of canonical results (Hershey-Chase, Meselson-Stahl, the Luria-Delbrück fluctuation test).

Where the model does damage: producing specific facts about specific species or specific genes that may be wrong (and confidently presented as right), declaring contested mechanisms settled, treating recent discoveries it has read about as established when they are still being replicated, and confabulating gene names, protein functions, or pathway components that sound real and are not. The naming conventions in biology are dense and the model has read enough nomenclature to fluently invent plausible variants.

A specific failure mode worth naming: the model conflates the *what* and the *why* in evolutionary explanations. Asked why a trait exists, it will produce a smooth adaptationist story when the actual evolutionary history may involve drift, sexual selection, developmental constraint, or unresolved mystery. Treat any "this evolved because" sentence from the model as a hypothesis worth testing, not a conclusion worth memorizing.

The rule that covers all three: textbook biology from the model, with care; specific facts about specific organisms or genes verified against NCBI, the relevant taxonomy database, or a current edition of the textbook. The model is your tutor on principles. The literature is your authority on specifics. The model is a useful interlocutor; the textbook diagrams and the actual data are the ground truth.

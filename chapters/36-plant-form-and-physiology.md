# Chapter 36 — Plant Form and Physiology

*A tree has no heart. It moves water 100 meters upward against gravity without a pump. The explanation is physics, and it is worth understanding.*

---

A white spruce seedling, less than two centimeters tall. Place it on its side. Within a few hours, the root bends back downward, the shoot bends back upward. Nothing was watching. Nothing decided anything. Physics and chemistry did the work.

This is the character of plant biology. Plants cannot see the way animals can. They have no nervous systems. They have no behavior in the intentional sense. What they have is a set of mechanical systems — hydraulic, chemical, structural — so elegantly tuned that the outcome looks like awareness. The goal of this chapter is to see the machinery behind the appearance, and to understand why each piece works the way it does.

---

## The Architecture of Fixity

A plant is fixed in place. Everything about its form follows from that fact.

An animal that is fixed in place would starve. A plant turns fixity into a design constraint and solves it with two organ systems that face opposite directions. The shoot system grows upward, toward light and away from gravity. It builds leaves for photosynthesis, flowers for reproduction. The root system grows downward, into soil, toward water and minerals. It anchors the plant and absorbs what the shoot cannot.

Both systems are built from the same three tissue types, arranged differently. Dermal tissue covers the outside: the epidermis of roots and the waxy cuticle of leaves, with stomata — adjustable pores — for gas exchange. Vascular tissue runs through the middle: xylem conducts water upward, phloem conducts sugar in any direction. Ground tissue fills the rest: photosynthesizing cells in leaves, storage in roots, structural support throughout.

Student should see that the same tissue toolkit produces radically different internal architectures in the two organ systems.] -->

The vascular tissues are the architecture's spine, and the difference between them is worth noting. Xylem cells are dead at maturity. They are hollow tubes, stacked end to end, with their contents removed and their walls reinforced with lignin. They are plumbing. Phloem cells are alive at maturity — they must be alive, because they actively load and unload sugar using energy. Dead cells cannot regulate concentration gradients. So the two transport systems differ not just in what they carry but in whether they are alive or dead, and for a physical reason that will become clear when we look at how each one moves its cargo.

## How the Plant Gets Longer

At the tip of every root and every shoot is a meristem: a population of cells that divide constantly and never specialize. They are permanently embryonic. This is primary growth — growth in length — and it is the only kind of growth for most plants.

The shoot apical meristem produces cells that elongate below it, pushing the tip upward. The root apical meristem does the same below ground. Neither meristem moves through space; the elongating cells behind them push them forward. A root tip is protected from soil abrasion by the root cap, a disposable layer of cells that sloughs off as the root advances and is continuously replaced.

One direct consequence of having a growing tip is apical dominance. The shoot tip produces auxin — a hormone that flows downward and inhibits lateral buds from growing. While the tip is intact, the plant grows mostly tall rather than wide. Cut off the tip, and the auxin source is removed. The lateral buds are released. The plant branches.

This is why hedges are trimmed, why pruning produces bushier plants, why a Christmas tree grows tall and slender when left alone. The mechanism is not a decision by the plant. It is an auxin gradient. Auxin present means lateral bud suppressed. Auxin absent means lateral bud grows.

In woody plants, a second kind of meristem does different work. The vascular cambium is a thin cylinder of cells just inside the bark. It divides sideways, adding xylem inward and phloem outward. This is secondary growth — growth in girth. Each growing season, the vascular cambium lays down a new layer of xylem. In spring, when water is plentiful, the new xylem cells are large and thin-walled — earlywood. In summer, they are smaller and thick-walled — latewood. The boundary between them is visible as an annual ring. Count them and you count years.

Outside the cambium, the cork cambium produces bark: dead cork cells impregnated with suberin, a waxy polymer that sheds water and resists pathogens. The bark cracks as the trunk expands. The furrows you see on an old oak are records of secondary growth stretching a skin that cannot stretch — because the bark is dead.

## Water Moving 100 Meters Without a Pump

Now the mechanism that is most worth understanding, because it seems impossible until you see how it works.

Water enters a plant at the roots by osmosis. Root cells are full of dissolved sugars and ions — their water potential is more negative than the surrounding soil water. Water always moves from higher to lower water potential, which means from dilute solution toward concentrated solution. So water moves from soil into the root, passively, without any energy expenditure.

That gets water into the root. How does it get to a leaf 100 meters above?

The answer is that the tree does not push the water up. The leaves pull it.

Here is the mechanism. Inside a leaf, water evaporates from the surfaces of mesophyll cells into the intercellular air spaces. This evaporation — transpiration — leaves behind a thin water film on the cell surface. As water evaporates, the film gets thinner, and the menisci at the air-water interfaces in the cell wall pores get more curved. More curved menisci means greater surface tension, which means greater pull on the water below.

Now: water molecules are held together by hydrogen bonds. A column of water in a narrow tube resists being pulled apart — this is cohesion. When the leaf pulls on the water at the top of the xylem column, that pull is transmitted continuously down through the entire column to the roots. The column acts like a rope. The leaf is pulling the rope from the top. Root water replaces the water at the bottom as it is drawn in by osmosis.

This is the cohesion-tension theory. It has been measured and confirmed. The tension in the water column of a transpiring tree can exceed two megapascals — two million newtons per square meter — enough to pull water upward against both gravity and friction in the xylem tubes.

Water potential quantifies all of this. Pure water at atmospheric pressure has a water potential of zero (by convention). Adding dissolved solutes lowers water potential — makes it more negative. Applying tension to the water column also lowers water potential. In a transpiring tall tree, the water potential at the leaf can fall to −2 or −3 megapascals. Soil water, relatively dilute, sits near zero. The gradient is established. Water flows from high potential (soil, near zero) to low potential (leaf, strongly negative).

Student should see that no pump exists — the gradient is established by evaporation at the top.] -->

The risk is real: if air enters the xylem, the column breaks. A gas bubble cannot transmit tension. This is called cavitation or embolism. The xylem vessel is blocked. In drought conditions, cavitation spreads through many vessels and the tree cannot move water efficiently even if water is available in the soil. This is why drought kills trees from the top down — the uppermost vessels, under the greatest tension, cavitate first.

Trees manage this risk through vessel anatomy — ringed walls that prevent collapse under tension, pits that prevent a single bubble from spreading to adjacent vessels — but the risk is never eliminated. The calculation for cohesion-tension predicts that trees should cavitate frequently above about 70 meters. Redwoods exceed 110 meters. How they manage this is still not fully understood.

You can see the xylem flow directly. Cut a stalk of celery and stand it in red food coloring. Within hours, the colored water rises through the vascular bundles and stains the leaf veins. The color travels not by pumping but by the cohesion-tension pull that the leaf exerts as it transpires.

## Stomata: The Valve Between Carbon and Water

Photosynthesis requires CO₂. CO₂ enters through stomata. But open stomata also let water vapor escape. The plant cannot have more photosynthesis without more water loss. This is the central trade-off of plant physiology: fix carbon or conserve water.

Stomata open and close through the turgor of their guard cells. Two guard cells surround each stomatal pore. Their walls are thicker on the side adjacent to the pore, so when turgor rises and the cells swell, they bow apart and the pore opens. When turgor falls, they straighten and the pore closes.

Turgor in guard cells is controlled primarily by potassium. In the morning, potassium ions are actively pumped into the guard cells (this requires ATP). Water follows osmotically. The cells swell. The stoma opens. In the afternoon, especially if the soil is dry or the leaf is under heat stress, the stress hormone abscisic acid (ABA) signals the guard cells to release potassium. Water leaves by osmosis. The cells lose turgor. The stoma closes.

<!-- → [IMAGE: guard cell mechanism diagram — two panels. When soil water is scarce, roots produce ABA and transport it to leaves. ABA reaches the guard cells and triggers stomatal closure throughout the canopy. The plant sacrifices photosynthesis to prevent wilting. This is not a decision; it is a chemical gradient. But the outcome is adaptive.

## Sugar Flows by Pressure, Not by Tension

The phloem carries sugars in the opposite direction from how we just described water movement, and by a completely different mechanism.

In a source — a photosynthesizing leaf or a storage organ releasing starch — sucrose is actively loaded into the phloem sieve-tube elements by companion cells using ATP. This is expensive. But once the sugar is inside, osmosis takes over. The sieve-tube cell now has a high solute concentration. Water enters from the adjacent xylem by osmosis. The cell swells. Turgor pressure rises.

At a sink — a growing root, a developing seed, a ripening fruit — sugar is actively unloaded and consumed or stored. The sieve-tube cell at the sink end has less dissolved sugar than the source end. Less sugar means higher water potential means water leaves by osmosis. Turgor pressure is lower.

The pressure difference between source (high turgor) and sink (low turgor) drives bulk flow of sap from source to sink. This is the pressure-flow model. Unlike xylem, phloem does not depend on evaporation. It is driven by active loading and unloading at the two ends, with osmosis maintaining the pressure differential in between.

A striking demonstration: girdle a tree — remove a ring of bark all the way around the trunk, cutting the phloem but leaving the xylem. The tree continues to take up water and the crown continues to photosynthesize. But the sugar cannot reach the roots through the severed phloem. The roots starve. Within a growing season, the tree dies from the roots up, even though the leaves appear healthy for weeks. The xylem and phloem are parallel systems, anatomically adjacent, but carrying their cargo by entirely different mechanisms and serving different parts of the plant.

## How Plants Steer Growth

A plant cannot walk toward the light. It steers by growing faster on one side than the other. This is tropism: differential growth that bends the plant axis.

The shoot bends toward light because of auxin. The tip of the shoot contains phototropins — blue-light receptors. When blue light hits the shoot from one side, the phototropins on the lit side become active and somehow cause auxin to migrate laterally to the shaded side. More auxin on the shaded side means more elongation on the shaded side. The shoot bends toward the light, not because it aims at the light, but because one side of it grows faster than the other.

The experiment that established this was done by Peter Boysen-Jensen in 1913. He cut the tip off a seedling and replaced it on a gelatin block, then illuminated the seedling from one side. The seedling bent toward the light. The chemical signal crossed the gelatin. When he inserted an impermeable barrier on the illuminated side between the tip and the stalk, the bending stopped. When he inserted it on the shaded side, bending continued. The conclusion: a chemical travels from the tip down the shaded side to promote elongation there.

The root grows downward for a mechanically similar but chemically opposite reason. In the root cap — the protective tip of the root — cells called statocytes contain amyloplasts: dense starch granules that settle downward under gravity like sediment in a jar. When the root is tilted, the amyloplasts shift to the new lower wall. This shift triggers calcium signaling that redirects auxin transport to the lower side of the root.

Here is the reversal: in a shoot, auxin promotes elongation. In a root, auxin inhibits elongation. So auxin accumulating on the lower side of a root inhibits elongation there, while the upper side — with less auxin — elongates normally. The result is downward bending. The same hormone, in the same direction, produces opposite bending in root versus shoot, because the concentration threshold for inhibition versus stimulation differs between the two tissues.

## The Five Hormones and What They Actually Do

Five plant hormones are classically described. Each has a specific mechanistic role; each interacts with the others; none acts alone.

Auxin (primarily indole-3-acetic acid) promotes cell elongation in shoots and roots and controls the directionality of growth through lateral redistribution. It maintains apical dominance by flowing downward from the shoot tip and suppressing lateral buds. It is produced primarily in growing tips.

Gibberellins promote stem elongation, seed germination, and fruit development. In seeds, gibberellin breaks dormancy by activating amylase enzymes that digest starch reserves, giving the emerging seedling energy to grow. Dwarf varieties of wheat and rice — the foundation of the Green Revolution — produce less gibberellin or are less sensitive to it. Their shorter stems require less carbon allocation and are less likely to fall over (lodging) under the weight of grain-heavy seed heads.

Cytokinins promote cell division. Produced mainly in root tips, they travel upward through the xylem to shoot tissues. They delay leaf aging and counteract apical dominance by stimulating lateral bud growth. The balance between auxin (from the shoot tip, suppressing lateral buds) and cytokinin (from roots, promoting lateral bud growth) is one mechanism controlling plant architecture.

Abscisic acid is the stress signal. It accumulates when the plant is dehydrated, cold, or entering dormancy. It closes stomata. It induces dormancy in seeds and buds. As it breaks down over winter, the bud is released from dormancy and growth resumes. ABA is the molecular reason that seeds do not germinate in autumn, only to be killed by winter — the high ABA concentration suppresses germination until conditions are favorable.

Ethylene is a gas: C₂H₄. It diffuses through air and between cells. It promotes fruit ripening by triggering the synthesis of cell-wall-degrading enzymes and color-changing pigments. Ripe fruit emits ethylene, which accelerates ripening in nearby fruit — the mechanism behind the practice of storing fruit with an apple to speed ripening, or shipping fruit unripe and then exposing it to ethylene gas in warehouses to ripen it on demand. Ethylene also promotes leaf drop in autumn by weakening the abscission layer at the leaf base.

The interactions between these five are the actual control system. Auxin and cytokinin together determine whether a piece of plant tissue will form roots or shoots in tissue culture — the ratio decides the outcome. Gibberellin and ABA antagonize each other in seed dormancy. Ethylene and auxin interact in fruit set. No single hormone acts alone.

## Light, Time, and Flowering

Plants track the length of the night to determine when to flower. This is photoperiodism, and the mechanism involves a single photoreceptor molecule.

Phytochrome is a pigment that exists in two interconvertible forms. Pr absorbs red light (660 nm) and converts to Pfr. Pfr absorbs far-red light (730 nm) and reverts to Pr. In the dark, Pfr slowly converts back to Pr over several hours. In sunlight, the ratio is pushed strongly toward Pfr because sunlight is rich in red light.

By measuring how much Pfr remains at dawn — which reflects how long the night was — the plant can determine the season. A long night means most Pfr has reverted to Pr by dawn: autumn or winter is coming. A short night means substantial Pfr remains at dawn: summer is here. This pigment chemistry is the plant's calendar.

Long-day plants flower when nights are short (spinach, lettuce, some cereals). Short-day plants flower when nights are long (chrysanthemums, soybeans, poinsettias). What both are actually measuring is night length — not day length. A flash of red light in the middle of the night resets the phytochrome from Pr back to Pfr and prevents a short-day plant from flowering, even if the total night was long. That experiment demonstrates that it is the darkness that is being measured, not the light.

Shade adds another dimension. Under a forest canopy, sunlight is filtered through leaves. Chlorophyll absorbs red light preferentially. The light reaching a plant in the shade is enriched in far-red, depleted in red. The phytochrome ratio shifts toward Pr — the inactive form. The plant "detects" shade as a high far-red to red ratio. The response: rapid shoot elongation (the shade avoidance response), racing upward to escape the canopy. Seedlings in dense forest grow tall and thin; seedlings in clearings grow short and sturdy. The phytochrome ratio is doing the work.

## When Plants Call for Help

When a caterpillar chews on a tomato plant, the plant does not passively accept the damage. The wounding triggers production of jasmonates — lipid-derived signaling compounds — which activate genes for protease inhibitors. These inhibitors are proteins that disrupt the caterpillar's digestive enzymes. The caterpillar extracts less nutrition from the tissue it eats. The plant is chemically fighting back.

But it also signals outward. Volatile compounds — terpenes, alcohols, and green leaf volatiles — are released from the wound site into the air. These volatiles attract parasitoid wasps that lay eggs in caterpillar larvae. The larvae are eventually killed from the inside when the wasp eggs hatch. The plant is, in a functional sense, calling the predators of its predators.

None of this involves awareness. A wound activates a signaling pathway. The pathway produces volatiles. The volatiles happen to be attractive to wasps. The plants that produced the most effective volatiles survived to reproduce; over generations, selection refined the chemistry. The result looks like strategy. It is chemistry that was selected because it worked.

This is the theme that runs through all of plant physiology. The outcomes — growing toward light, sensing gravity, closing stomata in drought, calling wasps during attack — all look like decisions. None of them are. Each is a mechanical response to a chemical signal, produced by a gene, shaped by selection. Understanding the mechanism does not diminish the outcome. It makes it more impressive that physics and chemistry alone can produce a response so precisely calibrated to the problem.

A tree pulls water 100 meters upward using only hydrogen bonds and evaporation. No pump. No heart. Physics.

**Exercise 1 — Plant body plan: roots, shoots, and the modular logic**
Prompt a model: *"Plants have a fundamentally modular body plan — repeated units (nodes, internodes, leaves) generated by meristems at the tips of stems and roots. Walk me through why this modular plan suits a sessile organism (cannot move toward resources) and what advantages it provides over the determinate, fixed body plan of most animals. Specifically: how does indeterminate growth from meristems allow plants to respond to environmental conditions throughout life rather than during a defined developmental window?"*

Evaluate whether the model engages with the trade-off (animals achieve more efficient resource gathering through movement and complex sensory-motor integration; plants achieve resilience through modularity and the ability to regenerate from preserved meristems after damage), and whether it correctly identifies that plants can grow indefinitely in some directions because meristems retain the developmental capacity that animals lose after embryogenesis.

**Exercise 2 — Xylem and the cohesion-tension theory**
Prompt: *"A coast redwood can lift water 100+ meters from soil to canopy without any active pumping mechanism — the energy comes from solar-driven evaporation at the leaves. Walk me through the cohesion-tension theory: how transpiration creates negative pressure that pulls water up through xylem vessels, how the unique properties of water (cohesion through hydrogen bonding, adhesion to xylem walls) make this possible, and what the physical limit on tree height appears to be. What happens when the negative pressure exceeds the cohesion limit (cavitation), and how do trees recover?"*

Evaluate whether the model engages with the physics: water columns can sustain negative pressures of -10 to -100 atmospheres before cavitating, and tall trees operate near this limit. Cavitation produces gas bubbles that block xylem; trees recover via sap-pressure refilling (in some species) or by isolating cavitated xylem and growing new vessels.

**Exercise 3 — Stomata as the gas-exchange compromise**
Prompt: *"Stomata are pores in the leaf surface that open to allow CO₂ in for photosynthesis but consequently lose water through transpiration. Walk me through the central trade-off: a plant cannot photosynthesize without losing water, and the rate of water loss is typically much higher than the rate of CO₂ uptake (perhaps 100-fold). How do guard cells regulate stomatal opening in response to light, CO₂, water status, and circadian rhythms? What strategies do desert plants (CAM, succulence) use to minimize this loss?"*

Evaluate whether the model engages with the guard-cell mechanism (changes in turgor pressure controlled by ion movements — K⁺ uptake increases osmotic pressure, drawing in water and bowing the cells outward to open the pore), and the desert-plant strategies (CAM plants open stomata at night when humidity is high; succulents store water internally and reduce surface area).

**Exercise 4 — Phloem and pressure-flow loading**
Prompt: *"Phloem transports sugars from sources (mature leaves) to sinks (roots, fruits, growing tissues). Walk me through the pressure-flow hypothesis: active loading of sucrose into sieve tubes at sources increases solute concentration, drawing water from adjacent xylem by osmosis, raising hydrostatic pressure that drives bulk flow toward sinks where sucrose is unloaded. Why is this mechanism harder to study than xylem transport, and what does it explain about how plants allocate resources during seasonal transitions (e.g., spring leaf-out from stem reserves)?"*

Evaluate whether the model engages with the source-to-sink reversal logic: in spring, dormant trees mobilize stored carbohydrate in roots and stems to feed leaf-out, then mature leaves become sources that send sugar back to feed root growth and storage. The sieve-tube system can reverse direction depending on which tissues are donors and recipients.

**Exercise 5 — Plant hormones and the integration problem**
Prompt: *"Plants use a small set of hormones (auxin, cytokinin, gibberellin, abscisic acid, ethylene, brassinosteroids, and several minor classes) to coordinate growth, development, and stress responses. Walk me through how a single signal — auxin transport from shoot apex to root — integrates information about light direction (phototropism), gravity (gravitropism), and apical dominance (suppression of lateral bud growth). Why do plants use overlapping hormone signals rather than dedicated signal molecules for each function?"*

Evaluate whether the model engages with the few-hormones-many-effects logic: combinatorial signaling allows plants to respond context-specifically to environmental cues without evolving a unique hormone for each situation. The same auxin gradient triggers different responses in different cell types and developmental stages, modulated by which transcription factors and downstream genes are accessible.

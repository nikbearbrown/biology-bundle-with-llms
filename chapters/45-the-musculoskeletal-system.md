# Chapter 45 — The Musculoskeletal System
*How two molecules — ATP and calcium — run every movement you have ever made.*

---

Here is a fact about dead muscle that illuminates living muscle better than anything else I know.

When an animal dies and ATP production stops, the muscles stiffen. Within hours of death, every large muscle in the body locks rigid. You cannot bend the limbs. The phenomenon is called rigor mortis, and it lasts until the muscle proteins begin to decompose.

Why does the muscle lock?

Because contraction is not what requires ATP. *Relaxation* requires ATP. In a living muscle, a myosin molecule grips actin, performs a power stroke, and then — to let go — it must bind a new ATP molecule. The ATP binds, the grip releases, the myosin resets. When ATP runs out, the grip cannot release. Every myosin head in every muscle in the body is frozen mid-stroke, locked to actin, unable to let go.

The whole animal becomes one large rigor mortis. It is, in a sense, fully contracted and unable to relax.

This tells you the essential thing about muscle: it is a machine that requires continuous energy input not just to contract but to *stop* contracting. Understanding why — understanding the exact sequence of molecular events in the cross-bridge cycle — is understanding muscle. And understanding muscle leads directly to bone, because bone and muscle are a single system, each shaped by the other, both controlled by the same nervous architecture.

## What Bone Actually Is

Bone looks like a single material. It is not. Strip away the mineral and bone becomes rubbery — it will bend but not break. Heat it to incinerate the protein and it becomes brittle and shatters. Neither material alone is bone. Bone is a composite: collagen fibers that give tensile strength (resistance to being pulled apart) and hydroxyapatite crystals — calcium phosphate minerals — that give compressive strength (resistance to being crushed). The two are woven together at the nanoscale. The collagen handles tension; the mineral handles compression. Together they handle both.

Inside this composite matrix are four types of cells. Osteoblasts build: they secrete the protein matrix and deposit mineral crystals into it. As they work, they become trapped in lacunae — tiny spaces in the hardening matrix — and mature into osteocytes, which are the main sensor cells of bone. Osteoclasts demolish: large, multinucleate cells that release acids and enzymes to dissolve bone mineral and recycle calcium into the bloodstream. Osteoprogenitor cells are stem cells that divide to create new osteoblasts when damage requires repair.

The observation that changes how you think about bone is this: it is not static. About five to seven percent of your bone mass is recycled every week. Osteoclasts constantly dissolve small regions; osteoblasts constantly rebuild them. The result is bone that is not like a piece of lumber — passive, fixed, accumulating damage — but like a city that is continuously tearing down old neighborhoods and building new ones, always according to current need.

That current need is determined by mechanical stress. When bone is loaded — compressed by body weight, bent by muscle force, impacted during exercise — osteocytes sense the deformation and send signals that recruit osteoblasts. The bone thickens precisely at the point of stress. When bone is unloaded — a broken limb in a cast, a leg in zero gravity — osteoclasts work unopposed and the bone thins. This is Wolff's Law: bone architecture reflects the forces that act on it, because bone is always rebuilding itself according to those forces.

This has immediate practical consequences. Weight-bearing exercise strengthens bone not just by stressing muscle but by stressing bone directly, triggering remodeling toward a stronger structure. Immobilization weakens bone. An astronaut who spends six months in microgravity loses roughly one to two percent of bone mass per month in the weight-bearing skeleton — not because bone is passive and atrophies like unused muscle, but because the normal load is absent, osteoclast activity exceeds osteoblast activity, and the bone remodels toward a lighter, less-stressed structure. It is doing exactly what it is supposed to do, just in the wrong direction for someone who will eventually return to Earth's gravity.

The architecture of bone also reflects its function at two levels. Compact bone is the dense outer layer, organized into osteons — cylinders aligned along the long axis of the bone, with blood vessels running through central canals. Compact bone is good at resisting stress along that axis: compression and bending forces that run lengthwise. Spongy bone fills the interior of long bones and especially the ends, where joints receive impact from multiple directions. It is not solid; it is a lattice of struts and plates called trabeculae, aligned precisely along lines of stress. Spongy bone can compress slightly and redirect force through its lattice, handling the complex, multi-directional loads of joints in a way that compact bone could not.

## The Sliding Filament: How a Muscle Shortens

Now the muscle. And I want to start not with the contraction but with why the cell is built the way it is.

A muscle fiber is a single cell — one of the largest cells in the body, sometimes thirty centimeters long and a hundred micrometers in diameter. Inside it run hundreds of myofibrils, cylindrical protein bundles aligned in parallel. When you look at a muscle fiber under the microscope, you see repeating light and dark bands running perpendicular to the fiber's length. These stripes are not decorative. They are the structural signature of the molecular machine inside.

The repeating unit is the sarcomere. Each sarcomere is bounded by Z discs — protein structures that anchor thin filaments. From each Z disc, thin filaments extend inward toward the center of the sarcomere. In the center, thick filaments extend from a region called the M line. The thin filaments from each side and the thick filaments from the center overlap in two zones — this overlap zone is where contraction actually occurs.

The thin filaments are made of three proteins. Actin forms the structural backbone: two helical chains of actin monomers wound together like a twisted string of beads. Laid along the groove of that helix is tropomyosin, a long rod-like protein. Sitting at regular intervals along tropomyosin is troponin, a three-subunit protein complex. At rest, tropomyosin is positioned to physically block the sites on actin where myosin can bind.

The thick filaments are made of myosin. Each myosin molecule is a long protein with a globular head at one end. The heads project outward from the thick filament like oars from a boat. These heads have two critical properties: they can bind to actin, and they are ATPase enzymes — they break ATP down to ADP and inorganic phosphate, using that energy to cock themselves into a high-energy configuration.

<!-- → [IMAGE: Sarcomere diagram at rest and during contraction — top panel: relaxed sarcomere showing Z discs at each end, thin filaments (actin/tropomyosin/troponin) anchored to Z discs, thick filaments (myosin) in the center, A band, I band, and H zone all labeled with their lengths; bottom panel: contracted sarcomere showing thin filaments slid inward toward the M line, I bands narrowed, H zone narrowed, Z discs closer together, but A band unchanged — annotate: "filaments don't shorten; they slide past each other"] -->

Here is the sequence of events in one cross-bridge cycle:

First, a myosin head hydrolyzes ATP and uses the energy to pivot into its "cocked" position, like a lever pulled back. The ADP and phosphate remain bound to the head.

Second, the head attaches to actin. This attachment is possible only when actin's binding sites are exposed — more on that in a moment.

Third, the power stroke: the head pivots, pulling the actin filament toward the center of the sarcomere. ADP and phosphate are released. The head is now in its low-energy "post-stroke" position, still attached to actin.

Fourth, a new ATP molecule binds to the myosin head. This binding causes the head to release actin.

Fifth, the ATP is hydrolyzed, cocking the head back into its high-energy position. It is ready to attach again.

This cycle repeats. One full stroke moves actin roughly ten nanometers. With hundreds of myosin heads cycling simultaneously, each attached to actin for only a fraction of a second, the net effect is that thin filaments slide continuously toward the center of the sarcomere. The sarcomere shortens. The myofibril shortens. The muscle fiber shortens. The muscle contracts.

The key insight — the sliding filament theory of contraction — is that neither the thin nor the thick filaments change length. They slide past each other. This is why the A band, which spans the full length of the thick filaments, does not change length during contraction: the thick filaments don't shorten. The I bands, which contain only thin filaments, do narrow: thin filaments slide inward. The H zone, the central region where only thick filaments exist and no thin filaments overlap, narrows as thin filaments intrude. The distance between Z discs decreases. The sarcomere shortens.

## ATP Provides the Energy; Calcium Provides the Permission

This is the distinction the chapter title gestures toward, and it is worth being precise about.

ATP does the work. It cocks the myosin head. It releases the myosin from actin so the cycle can continue. Without ATP, every myosin head stays attached — the rigor mortis of the opening paragraph. With ATP continuously available, the cycle runs.

But ATP alone is not sufficient for contraction. At rest, even with plenty of ATP, the muscle does not contract. Why? Because tropomyosin is blocking the myosin-binding sites on actin. Myosin cannot bind what it cannot reach.

Calcium removes the block.

When a nerve signal tells a muscle to contract, calcium ions flood out of the sarcoplasmic reticulum — a calcium-storing compartment wrapped tightly around each myofibril — into the cytoplasm of the muscle cell. The calcium concentration in the cytoplasm rises by a factor of a hundred or more.

Calcium binds to troponin. This causes troponin to change shape, which in turn pulls tropomyosin along the actin helix, rotating it out of the blocking position. The myosin-binding sites on actin are now exposed. Cross-bridge cycling begins.

<!-- → [IMAGE: Two-panel actin regulatory diagram — left panel (relaxed): actin filament shown as helical backbone with tropomyosin lying in the groove blocking myosin-binding sites (labeled "sites blocked"), troponin complex sitting on tropomyosin, no Ca²⁺ present; right panel (activated): Ca²⁺ ions bound to troponin, troponin changed shape, tropomyosin rotated out of groove, myosin-binding sites now exposed (labeled "sites open"), myosin head approaching to bind — annotate: "calcium is the on/off switch; its presence permits contraction, its absence stops it"] -->

When the nerve stops firing, calcium is pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum by active transport — again requiring ATP. Calcium leaves troponin. Troponin returns to its original shape. Tropomyosin swings back into the blocking position. Actin sites are covered. The cross-bridge cycle stops. The muscle relaxes.

Calcium is the on/off switch. Its presence permits contraction. Its absence stops it.

This architecture has clinical implications everywhere. Certain poisons work by disrupting calcium reuptake, leaving tropomyosin permanently displaced, the muscle permanently activated. Strychnine, which causes the violent, rigid convulsions associated with its poisoning, acts partly this way. Cardiac function depends critically on the precise timing and magnitude of calcium release in heart muscle; dysregulation of cardiac calcium channels underlies several forms of heart disease. The calcium channels that release calcium in muscle cells are targets of several classes of anesthetic and antiarrhythmic drugs.

## From Nerve to Contraction

The nerve that tells a muscle to contract does not touch the muscle fiber. There is a gap — the neuromuscular junction, sometimes called the motor end plate — between the motor neuron's terminal and the muscle cell membrane. Across this gap, one molecule carries the command: acetylcholine.

When an action potential reaches the axon terminal, voltage-gated calcium channels open. Calcium floods the terminal. This triggers synaptic vesicles packed with acetylcholine to fuse with the terminal membrane and release their contents into the synaptic cleft.

Acetylcholine diffuses across the cleft and binds to receptors on the muscle cell membrane. These receptors are ion channels: when acetylcholine binds, they open, allowing sodium to flood into the muscle cell and potassium to flow out. The net effect is depolarization — the membrane potential of the muscle cell rises toward zero.

This depolarization spreads rapidly along the muscle cell membrane and deep into the fiber via T-tubules — invaginations of the membrane that carry the electrical signal to the interior of the fiber, where it contacts the sarcoplasmic reticulum. The depolarization triggers calcium release.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: Neuromuscular junction cascade — seven labeled steps in a flow diagram: (1) action potential arrives at axon terminal; (2) voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open, Ca²⁺ enters terminal; (3) synaptic vesicles fuse, ACh released into cleft; (4) ACh binds receptors on muscle membrane, Na⁺ flows in; (5) membrane depolarization spreads along sarcolemma and down T-tubules; (6) T-tubule depolarization triggers Ca²⁺ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum; (7) Ca²⁺ binds troponin → tropomyosin moves → cross-bridge cycling begins — annotate: "every link must function; any break stops contraction"; show acetylcholinesterase in the cleft breaking down ACh after transmission] -->

From the outside, the entire sequence looks instantaneous: a nerve fires, a muscle contracts. Inside, it is a cascade: action potential → calcium into axon terminal → acetylcholine release → acetylcholine binding → membrane depolarization → T-tubule conduction → calcium out of sarcoplasmic reticulum → troponin binding → tropomyosin displacement → cross-bridge cycling. Every link must function.

Acetylcholine does not stay in the cleft indefinitely. An enzyme called acetylcholinesterase, present in the cleft, degrades acetylcholine into acetyl and choline within milliseconds. Without this cleanup, the receptor channels would stay open, the muscle would stay depolarized, and contraction would be sustained indefinitely. This is exactly how certain nerve agents work: organophosphate compounds like sarin inhibit acetylcholinesterase. Acetylcholine accumulates in the cleft, receptors remain permanently activated, muscles contract and cannot relax — the victim experiences convulsions, then paralysis when the muscle fibers exhaust their ATP and cannot continue.

## Three Kinds of Muscle

Everything described above applies specifically to skeletal muscle, the muscle attached to bone and controlled voluntarily. But the body has two other kinds of muscle, each with a different architecture suited to a different purpose.

Cardiac muscle is striated — it has the same sarcomere organization as skeletal muscle, the same actin-myosin-tropomyosin-troponin regulatory system, the same calcium-dependent activation. But cardiac muscle cells differ in crucial ways. Each cardiac cell has only one nucleus and is smaller than a skeletal muscle fiber. Adjacent cardiac cells are connected by intercalated discs that contain gap junctions — direct electrical connections between cells. When one cardiac cell depolarizes, the depolarization spreads instantaneously to its neighbors through these junctions. The heart contracts as a single synchronized unit. This synchronization is not optional: if cardiac cells contracted independently, the heart would not pump — it would just twitch inefficiently.

Smooth muscle lines hollow organs: blood vessels, intestines, the bladder, the uterus. Its cells are small, each with a single nucleus, and they are not striated — there is no regular sarcomere organization visible under the microscope. The regulatory system is also different: calcium still triggers contraction, but it acts through a different protein (calmodulin rather than troponin), and the process is slower. Smooth muscle contracts slowly and can sustain contraction for long periods without fatigue. It is controlled by the autonomic nervous system and by hormones, not by voluntary neural commands. You do not decide to contract your intestinal smooth muscle; it contracts because the autonomic nervous system and local hormone signals coordinate it.

The three muscle types represent three points on a trade-off surface. Skeletal muscle is fast and strong but fatigues quickly and requires active neural control for every contraction. Smooth muscle is slow and can sustain contraction but cannot generate the rapid, powerful forces needed for locomotion. Cardiac muscle sits between them: striated and capable of sustained rhythmic contraction, synchronized electrically so it functions as a unit.

## Bone and Muscle as One System

A bone by itself is a lever. A muscle by itself has nothing to pull. The system only makes sense as a unit.

Muscles attach to bones at specific points. The origin is typically the more stable attachment, on the bone that does not move during the contraction. The insertion is on the bone that moves. Between them is the joint, which acts as a fulcrum. When the muscle shortens, the insertion bone rotates around the joint.

The geometry of this lever determines the force and speed of movement. Muscles whose insertions attach close to the joint sacrifice mechanical advantage — the lever arm is short — but gain speed. The elbow muscles attach close to the elbow joint, which is why the hand can move through a large arc quickly even though the muscle shortens only a few centimeters. Muscles whose insertions attach far from the joint gain mechanical advantage but sacrifice speed.

Muscles work in opposing pairs because they can only pull, never push. The biceps flexes the elbow; the triceps extends it. The quadriceps extends the knee; the hamstrings flex it. To move in one direction, one muscle of the pair contracts while the other relaxes. To hold a joint rigid, both contract simultaneously. Walking involves the coordinated alternation of dozens of such pairs, timed to keep the body balanced and propelled forward.

The nervous system controls force not by varying the size of individual muscle contractions — each fiber either fires fully or not at all — but by varying how many fibers fire simultaneously. A motor unit is one motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates. Small motor units, with few fibers, produce fine, weak contractions. Large motor units, with hundreds of fibers, produce powerful contractions. Precise movements like writing recruit small units. Heavy lifting recruits large ones.

And underneath all of this, bone is adapting. The force patterns that muscles exert on bone — day after day, training session after training session — are exactly the mechanical signals that drive bone remodeling. The athlete's skeleton does not just support the muscles; it is actively rebuilt by them. Bone and muscle form a dynamic partnership that changes together, failing together, strengthening together, through every physical demand placed on the body.

## What Rigor Mortis Explains

Return to where we began. A dead muscle stiffens because myosin cannot release actin without ATP. Every sarcomere is locked at whatever state it was in when ATP production stopped.

A living muscle can relax because ATP is always available to detach myosin from actin, and calcium is pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum to re-cover the actin binding sites. Relaxation is an active process requiring both energy and the functioning of the calcium pumps. When either fails, the muscle seizes.

This asymmetry — that it costs energy to *stop* — is unusual in machines and deeply characteristic of living systems. A car engine does no work when it is off. A muscle does metabolic work even at rest, just to stay prepared to move. It is burning ATP to maintain the resting state that allows rapid activation.

The entire nervous system is similar: neurons burn ATP continuously to maintain the ion gradients that make action potentials possible. The brain, which is two percent of body mass, uses twenty percent of the body's energy at rest, doing nothing but holding itself ready.

Life is not a machine you turn on. It is a machine that runs continuously and only does useful work when called upon. Bone remodeling runs continuously, maintaining the architecture appropriate to current loads. Calcium is continuously pumped in and out of storage, ready to trigger or terminate contraction. ATP is continuously produced and consumed.

That continuous expenditure of energy — that relentless opposition to equilibrium — is what distinguishes a living musculoskeletal system from an inert one. The instant energy production stops, the system begins its slow drift toward the equilibrium state: muscles locked, bone unmaintained, the whole structure settling toward the mineral scaffolding it ultimately is.

# Chapter 7 — The Plasma Membrane: How a Cell Keeps Itself Separate

*The cost of being alive is the cost of staying different.*

---

A salmon dies. Within hours, its muscle cells begin to swell. Not from decomposition — the proteins are intact, the DNA is undamaged. What is happening is pure physics. Water molecules are flooding into the cells because the concentration of dissolved material inside is higher than outside, and nothing is stopping them anymore.

That is the thing to notice. The membrane was stopping them before. The pump inside the cell was actively undoing what physics was constantly trying to do. The moment the pump stopped, physics won.

This is what the plasma membrane actually is: not a wall, but a system that holds entropy at bay. Everything a cell does — every signal it sends, every molecule it builds, every time it contracts or divides — depends on the membrane maintaining conditions inside that are radically different from conditions outside. The inside of a cell is not a diluted version of the world around it. It is a chemically distinct environment, maintained by constant expenditure of energy, and the moment that expenditure stops, the environment decays.

The question worth asking is: how does a thin sheet of molecules accomplish this? How can it be impermeable to some things and permeable to others? How can it be stable and fluid at the same time? How can it let a cell spend energy to push ions uphill against their concentration gradients, and then use those gradients to do work?

The answers all come back to one underlying chemistry problem — how to build a stable structure from molecules that refuse to sit next to each other.

---

## The Self-Assembling Wall

The core material of the membrane is the phospholipid, and the phospholipid has a problem: it is two incompatible things at once. One end of the molecule — the head — is charged and polar. It loves water. It forms hydrogen bonds with water molecules and is happy in an aqueous environment. The other end — two long fatty acid tails — is a hydrocarbon chain, nonpolar and hydrophobic. It hates water. Force it to sit next to a water molecule and you cost the system energy. Water molecules cannot form hydrogen bonds with the hydrocarbon tails, and water molecules really want to form hydrogen bonds.

<!-- → [IMAGE: single phospholipid molecule with labeled parts — hydrophilic phosphate head (circled, with annotation "charged, polar, hydrogen-bonds with water") and two hydrophobic fatty acid tails (annotated "nonpolar, no hydrogen bonding possible"). Student should see the structural basis for amphipathic behavior before seeing how this drives bilayer formation.] -->

So what happens when you drop phospholipids into water? Chemistry solves the problem for you. The molecules arrange themselves so that the water-hating tails hide from water. The water-loving heads face outward. The stable configuration — the lowest-energy arrangement — is a bilayer: two sheets of phospholipids back to back, heads facing the aqueous environment on both sides, tails pointing inward, away from water, forming a hydrophobic core in the middle.

This bilayer does not need to be assembled by the cell. It assembles itself. No blueprint, no energy input, no enzyme required. It is thermodynamically spontaneous. The laws of physics build the membrane.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: two-panel diagram. Left panel: phospholipids scattered in water with arrows showing random orientation. Right panel: the same molecules now arranged into a bilayer — heads facing the aqueous environments on both sides, tails meeting in the hydrophobic interior. A label should note "no energy required — this is the lowest-energy configuration." Student should understand self-assembly as a thermodynamic inevitability, not a biological design decision.] -->

But here is the immediate consequence: that hydrophobic core, which is so stable, is also completely impassable to anything charged or polar. A glucose molecule cannot cross it. A sodium ion cannot cross it. Even water crosses slowly, only because individual water molecules are small enough to slip through. The same property that makes the bilayer stable — the hydrophobic interior — makes it a barrier to almost everything the cell needs to import and export.

The cell solves this not by making the bilayer more permeable but by embedding proteins in it. The bilayer is the barrier. The proteins are the gates. This is the fluid mosaic model: a fluid bilayer of lipids with proteins floating in it like ships in an ocean, each protein doing a specific job — forming channels, carrying molecules, pumping ions, receiving signals.

"Fluid" is the right word. The lipids are not fixed in place. They move laterally within the membrane constantly, changing neighbors, drifting across the surface. Proteins drift too, though some are anchored. The membrane is not a rigid structure. It is a two-dimensional liquid that happens to be impermeable to most things.

<!-- → [IMAGE: cross-section of the fluid mosaic membrane — phospholipid bilayer with integral proteins spanning the membrane, peripheral proteins attached to the surface, cholesterol molecules interspersed among the phospholipid tails, and carbohydrate chains extending from glycoproteins on the outer face. Each component labeled. Student should see the membrane as a composite system, not a uniform lipid sheet.] -->

Cholesterol sits among the phospholipids and acts as a fluidity buffer. In cold temperatures, cholesterol prevents the membrane from freezing solid — it inserts between the fatty acid tails and prevents them from locking together. In warmth, it prevents the membrane from becoming too fluid and losing its integrity. An animal that lives in cold water needs more unsaturated fatty acids in its membrane — the kinks from double bonds prevent tight packing. A warm-blooded mammal uses more saturated fatty acids. The membrane's physical state is tuned to the temperature of the organism. The chemistry matches the environment.

---

## What Can and Cannot Cross, and Why

Not all molecules are equal in front of a membrane. The rules are almost entirely determined by charge and size.

Small, uncharged molecules — oxygen, carbon dioxide, water itself — can dissolve in the hydrophobic core and cross. They are nonpolar enough to enter the lipid environment. Oxygen diffuses from the lungs into the blood, into cells, without any protein help. Carbon dioxide diffuses out the same way. This is simple diffusion: the molecule moves from high concentration to low concentration, no machinery required, no energy spent.

Large or charged molecules cannot do this. Glucose, sodium, potassium, chloride, amino acids — all of these need a protein to cross. There are two kinds of protein-assisted crossing, and the difference between them matters.

Facilitated diffusion uses a carrier protein that has a binding site on one side of the membrane. A glucose molecule binds. The protein changes shape and delivers the glucose to the other side. The glucose falls off. The protein resets. This is still passive — no ATP is consumed. The driving force is the concentration gradient. Glucose flows from where it is more concentrated to where it is less concentrated, and the protein just provides the route.

The trade-off in facilitated diffusion is that the carrier has a maximum speed. Only so many glucose molecules can bind and be carried per second. If you increase the concentration gradient steeply enough, you reach a point where every carrier protein is occupied and the rate cannot increase further. This is carrier saturation. Simple diffusion has no equivalent limit — the more steeply the gradient slopes, the faster the net flow, without any cap.

<!-- → [CHART: two curves on the same axes — transport rate (y-axis) vs. concentration gradient steepness (x-axis). Simple diffusion: straight line rising indefinitely. Facilitated diffusion: curve that rises then flattens at a horizontal asymptote labeled "carrier saturation." Student should see that facilitated diffusion gains selectivity but loses the ability to scale linearly with gradient strength.] -->

Ion channels work a similar way but differently: rather than changing shape to carry one ion at a time, they form an open pore that ions flow through. A potassium channel is a tunnel through the protein, lined with atoms at precisely the right spacing to interact with a potassium ion but not with sodium, even though sodium is slightly smaller. The selectivity is geometric and chemical. The ion must fit the pore, shed its water shell, and interact with the protein's lining. Potassium channels pass potassium readily and exclude sodium almost completely. This specificity is not engineered — it evolved. But the precision is remarkable.

Now: all of the above is passive. The cell spends nothing on facilitated diffusion or ion channels. The concentration gradient does the work. But this creates a problem. If the cell only uses passive transport, the inside of the cell will eventually equilibrate with the outside. Sodium will diffuse in until the concentration equalizes. Potassium will diffuse out. The gradients will collapse. The cell will lose its chemical distinctiveness. And without those gradients, it cannot do much of what makes it alive.

This is why active transport exists.

## The Pump That Costs a Third of Your Energy

The sodium-potassium pump is a protein that sits in the membrane and moves sodium out and potassium in, against their concentration gradients. It does this using ATP. It breaks a phosphate bond, releases energy, and uses that energy to change its own shape in a controlled sequence of steps that moves the ions.

Let us trace those steps carefully, because the mechanism is the point.

The pump begins in a conformation that faces the cell's interior. In this state, it has high affinity for sodium — it binds three sodium ions from inside the cell.

ATP arrives. The pump takes the phosphate group from ATP and attaches it to itself. This phosphorylation stores the energy of the ATP bond in the protein's structure.

The phosphorylation causes a conformational change. The pump reorients, now facing the exterior. The new shape has low affinity for sodium. The three sodium ions release outside the cell.

On the outside, two potassium ions encounter the pump and bind. The pump now has high affinity for potassium in its exterior-facing conformation.

The phosphate group detaches. This triggers another conformational change. The pump reorients back toward the interior. Affinity for potassium drops. The two potassium ions release inside the cell.

The pump returns to its original state and is ready to run again.

Arrows between steps. Running tally beneath each step: "Na+ inside: 3, outside: 0" through the cycle to "Na+ inside: 0, outside: 3 / K+ inside: 2, outside: 0." Every cycle of this pump: three sodium ions exit, two potassium ions enter, one ATP is consumed.

The accounting matters. Three sodium out and two potassium in means more positive charge leaves the cell than enters. The inside of the cell becomes slightly more negative. This electrical imbalance — the cell's negativity — is not an accident. It is a tool. It will be the voltage that a neuron uses to fire an action potential. It will be the electrical gradient that drives calcium ions into cells, triggering muscle contraction.

And this pump runs constantly. Not occasionally. Not in response to a signal. Constantly. Because the gradients are always decaying. Sodium is always leaking inward; potassium is always leaking outward. The pump is running against a permanent leak. Stop the pump and the gradients collapse in minutes.

The cost is real: roughly one-third of a cell's total ATP budget goes to running this pump. A neuron, which uses the gradient most intensively, may spend even more. This is the cell's greatest metabolic expense, and the cell pays it without hesitation, because the gradient is more valuable than the ATP it costs.

Here is the elegant architecture behind this expense. The sodium gradient the pump creates is not only useful directly. It becomes the driving force for a dozen other transport processes. Glucose enters intestinal cells by secondary active transport — a carrier protein that moves glucose inward simultaneously with sodium flowing inward down its gradient. The glucose rides the sodium gradient. The cell spends ATP once to create the sodium gradient, and then amortizes that cost across every nutrient molecule that piggybacks on sodium's inward flow. One pump. Many passengers.

## The Numbers Behind the Gradient

The electrochemical gradient for an ion is not just a concentration difference. It includes the electrical potential across the membrane. Both components — chemical and electrical — determine how strongly an ion "wants" to cross.

The equilibrium potential for an ion — the voltage at which the electrical gradient exactly cancels the concentration gradient, producing zero net flow — is given by the Nernst equation:

$$E_{\text{ion}} = \frac{RT}{zF} \ln\left(\frac{[\text{ion}]_{\text{out}}}{[\text{ion}]_{\text{in}}}\right)$$

Here $R$ is the gas constant, $T$ is absolute temperature, $z$ is the charge of the ion, and $F$ is Faraday's constant — the charge carried by one mole of electrons.

For sodium at body temperature (310 K), with a concentration of about 145 mM outside and 12 mM inside, and $z = +1$:

$$E_{\text{Na}} = \frac{(8.314)(310)}{(1)(96485)} \ln\left(\frac{145}{12}\right) = 0.0267 \times \ln(12.08) \approx +67 \text{ mV}$$

Sodium "wants" to flow inward, driven by both the concentration gradient and the cell's negativity (the resting membrane potential is about −70 mV). The pump must push sodium out against a total driving force of about 67 mV.

For potassium, with about 155 mM inside and 5 mM outside:

$$E_{\text{K}} = 0.0267 \times \ln\left(\frac{5}{155}\right) \approx -97 \text{ mV}$$

Potassium "wants" to flow outward, driven by its concentration gradient, but the cell's negativity holds it in. At −70 mV resting potential, there is still a net outward driving force, but smaller. The pump pushes potassium inward against a smaller gradient.

Rows: Na+, K+, Ca2+, Cl−. This asymmetry — sodium is harder to pump out than potassium is to pump in — is consistent with the 3:2 ratio. More sodium must be moved per cycle because it is fighting a steeper uphill slope.

The total energy released by one mole of ATP under cellular conditions is about 30.5 kilojoules. The pump captures about 40% of this as useful ion movement work. The rest becomes heat. The pump is not perfectly efficient. No biological machine is. But 40% efficiency for a molecular machine cycling millions of times per second is remarkable engineering.

Now consider what happens when the pump is poisoned. A drug called ouabain binds the sodium-potassium pump on the extracellular side and blocks the potassium binding step. The pump stops. Sodium accumulates inside. The rising sodium concentration increases the osmotic pressure inside the cell, and water flows in. The cell swells. If the block persists, the cell lyses.

This is exactly what happened to the salmon. No poison needed. The salmon stopped making ATP. The pump stopped. Physics won.

## The Three Systems as One

Here is how it comes together.

The bilayer provides the barrier. It is stable because the hydrophobic tails are hiding from water, and they will hide from water indefinitely. You do not need to maintain the bilayer. It maintains itself.

Passive transport provides the cheap routes. Oxygen flows in without any cost. Carbon dioxide flows out without any cost. Facilitated diffusion moves glucose inward down its gradient, paying nothing in ATP. Ion channels permit rapid ion flow when they open, without energy expenditure. The cell gets a lot of transport done for free, as long as it maintains the concentration gradients.

Active transport maintains the gradients. The sodium-potassium pump is the core investment. Run it continuously, spend one-third of ATP, and the cell has: a sodium gradient pointing inward (used for secondary active transport of nutrients), a potassium gradient pointing outward (important for membrane potential), and a net negative charge inside (the foundation of electrical signaling). Three functional consequences from one pump.

Labels indicate which processes are free and which cost ATP. Student should see the three systems as one coordinated economy, not independent mechanisms.] -->

The three systems are not alternatives. They are interdependent. Knock out the pump, and the gradients collapse, and facilitated diffusion runs backward (glucose diffuses out, not in), and the electrical potential collapses. The cell is not a set of independent mechanisms. It is one system with multiple components.

The neuron demonstrates this most clearly. At rest, the membrane potential is −70 millivolts — inside negative, outside positive. This is entirely a creation of the sodium-potassium pump running against passive ion leaks. When a stimulus arrives, sodium channels open. Sodium floods in down its electrochemical gradient. The inside becomes less negative — it depolarizes. This is an action potential. The cell uses the stored gradient as a battery. The pump loaded the battery. The opening of a channel discharges it. The signal propagates.

Stop the pump for long enough and the battery is dead. The neuron cannot fire. The muscle cannot contract. The thought does not complete.

---

## What This Actually Means

The salmon is back in the water. Every one of its cells, while it was alive, was running this same calculation: spend ATP to push sodium out, keep potassium in, maintain a charge gradient, use that gradient to import nutrients and fire signals. Every cell. Every moment. The cost is real. One-third of all metabolic energy. This is not overhead. It is the work of being alive.

The membrane is not passive infrastructure. It is an active, energy-consuming machine that creates and maintains chemical difference. The inside of a cell is not the inside of its environment. It is a separate place with a separate chemistry, bounded by a thin sheet of self-assembled molecules and maintained by pumps running against entropy.

When the fish dies and the pumps stop, the inside becomes the outside. The membranes fail, the ions equilibrate, water floods in, and the cells swell. The chemistry that was maintained for years disappears in hours.

Life is not a structure. It is a process — the continuous expenditure of energy to stay different from the world around you.

The membrane is where that expenditure happens.

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. A phospholipid has a hydrophilic head and two hydrophobic tails. When placed in water, which part faces outward and which faces inward in the resulting bilayer? Explain why in terms of the energetics of hydrogen bonding. *Tests: structure of phospholipids and the thermodynamic basis of bilayer formation.*

2. Oxygen moves freely across the plasma membrane; sodium does not. Without invoking any specific transport protein, explain what property of each molecule determines whether it can dissolve in the hydrophobic core of the bilayer. *Tests: the relationship between molecular polarity and membrane permeability.*

3. The sodium-potassium pump moves three sodium ions out for every two potassium ions it moves in. Does this ratio mean the pump is moving more total mass in one direction than the other, or is there another consequence that matters more? Identify it and explain why it is significant. *Tests: understanding the electrical consequence of the 3:2 stoichiometry.*

**Application**

4. A cell is placed in a hypotonic solution — one with a lower solute concentration than the cell's interior. Predict the direction of water movement and the effect on cell volume. Now apply the same reasoning to a cell placed in a hypertonic solution. Why does the cell not simply run its sodium-potassium pump faster to counteract the osmotic imbalance? *Tests: osmosis and the limits of active transport as a response to osmotic stress.*

5. A drug inhibits all carrier proteins responsible for facilitated diffusion of glucose into muscle cells, while leaving the sodium-potassium pump fully functional. Predict the effect on intracellular glucose concentration over time. Then predict what happens to ATP production in those cells, and explain the causal chain. *Tests: the downstream metabolic consequences of blocking a specific passive transport route.*

6. Using the Nernst equation, calculate the equilibrium potential for chloride (Cl⁻, $z = -1$) given an intracellular concentration of 5 mM and an extracellular concentration of 110 mM at body temperature (310 K). Is chloride at equilibrium if the resting membrane potential is −70 mV? If not, in which direction will chloride flow when a chloride channel opens, and why? *Tests: application of the Nernst equation and interpretation of the result against a real membrane potential.*

**Synthesis**

7. The sodium gradient created by the sodium-potassium pump powers secondary active transport of glucose in intestinal cells. A student argues: "The cell is getting glucose for free — it isn't spending ATP on the glucose transport itself." Evaluate this argument. Is it correct? What is it missing? *Tests: the concept of energy amortization and the indirect cost of gradient-dependent transport.*

8. A researcher proposes that a membrane composed entirely of saturated phospholipids would be a better barrier — more impermeable — than one containing unsaturated phospholipids and cholesterol. Evaluate this claim. Under what conditions might a fully saturated membrane be problematic, and what specific function would it impair? *Tests: synthesis of lipid saturation, membrane fluidity, and the functional requirements of a working membrane.*

**Challenge**

9. A neuron is treated with ouabain, which blocks the potassium-binding step of the sodium-potassium pump. Trace the sequence of events from pump inhibition to the loss of the neuron's ability to fire an action potential. Your answer should name the specific gradients involved, the direction each collapses, and the consequence for membrane potential. At what point in this sequence would you expect the neuron to become unable to generate a signal, and why at that point rather than earlier or later? *Tests: causal chain from molecular mechanism to cellular failure; integrates pump stoichiometry, electrochemical gradients, and the electrical basis of neural signaling.*

# Chapter 27: Prokaryotes — Bacteria and Archaea

## TL;DR
Prokaryotes are the oldest, most abundant, and most metabolically diverse life forms on Earth. The discovery that prokaryotes split into two fundamentally different domains (Bacteria and Archaea) revealed that the tree of life has three major branches, not two—and that simple cellular architecture masks extraordinary biochemical range.

## Cold Open: Carl Woese's Surprise

In 1977, Carl Woese was examining something mundane: tiny differences in ribosomal RNA sequences from bacteria and organisms he called "archaebacteria." The data was supposed to show a minor branching. Instead, it showed that organisms traditionally grouped together were less closely related than bacteria were to humans.

Woese had just discovered that there were three fundamental branches of life, not two. And he'd done it not with a new microscope or fossil discovery, but by watching how RNA degraded differently in different creatures. The insight came not from looking at what organisms *were*, but from comparing what they could *do* at the molecular level.

This was the moment prokaryotic biology stopped being "bacteria and other bacteria" and became something far more complicated: the revelation that simple architecture could hide radical difference.

## Section 1: The Prokaryotic Body Plan — Simplicity That Persists

When you look at a prokaryote—any prokaryote—you are looking at a cell that has solved one problem: how to be small enough to respond to its environment quickly, large enough to do real chemistry, and resilient enough to survive almost anywhere. Call it the minimal cell.

**The negative definition problem.** Prokaryotes are defined by what they lack: no nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles, no compartmentalization. This definition is honest but unhelpful. It's like describing an Olympic athlete by saying what they *don't* have. The real story is what the architecture allows.

All prokaryotes share four structures. The **plasma membrane**—a thin lipid bilayer separating inside from outside. The **cytoplasm**—a crowded solution of organic molecules and salts where most chemistry happens. A **circular chromosome**—usually one, made of double-stranded DNA, located in a region called the nucleoid, not walled off from the rest of the cell. And **ribosomes**—the machines that read DNA and build proteins.

Then there are the additions: a **cell wall** outside the membrane that prevents osmotic burst; sometimes a sticky **capsule** that helps the cell cling to surfaces; **flagella** (whip-like structures) for motion; **pili** (hair-like projections) for attachment or gene transfer; and **plasmids**—small circles of extra DNA carrying bonus capabilities.

**Why the cell wall matters.** Prokaryotes live in osmotically hostile environments. Inside the cell, dissolved solutes create outward pressure. Without a rigid wall outside the membrane, water would rush in and the cell would burst. The wall prevents that. But it does more: it gives the cell its shape. A coccus is spherical because its wall is spherical. A bacillus is rod-shaped because its wall constrains growth that way.

**The chemical architecture varies.** And here is where Woese's insight enters. Bacteria use **peptidoglycan**—polysaccharide chains cross-linked by unusual peptides (including D-amino acids, which proteins normally never contain). This gave bacteria an evolutionary vulnerability: our antibiotics learned to mimic D-amino acids and sabotage the wall.

Archaea do not use peptidoglycan. They use something chemically similar in function but structurally different: **pseudopeptidoglycan**, or in some species, polysaccharides, glycoproteins, or pure protein walls. This difference is not decorative. Archaeal walls are resistant to the antibiotics that demolish bacterial walls.

**The membrane itself differs.** Bacterial membranes are built from fatty acids attached to glycerol—the standard setup. Archaeal membranes use **phytanyl groups** (hydrocarbons) attached to glycerol instead. In some archaea, these form a **lipid monolayer** instead of a bilayer. Same function (barrier between inside and outside), different chemistry.

**The deep consequence.** Bacteria and Archaea are not "more primitive and less primitive" versions of the same thing. They are two independently evolved solutions to the problem of being a small, fast cell in a hostile environment. They converged on the same basic architecture (cell wall, membrane, circular DNA, ribosomes) but built it from different chemical alphabets. This is what Woese discovered: they are as different from each other as either is from us. The tree of life is not a tree with bacteria on one branch and everything else on another. It is a tree with three equal trunks.

**Worked example: Gram staining reveals the difference.** Hans Christian Gram invented a stain (crystal violet, then iodine, then alcohol). It reveals peptidoglycan. Bacteria with thick peptidoglycan walls (Gram-positive) absorb the stain and turn blue. Bacteria with thin walls and an outer lipopolysaccharide envelope (Gram-negative) do not. Archaea do not contain peptidoglycan, so they do not respond to the stain at all. Under the microscope, one stain separates two bacterial families and excludes an entire domain. The chemistry writes itself into the color.

**Common misconception.** Archaea are not "primitive bacteria" or "archaebacteria." Woese called them that early on, but the name stuck like a misconception. The name misled: it suggested they were old bacteria. In fact, they are a separate domain. DNA-handling machinery in archaea is more similar to eukaryotic machinery than to bacterial machinery. Archaea are eukaryotes' cousins more than bacteria's cousins, even though all three are distinct.

## Section 2: Extremophiles — The Organism as Instrument

Prokaryotes do not simply tolerate extreme conditions. They have made extreme conditions home. A **thermophile** thrives in hot springs at 60 to 80 °C. A **hyperthermophile** at 80 to 122 °C. A **psychrophile** at -15 to 10 °C or colder. An **acidophile** at pH 3 or below. An **alkaliphile** at pH 9 or above. A **halophile** requires salt concentration of at least 0.2 M (seawater is about 0.6 M). A **radioresistant organism** survives high radiation.

**Why does this matter?** Extremophiles reveal that what we call "extreme" is a matter of perspective. Life does not have one preferred temperature, one preferred pH, one preferred salinity. Life has found thousands. The biochemistry adapts. Enzymes in thermophiles remain stable at temperatures that would denature most proteins. Membranes in psychrophiles stay fluid in ice. Cell walls in halophiles retain function when surrounded by brine.

**The Dead Sea case.** Between Jordan and Israel lies the Dead Sea, a hypersaline basin with sodium ten times higher than seawater, magnesium forty times higher, and acidic pH around 6.0. This should be sterile. It is not. Halophilic bacteria (*Halobacterium*, *Halorubrum sodomense*, *Halobaculum gomorrense*) and archaeans (*Haloarcula marismortui*) thrive there in thick bacterial mats. When salt-loving organisms concentrate pigments in their membranes to protect themselves from intense solar radiation in shallow water, their mats turn red or orange. The color is a chemical solution to a physical problem.

**The deeper principle.** Extremophiles do not prove that prokaryotes are tough. They prove that prokaryotic chemistry is flexible. Genes encode proteins. Proteins fold into shapes that perform chemistry. Different sequences fold into shapes that perform chemistry at different temperatures, different pH values, different salinity. There is no universal temperature for life, no universal pH. There is only: *whatever pH the enzymes in that organism were selected to tolerate*.

**The cultivation problem.** Over 99 percent of prokaryotes cannot be cultured in the laboratory. This is not because they are fragile. It is because we do not know what they eat, what temperature they prefer, what micronutrients they need, or whether they require the presence of other species. Some are obligate intracellular parasites: they cannot survive outside a host cell. Others enter a **viable-but-non-culturable (VBNC) state**—a dormancy triggered by environmental stress, from which they can revive when conditions improve. Most prokaryotes in soil and ocean are non-culturable. Yet they are alive, as shown by extracting their DNA and amplifying specific genes like 16S rRNA using PCR.

**Common misconception.** Extremophiles are not weird leftovers from Earth's ancient past. They are not "living fossils" of what the first life looked like. They are modern organisms, adapted to modern niches. Some thrive in places that have been extreme for billions of years (like hot springs). Others thrive in places humans made extreme (like soda lakes with unusually high pH, or polluted sites with high metal concentrations). Extremophily is not primitiveness. It is specialization.

## Section 3: How Prokaryotes Metabolize — The Four Dimensions of Feeding

Prokaryotes obtain energy and carbon in combinations that account for eight different nutritional types. Two dimensions matter: *Where does the energy come from?* and *Where does the carbon come from?*

**Energy sources: light or chemicals.** A **phototroph** captures energy from sunlight, using chlorophyll or (in some cases) bacterial rhodopsin. A **chemotroph** extracts energy from chemical compounds. Within chemotrophs: **chemoorganotrophs** use organic compounds (like glucose); **chemolithotrophs** use inorganic compounds (like sulfur or iron).

**Carbon sources: CO2 or organic molecules.** An **autotroph** builds all its organic molecules from inorganic carbon dioxide. A **heterotroph** uses carbon from organic compounds.

**Combine them.** A **photoautotroph** uses light energy and carbon dioxide—like cyanobacteria and all green plants. A **chemolithoautotroph** uses chemical energy from inorganic compounds and carbon dioxide—like bacteria living near hydrothermal vents, which oxidize hydrogen sulfide. A **chemoorganoheterotroph** uses energy and carbon from organic matter—like us, and like most bacteria that decompose dead things. There are also **photoheterotrophs** (rare): they use light energy but get carbon from organic compounds.

**Why this matters.** Different metabolic types fill different niches. Prokaryotes have partitioned Earth's energy sources among themselves. No ecosystem contains only one type. A healthy soil has photoautotrophs (cyanobacteria fixing carbon), chemolithotrophs (bacteria oxidizing minerals), chemoorganoheterotrophs (bacteria decomposing dead matter). Each uses what the others leave behind. This is not cooperation designed by evolution. It is a partition of resources driven by each organism's genes.

**The oxygen story.** Early Earth had no free oxygen. Anaerobic organisms dominated—chemotrophs using inorganic compounds as energy sources and terminal electron acceptors. Then, about 2.5 to 3 billion years ago, **cyanobacteria** evolved photosynthesis that split water molecules, releasing oxygen as waste. Oxygen accumulated in the atmosphere over hundreds of millions of years. This "Great Oxidation Event" was not progress. It was a poisoning event. Oxygen is reactive. It damages proteins and DNA. Organisms that had thrived in anaerobic oceans for billions of years had to either die, move to oxygen-free niches (where descendants still live), or evolve enzymes to tolerate oxygen and eventually exploit it for energy.

Aerobic respiration, using oxygen as the final electron acceptor, extracts more energy per glucose molecule than any anaerobic pathway. This advantage drove selection for oxygen-tolerant metabolism. But it was not inevitable. Even today, vast regions of the ocean bottom remain anoxic, and anaerobic prokaryotes thrive there.

**Worked example: Nitrogen fixation.** Nitrogen gas (N2) makes up 78 percent of Earth's atmosphere. Plants cannot use it. Eukaryotes cannot use it. The largest pool of nitrogen available—the air itself—is inaccessible to the kingdoms that need nitrogen most.

Prokaryotes can use it. Specifically, certain bacteria carry the enzyme **nitrogenase**, which catalyzes this reaction:

$$N_2 + 16\text{ ATP} + 8e^- + 8H^+ \rightarrow 2NH_3 + 16\text{ ADP} + 16P_i + H_2$$

This reaction fixes about 100 to 180 million metric tons of nitrogen per year, contributing 65 percent of the nitrogen used in agriculture. **Nitrogen-fixing bacteria** like *Azotobacter* live freely in soil. Others, like *Rhizobium*, live symbiotically in root nodules of legumes (beans, peas, soybeans). The legume provides the bacterium with carbohydrates from photosynthesis. The bacterium provides the plant with ammonia, a form of nitrogen the plant can use. The plant benefits from an endless source of atmospheric nitrogen. The bacterium gets shelter and sugar. The soil gets fertilized for free. This is why crop rotation works: plant beans this year, corn next year, and the soil nitrogen level stays high.

## Section 4: The Three Domains — Woese Rewrites the Tree of Life

In the 1970s, the textbook taxonomy of life was straightforward. There was a kingdom Bacteria and a kingdom Archaebacteria (confusingly grouped with bacteria despite being distinct). Then there were four more kingdoms: Monera, Protista, Plantae, Fungi, Animalia. The divisions were based on visible features: nucleus or no nucleus, single-celled or multicellular, how the organism made its living.

Carl Woese questioned this. Instead of looking at morphology, he sequenced small-subunit ribosomal RNA (16S rRNA) from dozens of organisms. Ribosomes are ancient, found in all life. Ribosomal RNA changes slowly enough that distant relationships show up, fast enough that recent ones do too. It is a molecular clock.

The data surprised everyone. The tree did not show bacteria as one group and archaebacteria as another. It showed three separate trunks.

**Domain Bacteria.** The familiar prokaryotes: *E. coli*, *Salmonella*, cyanobacteria, the bacteria in your gut. They use peptidoglycan cell walls (with rare exceptions), fatty acid membranes, and standard ribosomal RNA.

**Domain Archaea.** Organisms that had been lumped with bacteria but were profoundly different. No peptidoglycan walls. Phytanyl-based membranes. DNA replication machinery closer to eukaryotic machinery than bacterial. RNA polymerases closer to eukaryotic polymerases. The initiator tRNA is methionine in Archaea, formyl-methionine in Bacteria—a tiny but decisive difference in how protein synthesis begins.

**Domain Eukarya.** Ourselves, plants, fungi, and protists.

**Why did this take so long to discover?** Because Archaea are rare in the habitats humans knew well. Most known archaea lived in extreme environments—hot springs, salt lakes, the deep subsurface. Organisms called methanogens, which produce methane in anaerobic sediments. Organisms called halophiles, thriving in salt. When you study bacteria growing in hospitals and laboratories, you miss an entire domain.

**The implications.** The tree of life is not a ladder with prokaryotes at the bottom and eukaryotes on top. It is a bush with three equal trunks growing from the root. The most recent common ancestor of all life lived perhaps 3.5 to 4 billion years ago. From that ancestor, three lineages diverged. Bacteria evolved for billions of years before plants and animals existed. Archaea evolved in parallel. Eukaryotes eventually diverged from the archaean line (or possibly from an interaction between bacteria and archaea—the origins of eukaryotes remain partly mysterious).

**Common misconception.** Archaea are not "primitive." DNA-handling machinery in archaea is more similar to eukaryotic machinery than to bacterial machinery in ways that suggest Archaea and Eukarya share a more recent common ancestor than either does with Bacteria. Yet Archaea are also ancient, with lineages going back billions of years. Primitiveness is not the right word. Difference is.

## Section 5: Prokaryotes That Built the Biosphere — From Microbial Mats to the Modern World

**The earliest evidence of life.** The oldest fossils are **stromatolites**—layered sedimentary structures formed when bacteria in **microbial mats** precipitate minerals out of water. Evidence of stromatolites dates back 3.5 billion years. That is, life appeared on Earth roughly 1 billion years after Earth itself formed. A microbial mat is a multi-layered sheet of prokaryotes—mostly bacteria, sometimes archaea—growing on moist surfaces where different materials interface. The mat is held together by an extracellular matrix, a gluey substance the cells secrete. Different parts of the mat carry out different metabolic pathways, which is why they show different colors.

The first microbial mats likely obtained energy from chemicals at **hydrothermal vents**—fissures in the ocean floor releasing geothermally heated water rich in hydrogen sulfide and other reduced compounds. Chemosynthetic bacteria oxidized these compounds, earning energy. Photosynthesis evolved later, about 3 billion years ago. Some prokaryotes in the mats switched to sunlight as an energy source. Others remained dependent on chemicals from the vents. This metabolic partition—some using light, some using chemicals, all living in the same mat—allowed complex microbial communities to form even on a planet with little oxygen.

**The oxygenation of Earth.** Cyanobacteria are the culprits. They evolved photosynthesis capable of splitting water (H2O), releasing oxygen as waste. Over billions of years, oxygen accumulated. By 2.4 billion years ago, oxygen levels began rising sharply. The atmosphere went from anoxic to oxic. This killed most anaerobic organisms through oxidative stress. It enabled aerobic respiration and, eventually, large multicellular organisms. It was the largest mass extinction event in Earth's history, caused by prokaryotic waste.

**Biofilms and community living.** For decades, microbiologists thought of prokaryotes as isolated cells. Modern evidence shows they prefer communities. A **biofilm** is a multicellular aggregate held together in a matrix of polysaccharides, proteins, and nucleic acids that the cells secrete. Biofilms form on surfaces: rocks in streams, pipes in industrial plants, kitchen sinks, your teeth. Bacteria in biofilms are tougher than free-floating (planktonic) bacteria. The matrix excludes antibiotics and disinfectants. Cells in the interior transfer genes laterally to neighbors. A bacterium that produces an enzyme destroying antibiotic protects its neighbors. One antibiotic concentration that kills planktonic bacteria might require 1,000 times that dose to kill biofilm bacteria.

Biofilms are both blessing and curse. In rivers, biofilms are primary producers, converting light and nutrients into biomass. On medical catheters, they cause chronic infections. On food-processing equipment, they initiate outbreaks.

## Section 6: Prokaryotes as Pathogens — A Brief History of Plague

Prokaryotes are human inhabitants, mostly harmless. A few species are pathogenic—they damage their hosts. Understanding that damage is useful, but it distorts perspective. Focus only on pathogens and you see 1 percent of prokaryotic biology while imagining it is all there is.

**The Plague of Athens (430 B.C.).** During the Peloponnesian War, a disease killed one-quarter of Athens's troops. Researchers recovered DNA from a mass grave and identified *Salmonella enterica* serovar Typhi, which causes typhoid fever. The lesson: epidemiology can reach backward. You cannot perform an experiment on the past, but DNA is a kind of time machine.

**Bubonic plague.** *Yersinia pestis* circulates in rodent populations carried by fleas. When human trade routes intersected rodent habitats, the bacterium found a new host. The Plague of Justinian (541–750) killed 25 to 50 percent of the population in the eastern Mediterranean. The Black Death (1346–1361) killed an estimated 75 to 100 million people—roughly 25 percent of the world's population. The **pneumonic form**, spread human-to-human by coughing, spread faster than the bubonic form. Mortality was nearly 100 percent without modern antibiotics. Today, with antibiotics, mortality is low. The bacterium has not evolved resistance to the drugs we use against it. Antibiotics still work—for now.

**The antibiotic crisis.** When penicillin was first used clinically in 1941, it was miraculous. Bacterial infections that meant death became treatable. But overuse bred resistance. Resistance genes spread through bacterial populations via lateral gene transfer. Today, *Staphylococcus aureus* has evolved strains resistant to most antibiotics: methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA). MRSA infections are common in hospitals and, increasingly, in healthy people living in close quarters (military barracks, prisons). The average age of hospitalized MRSA patients is 68. The average age of community-associated MRSA patients is 23. The pathogen is evolving in real time, and our chemical tools are becoming less effective.

**Foodborne illness.** *E. coli* serotype O157:H7, *Salmonella*, *Listeria monocytogenes*, *Clostridium botulinum*—bacteria colonize food in biofilms on leaf surfaces and in processing equipment. The U.S. has one of the world's safest food supplies. The CDC reports 76 million foodborne illnesses annually in the U.S., more than 300,000 hospitalizations, 5,000 deaths. Most outbreaks trace to fresh produce, not meat. Raw spinach, alfalfa sprouts, vegetable sprouts in Germany—prokaryotic hitchhikers on food.

**Common misconception.** The problem is not that bacteria are becoming "stronger" in some mystical sense. They are becoming resistant to specific chemical molecules we have selected to kill them. Resistance evolves when antibiotics kill nonresistant cells, leaving resistant ones to multiply. The more antibiotics we use, the faster this selection happens. In livestock, antibiotics are added to feed in low doses to promote growth—conditions that maximize resistance without killing all the bacteria. These resistant bacteria transfer to humans through the food chain. This is not inevitable. It is a management problem created by overuse.

## Section 7: Prokaryotes as Partners — Why We Cannot Live Without Them

**Nitrogen fixation revisited.** Without nitrogen-fixing bacteria, agriculture as it exists would be impossible. Crops deplete nitrogen from soil. Applying chemical fertilizers costs money and energy. Planting legumes (beans, soybeans, alfalfa) allows *Rhizobium* bacteria in root nodules to fix atmospheric nitrogen, replenishing the soil. This is sustainable agriculture: using the metabolic capabilities of prokaryotes to manage the soil instead of industrial chemistry.

**The microbiome inside us.** Human bodies harbor prokaryotes—bacteria on skin, in the mouth, throughout the digestive tract. In the typical human, prokaryotic cells outnumber human cells roughly 10 to 1. These are not invaders. They are partners. They break down complex carbohydrates we cannot digest. They produce vitamins (like K and B12) we cannot synthesize. They occupy ecological space that would otherwise be colonized by pathogens. They train the immune system by presenting their own antigens, teaching immune cells what is self and what is other. Recent research suggests they may influence mood, activity level, and appetite. When antibiotics kill these communities, *Clostridium difficile*—normally rare because the healthy microbiota outcompetes it—blooms and causes severe diarrhea. The treatment is a **fecal transplant**: reinstalling the healthy bacterial community. This works because prokaryotic ecology is real ecology. Remove the competitors and invasive species take over. Restore the community and balance returns.

**Food fermentation.** Humans have used prokaryotes to process food for at least 6,000 years. Bread rises because yeast (a fungus) produces carbon dioxide and ethanol. Beer and wine are fermented beverages. Cheese hardens because lactic acid bacteria acidify milk, causing casein to precipitate. Yogurt develops its unique flavor from lactic acid-producing bacteria. These processes are ancient biotechnology: using prokaryotic metabolism to transform food into preserved, safe, flavorful products.

**Bioremediation.** Prokaryotes can degrade pollutants. *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* converts toxic mercury (Hg²⁺) into nontoxic elemental mercury (Hg⁰). Certain bacteria oxidize selenium oxyanions, removing them from contaminated groundwater. Oil-degrading bacteria like *Alcanivorax borkumensis* produce surfactants that solubilize oil, breaking it into smaller molecules. After the Exxon Valdez spill (1989), the BP oil spill (2010), and others, bioremediation—either natural (using wild oil-degrading bacteria) or enhanced (adding nutrients to stimulate bacterial growth)—accelerated cleanup. Under ideal conditions, up to 80 percent of nonvolatile oil components can degrade within a year. Aromatic and branched hydrocarbons persist longer, but prokaryotic metabolism is working on them.

## Section 8: Closing — The Machinery Underneath

A prokaryote is a solution to the problem: *How do you build a living cell with minimal machinery?* The answer is: very, very well. Circular DNA, ribosomes, a plasma membrane, a cell wall, and—often—a few optional extras. That is the hardware. The software—the genes—can be rewritten quickly by mutation, horizontal gene transfer, and selection. A prokaryote can evolve new metabolic capabilities on time scales of days to weeks.

We discovered that prokaryotes are not one category but two: Bacteria and Archaea, equally ancient, equally diverse, chemically distinct. This was not speculation about what existed. It was Carl Woese following the data where it led, using molecular tools to expose hidden kinship. The tree of life has three trunks, not two. We have been living with that fact only since the 1970s. It is still sinking in.

Prokaryotes built Earth's current chemistry. They generated the oxygen in the air you are breathing. They cycle nitrogen and carbon through ecosystems. They decompose dead matter and make its nutrients available again. Some are our partners, living in our bodies. Some are our parasites. Most are neither—they are just living their lives in the soil, the ocean, the subsurface, places where we rarely look. They were here for 3 billion years before we arrived. When we leave, they will still be here.

## Summary

Prokaryotes are unicellular organisms that lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Their simplicity is deceptive: within a single cell, they pack tremendous metabolic versatility. Two domains comprise prokaryotes: Bacteria (with peptidoglycan walls and fatty acid membranes) and Archaea (with alternative walls and different membrane chemistry). This distinction was discovered through molecular analysis of ribosomal RNA, revealing that the tree of life has three fundamental branches.

Prokaryotes occupy every environment on Earth, from moderate conditions to extremes (heat, cold, acid, salt, radiation). Most cannot be cultured in the laboratory, yet they are alive and detectable through molecular techniques. Many live in biofilms—communities held together by matrix material, more resistant to antibiotics than free-floating cells.

Prokaryotes metabolize using diverse energy and carbon sources, explaining their ecological success. Cyanobacteria evolved oxygen-releasing photosynthesis, transforming Earth's atmosphere. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen to ammonia, making it available for biological use. Prokaryotes decompose dead matter, recycle nutrients, and form symbiotic partnerships with eukaryotes. A few are human pathogens; most are essential partners or benign neighbors. Without prokaryotes, complex life on Earth would be impossible.

## What Would Change My Mind

If we discovered that archaea and bacteria evolved from a common eukaryotic ancestor (rather than eukaryotes evolving from the archaeal line), the three-domain hypothesis would need revision. Current evidence overwhelmingly supports three independent trunks, but new molecular data could challenge this.

## Still Puzzling

The origins of eukaryotes remain incompletely understood. Did eukaryotes arise from an archaean that engulfed a bacterium (producing the mitochondrion), or from a more complex interaction? The evidence leans toward symbiosis, but the exact mechanism and timing remain unsettled.

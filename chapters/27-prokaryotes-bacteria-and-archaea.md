# Chapter 27 — Prokaryotes: Bacteria and Archaea

*For three billion years, life on Earth was prokaryotic. Everything since has been an afterthought.*

---

In 1977, Carl Woese was comparing small differences in ribosomal RNA sequences — not the most glamorous work, but rigorous and patient. The sequences were supposed to reveal minor branching within the prokaryotes. Instead they revealed something that rewrote the tree of life.

The organisms everyone called "archaebacteria" — methane-producing cells from swamps, salt-loving cells from brine lakes, heat-tolerant cells from hot springs — were less related to other bacteria than bacteria were to us. Woese had not found a minor branch. He had found a third trunk. Life did not split early into prokaryotes and eukaryotes. It split into three: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya. Two of those domains have been quietly sharing the planet for over three billion years while we remained entirely unaware.

This is what happens when you stop looking at shape and start reading molecules.

---

## The Minimal Cell

A prokaryote is defined by what it lacks: no nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles, no internal compartments. This negative definition is honest but misleading, like describing a Swiss watch by noting it has no pendulum. What the architecture actually does is concentrate everything — DNA, ribosomes, reaction chemistry — into a single small space where nothing is separated, and everything communicates instantly.

All prokaryotes share four structures. The plasma membrane is a thin lipid bilayer that separates the cell's chemistry from the outside world. The cytoplasm is a dense solution of organic molecules, salts, and enzymes where almost all the cell's chemistry happens. The chromosome is a circular piece of double-stranded DNA floating in a region called the nucleoid — not enclosed, not separated, just there. And ribosomes, the protein-building machinery, are scattered through the cytoplasm in their thousands.

Around this core, different prokaryotes have added different tools. A cell wall outside the membrane prevents osmotic burst — without it, water rushing into the concentrated cytoplasm would pop the cell like a wet paper bag. A capsule outside the wall helps the cell stick to surfaces and evade immune attacks. Flagella provide motility, spinning like propellers. Pili are hair-like projections for attachment or for pulling DNA strands from neighboring cells. Plasmids are small circular loops of extra DNA carrying bonus capabilities — often including antibiotic resistance.

The cell wall is where the story gets interesting.

Bacteria build theirs from peptidoglycan — polysaccharide chains cross-linked by short peptide bridges containing D-amino acids. D-amino acids almost never appear in normal proteins, which is precisely why this structure is useful: it is alien enough to be a target. Antibiotics like penicillin mimic the D-amino acid bridges and sabotage the cross-linking reaction. The wall fails. The cell bursts. The drug works because the target is unique to bacteria.

Archaea build no peptidoglycan. Their walls use pseudopeptidoglycan, or polysaccharides, or glycoproteins, or pure protein — different in chemistry, analogous in function. This difference is not superficial. Antibiotics that demolish bacterial walls do nothing to archaeal walls. The walls serve the same mechanical purpose but are built from a different chemical alphabet.

The membranes differ too. Bacterial membranes are built from fatty acids esterified to glycerol — the standard lipid bilayer. Archaeal membranes use phytanyl chains — long branched hydrocarbons — etherlinked to glycerol. In some archaea, the phytanyl chains from opposite sides of the membrane bond to each other, forming not a bilayer but a monolayer. Same function: a barrier between inside and outside. Entirely different chemistry.

This is what Woese's molecular data was showing. Bacteria and Archaea are not a primitive version and a slightly less primitive version of the same thing. They are two independently evolved solutions to the same engineering problem. They converged on the same basic architecture — circular DNA, ribosomes, cell wall, membrane — but assembled it from different molecular components. They are, as Woese eventually argued, as different from each other as either is from us.

## Reading the Chemistry Under the Microscope

Hans Christian Gram invented a staining procedure in 1884 that reveals this chemistry without a mass spectrometer. You apply crystal violet to a bacterial smear, then iodine, then a decolorizing alcohol. Bacteria with thick peptidoglycan walls retain the stain and appear blue-purple — Gram-positive. Bacteria with thin peptidoglycan and an outer lipopolysaccharide envelope wash out under the alcohol and appear pink with a counterstain — Gram-negative.

Archaea contain no peptidoglycan at all. The stain does nothing predictable to them.

<!-- → [IMAGE: Gram stain comparison diagram — three panels showing cross-sections of cell wall architecture with stain behavior. Panel 1 (Gram-positive): thick peptidoglycan layer outside membrane, crystal violet retained, cell appears purple/blue. Panel 2 (Gram-negative): thin peptidoglycan beneath an outer lipopolysaccharide envelope, stain washed out by alcohol, counterstain picks up pink. Panel 3 (Archaea): no peptidoglycan, stain behavior unpredictable. Student should connect the wall chemistry from the previous section to the visible staining outcome and understand why Gram result predicts antibiotic susceptibility.] -->

Under a single light microscope, one chemical procedure separates three groups that molecular biology would later confirm are genuinely distinct in their evolutionary history. The chemistry writes itself into the color. Gram-positive bacteria like Staphylococcus are clinical targets for beta-lactam antibiotics because their thick peptidoglycan is exposed. Gram-negative bacteria like E. coli are harder to treat because the outer lipopolysaccharide envelope excludes many antibiotics before they reach the thin peptidoglycan layer beneath. Archaea, with no peptidoglycan at all, are resistant to all of these drugs by default.

## Extremophiles and the Meaning of Extreme

The textbook tells you that archaea live in extreme environments. This is true but backward. The correct statement is: what we call extreme is a matter of perspective, and prokaryotes have demonstrated that our perspective is narrow.

A thermophile grows optimally between 60 and 80 °C — temperatures that would denature most proteins, unravel most membranes, and kill most organisms. A hyperthermophile grows optimally above 80 °C; the current record holder, Methanopyrus kandleri, survives at 122 °C under pressure. A psychrophile grows at temperatures near 0 °C and below, in sea ice and permafrost. An acidophile thrives at pH 3 or lower — the acidity of lemon juice or battery acid. An alkaliphile thrives at pH 9 or above. A halophile requires at least 0.2 M sodium chloride and some species require the near-saturated brine of the Dead Sea.

The Dead Sea is instructive. Between Israel and Jordan, it is ten times saltier than seawater, forty times richer in magnesium, and acidic enough to damage human skin. The intuition is that nothing should live there. The observation is that halophilic bacteria and archaea thrive in it, forming dense mats visible from the shore. When the mats concentrate carotenoid pigments — which protect the cells from the intense solar radiation in shallow brine — they turn pink and red. The salt lake is not sterile. It is home. The color is a chemical solution to a physical problem.

The deeper point is not that these organisms are tough. It is that their enzymes fold into different shapes. Proteins have sequences, and sequences determine shapes, and shapes determine chemistry. A thermophile's enzyme is made of amino acids that form extra hydrogen bonds, extra disulfide bridges, tighter hydrophobic cores — molecular features that keep the protein folded at temperatures that would cook a mesophilic protein. A psychrophile's membrane contains more unsaturated fatty acids, which keep the bilayer fluid at temperatures where saturated fats would solidify. These are not exceptional properties. They are ordinary protein chemistry, adapted by mutation and selection to different operating conditions.

The practical consequence is significant. Thermostable polymerases from Thermus aquaticus gave us PCR. Every diagnostic test, every genomic sequencing run, every forensic DNA analysis depends on an enzyme pulled from a hot spring in Yellowstone and adapted for laboratory use. Extremophiles are not curiosities. They are reagent sources.

More than 99 percent of prokaryotes in any natural environment cannot be grown in laboratory culture. This is not fragility. It is ignorance. We do not know what they eat, what temperature they prefer, what micronutrients they require, whether they need chemical signals from neighboring species. Some enter a viable-but-non-culturable state — a dormancy triggered by stress from which they revive when conditions improve. We know they are alive because we can extract their DNA and amplify their 16S rRNA genes by PCR. We have sequenced millions of prokaryotic genes from ocean water, soil samples, and deep sediments. Most of those genes come from organisms we have never seen growing on a plate.

## The Chemistry of Feeding

Prokaryotes have partitioned the energy sources available on Earth among themselves in a way that no other group of organisms has managed.

The logic is two-dimensional. Where does the energy come from: light (phototroph) or chemicals (chemotroph)? Where does the carbon come from: carbon dioxide (autotroph) or organic molecules (heterotroph)?

Combine these and you get the metabolic types. A photoautotroph uses sunlight and builds its organic molecules from CO₂ — this is what cyanobacteria do, and what all green plants do. A chemoorganoheterotroph uses chemical energy from organic compounds and gets its carbon from organic compounds — this is what we do, and what most decomposing bacteria do. A chemolithotroph uses chemical energy from inorganic compounds: not glucose, but hydrogen sulfide, ammonia, iron ions, sulfur. At deep-sea hydrothermal vents, where sunlight never arrives, chemolithotrophs are the primary producers. They oxidize hydrogen sulfide pouring from the vents and use that energy to fix carbon from CO₂. Tube worms and clams at the vents do not photosynthesize. They harbor chemolithotrophic bacteria in their tissues and live off their output.

The oxygen story runs through all of this. Early Earth had essentially no free oxygen. For the first billion and a half years of life, prokaryotes used sulfur, iron, and other inorganic compounds as electron acceptors in energy metabolism. Then, about 2.7 to 3 billion years ago, cyanobacteria evolved a new kind of photosynthesis: one that splits water molecules. The reaction is:

$$2H_2O + \text{light energy} \rightarrow 4H^+ + 4e^- + O_2$$

Oxygen was a waste product. It accumulated. Over hundreds of millions of years, atmospheric oxygen rose from essentially zero to roughly the 21 percent it sits at today. This was not progress in any intentional sense. For most organisms alive at the time, it was poisoning. Oxygen is chemically reactive; it damages proteins and DNA. The anaerobes that had dominated Earth for billions of years either moved to oxygen-free refuges — deep sediments, swamps, animal guts — or died. The descendants of those refugees still live in those places, running the same ancient anaerobic chemistry their ancestors ran before the cyanobacteria changed everything.

For the organisms that survived in the oxygenating environment, there was a massive energetic opportunity. Aerobic respiration — using oxygen as the final electron acceptor — extracts far more energy per glucose molecule than any anaerobic pathway. This advantage drove selection for oxygen-tolerant and oxygen-exploiting metabolism. The mitochondria inside your cells are the descendants of aerobic bacteria, captured by an archaeal host cell perhaps 2 billion years ago in an endosymbiotic event that changed the trajectory of complex life.

Nitrogen fixation is the other metabolic capability that defines the modern biosphere. Nitrogen gas makes up 78 percent of the atmosphere. Plants cannot use it. Animals cannot use it. The largest nitrogen reservoir available is biologically inaccessible to almost every organism on Earth.

Certain prokaryotes carry nitrogenase, an enzyme that catalyzes:

$$N_2 + 16\text{ ATP} + 8e^- + 8H^+ \rightarrow 2\text{NH}_3 + 16\text{ ADP} + 16P_i + H_2$$

The reaction is expensive — 16 ATP per molecule of N₂ fixed — and nitrogenase is destroyed by oxygen, which is why root nodules housing Rhizobium bacteria in legumes maintain nearly anaerobic internal conditions despite living in aerobic soil. The energy cost and the oxygen sensitivity explain why this capability evolved in bacteria and has never been acquired by eukaryotes despite four billion years of opportunity.

Nitrogen-fixing bacteria contribute roughly 65 percent of the nitrogen that enters agricultural systems. Without them, the nitrogen cycle stops. Without the nitrogen cycle, protein synthesis in plants stops. Without plants, everything else stops. This is not hyperbole. The global food supply runs on prokaryotic chemistry.

## Three Domains, Not Two

The pre-Woese classification of life was: prokaryotes and eukaryotes, two fundamental categories, simple and complex. Prokaryotes lacked a nucleus; eukaryotes had one. The division was clean and based on visible cellular architecture.

Woese used ribosomal RNA rather than morphology. Ribosomes are ancient — they appear in every cell type known — and ribosomal RNA evolves slowly enough that ancient relationships leave traces. He sequenced the small subunit (16S in prokaryotes, 18S in eukaryotes) from dozens of organisms and compared them.

The resulting tree had three branches of equal depth. Bacteria: the familiar prokaryotes — E. coli, Salmonella, cyanobacteria, Mycobacterium, Streptococcus. Archaea: the methane-producers, the halophiles, the hyperthermophiles, organisms that had been classified as bacteria but whose molecular signatures were radically different. Eukarya: us, plants, fungi, protists.

The critical finding was not just that Archaea were different from Bacteria. It was which domain Archaea resembled more. The DNA replication machinery in Archaea — the polymerases, the replication origin recognition proteins, the histone-like proteins that compact the chromosome — resembles eukaryotic machinery far more than bacterial. The transcription apparatus in Archaea resembles eukaryotic RNA polymerase. The initiator tRNA in Archaea is methionine, as in eukaryotes, not the formyl-methionine used by bacteria. Archaea and Eukarya share a more recent common ancestor than either shares with Bacteria.

This means the deep split in life is not between cells-with-nuclei and cells-without. It is between Bacteria on one side and the ancestor of Archaea plus Eukarya on the other. Eukaryotes did not evolve from bacteria. They evolved from something much closer to the archaea — possibly from an archaeal lineage that acquired bacterial symbionts, one of which eventually became the mitochondrion.

Why did it take until 1977 to discover this? Because Archaea are rare in the environments humans study most intensively. Hospital bacteria, laboratory bacteria, gut bacteria — these are overwhelmingly from domain Bacteria. Archaea dominate in habitats that had not been seriously sampled: hot springs, deep-sea sediments, hypersaline lakes, the deep subsurface. Survey bacteria in familiar places and you miss an entire third of life entirely.

## Biofilms and the Community Life of Prokaryotes

Prokaryotes were thought, for most of the history of microbiology, to live as isolated cells — planktonic, free-floating, each pursuing its own metabolism independently. This view was a laboratory artifact. In nature, prokaryotes preferentially live in communities.

A biofilm is a multicellular aggregate attached to a surface, held together by a self-secreted matrix of polysaccharides, proteins, and extracellular DNA. The cells signal to each other, differentiating into distinct functional roles. Some cells in the biofilm are metabolically active; others form spores or dormant reserves. Nutrients flow through channels in the matrix. Waste products are expelled collectively. The biofilm is not a clump of identical cells. It is a structured community.

Biofilms are dramatically harder to kill than planktonic cells. Antibiotic concentrations that kill free-floating bacteria within an hour may require a thousand times the concentration to kill the same species in a biofilm. The matrix physically excludes many drugs before they reach the cells. Cells in the biofilm interior experience slow metabolism and reduced growth, making them insensitive to antibiotics that target actively dividing cells. Cells that produce antibiotic-degrading enzymes protect their neighbors through the shared matrix.

This has immediate clinical consequences. Chronic infections on medical implants — catheters, artificial joints, heart valves — are almost always biofilm infections. The bacteria are genetically identical to their planktonic relatives but behave like a different organism. Treating a biofilm infection requires either removing the infected device or using concentrations of antibiotics that would be toxic to the patient. The biofilm architecture itself is the pathological mechanism, not any particular toxin or virulence factor.

The same architecture that makes biofilms medically troublesome makes them ecologically essential. Biofilms on stream rocks are primary producers, converting light and nutrients into biomass for higher organisms. Biofilms in soil decompose organic matter and release nutrients. The biofilm on your teeth is a community of hundreds of species living in a matrix they built together. Disrupting it with antibiotics would work once, but the community would reassemble within days from cells in saliva, and the reassembled community might be more antibiotic-resistant than the original.

## The Biosphere as a Prokaryotic Project

Stromatolites are layered sedimentary structures, formed when prokaryotes in microbial mats precipitate calcium carbonate from the water around them. The oldest known stromatolites date to 3.5 billion years ago. Stromatolites are the first evidence of life on Earth. For the next 2 billion years — half of Earth's entire history — they were essentially the only complex ecosystem on the planet.

The organisms in modern microbial mats photosynthesize at the surface, ferment in the middle layers, and run sulfur chemistry at the bottom — different metabolic regimes stacked on top of each other, each using the outputs of the layer above as inputs. This stratification of metabolic types in a single mat is a miniature version of how ecosystems work at every scale.

Cyanobacteria in those early mats generated the oxygen that made the modern atmosphere. Sulfur-cycling bacteria in sediments run the sulfur half of the biogeochemical cycles. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria maintain the agricultural productivity of the planet. Methanogens in wetlands and cattle guts produce methane, a greenhouse gas that has always shaped Earth's climate. Decomposing bacteria break down every dead organism on Earth and return its constituent atoms to the soil and ocean where they can be used again.

Remove the prokaryotes and the biogeochemical cycles stop. Carbon accumulates in dead tissue. Nitrogen becomes inaccessible. Sulfur concentrates in sediments. Soil loses fertility. Photosynthesis continues in plants and algae for a time, but without the cycling that returns nutrients to the soil, the system degrades.

Prokaryotes are not primitive ancestors waiting to be replaced by more complex life. They are the operational infrastructure of the biosphere. They have been running the planet's chemistry for three billion years. Everything that came later — the eukaryotes, the plants, the animals — arrived as additions to a world already being maintained by prokaryotic metabolism, and depends on that metabolism for survival.

The MRSA in a hospital ward and the Rhizobium in a bean root nodule are both prokaryotes, both running metabolism that has been refined for billions of years, both following the same basic cellular logic. One we are trying to kill. One we could not live without. The difference is not that one is more advanced. It is that one has evolved in our antibiotic-rich environment and one has evolved in agricultural soil. Both are doing exactly what natural selection built them to do.

Three billion years is a long time to get good at something.

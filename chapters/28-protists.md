# Chapter 28 — Protists: The Eukaryotes That Aren't
*How life built complexity by theft, and why that matters for the oceans.*

---

Here is a fact that took me a long time to properly absorb.

Photosynthetic organisms too small to see with the naked eye perform roughly a quarter of all photosynthesis on Earth. Every year. Across the vast, mostly invisible surface of the world's oceans, single-celled creatures are fixing carbon at a rate three times larger than everything humans burn in a year. They regulate the composition of the atmosphere. They are the base of every marine food chain. If they disappeared next Tuesday, the ocean would collapse as an ecosystem within weeks, and atmospheric CO₂ would begin climbing toward levels the Earth has not seen in millions of years.

These organisms are protists. Most of them have no name you would recognize. They live and die in the ocean, in freshwater ponds, in soil, in the bodies of other organisms, doing their work invisibly. And yet the machinery they contain — the organelles they carry, the way those organelles were acquired — is the machinery that made your cells possible. Understanding protists is understanding the single most consequential event in the history of eukaryotic life: the moment one cell swallowed another and, instead of digesting it, kept it.

The term "protist" is, technically, a bad category. I want to explain why it is bad, and then explain why we keep using it anyway, because the explanation of both will tell you something real about how evolution works and how science names things that resist being named.

---

## Why the Category Is Broken

When the biologist Ernst Haeckel in 1866 needed a place to put organisms that were not animals, not plants, and not fungi, he called them Protista. The name means "first things" — as if these simpler creatures were the ancestral ground from which complexity eventually arose. It was a reasonable guess with the tools available. It was wrong.

Here is the problem, which molecular genetics made unavoidable in the 1990s. Two protists that look identical under a microscope — same shape, same habitat, same feeding strategy — can be more distantly related to each other than a mushroom is to an oak tree. Convergent evolution did that: similar environments produced similar-looking solutions from completely unrelated lineages. The appearance of similarity concealed an absence of common ancestry.

More fundamentally: plants, animals, and fungi are not separate from protists. They evolved *out of* protist lineages. Green algae — protists — are the ancestors of all land plants. The protist lineage that gave rise to animals is distinct from the one that gave rise to plants, which is distinct from the one that gave rise to fungi. If you draw a circle around "all protists" on the evolutionary tree, you either have to include plants, animals, and fungi inside that circle (making the category incoherent), or you draw a circle that cuts through the tree rather than enclosing a branch (making the category formally invalid).

This kind of invalid grouping has a name in systematics: paraphyletic. You have included an ancestor and some of its descendants but excluded others. The kingdom Protista is paraphyletic, which means it is not a real evolutionary unit.

And yet the term persists, and for good reason. Protists are eukaryotes that share a particular kind of existence: mostly aquatic, mostly microscopic, lacking the tissue specialization of plants and animals and fungi, and occupying ecological roles — photosynthesizer, predator, decomposer, parasite — that none of the multicellular kingdoms have monopolized. "Protist" does not name a clade. It names a way of being eukaryotic that was not replaced by the multicellular solution. It is ecologically real even if it is phylogenetically fiction.

When you use the term in this chapter, use it the way an engineer uses an approximation: knowing it is not exact, knowing why, and knowing what precision you are trading for the convenience.

## The Theft That Made Us

Now the mechanism. This is the part that rewards careful attention, because it explains not just protists but your own cells.

About two billion years ago, a large archaeal cell engulfed a smaller alpha-proteobacterium. You already know the story's ending: the bacterium became the mitochondrion. But let me dwell on the middle, because the middle is where the strangeness is.

The bacterium should have been digested. That is what predatory cells do: they engulf food, package it in a vesicle, and break it down. Instead, this bacterium survived inside the larger cell. It continued to respire. It continued to use oxygen. And critically — the host cell continued to benefit from that respiration. Over millions of years, the relationship locked in. The bacterium's own genome shrank, most of its genes transferring to the host cell's nucleus. The bacterium lost its autonomy. But it never lost its function. Today we call it the mitochondrion, and the evidence that it was once a free-living bacterium is written in its DNA: mitochondrial DNA is bacterial, and the genetic code used by mitochondrial ribosomes is slightly different from the code used by cytoplasmic ribosomes — a fossil difference that marks the ancient boundary between two organisms.

This first event — primary endosymbiosis — happened once, producing the mitochondrion, and then happened again in some lineages, producing the chloroplast. An ancestral eukaryote engulfed a cyanobacterium. The cyanobacterium became the chloroplast. The lineage that carries the legacy of this second engulfment is Archaeplastida, which includes green algae, red algae, and all land plants. The chloroplast's bacterial origin is also written in its DNA, its double membrane (one from the original bacterial cell membrane, one from the host cell's engulfing vesicle), and its ribosomes, which respond to bacterial antibiotics rather than eukaryotic inhibitors.

Primary endosymbiosis: one bacterium engulfed, one new organelle.

But in the oceans, something more intricate happened. Some protists did not acquire their chloroplasts by engulfing a cyanobacterium directly. They engulfed an entire eukaryotic algal cell that already had a chloroplast inside it. This is secondary endosymbiosis: a bacterium was engulfed once to make a chloroplast, and then that chloroplast, still inside its algal host, was engulfed again by a larger predatory protist.

The result is a chloroplast wrapped in four membranes instead of two. The outer two membranes are the remnants of the second engulfment — the vesicle membrane and the plasma membrane of the engulfed algal cell. The inner two are the original endosymbiotic membranes from the first engulfment. In some lineages, the remnant of the algal nucleus — a tiny, shrunken structure called a nucleomorph — can still be found sitting between the inner and outer membrane pairs, a ghost of the organism that was swallowed.

Secondary endosymbiosis happened not once but multiple times, in independent lineages. Diatoms acquired their chloroplasts from a red alga. Euglenids acquired theirs from a green alga. Dinoflagellates have a more complicated history still. Each acquisition left a different membrane signature, a different genetic legacy, a different metabolic capacity. This is why protists are described as a genome mosaic: at the center, archaeal-derived nuclear DNA; wrapped inside mitochondria, alpha-proteobacterial DNA; wrapped inside chloroplasts, cyanobacterial DNA; and in some cases, nested inside everything else, the shrunken remainder of an entire separate eukaryote's genome.

<!-- → [IMAGE: Cross-section diagram of a secondarily photosynthetic protist cell (e.g., a diatom or cryptomonad) showing the nested structures — nucleus (archaeal-derived DNA), mitochondria (alpha-proteobacterial DNA), and the 4-membrane chloroplast with the nucleomorph labeled between inner and outer membrane pairs; annotate each compartment with its evolutionary origin and the approximate time of acquisition] -->

You can read the history of these thefts in membrane counts. A chloroplast with two membranes came from primary endosymbiosis. A chloroplast with four membranes came from secondary endosymbiosis. The membranes are fossils. They record, in molecular geometry, a billion years of predation that became partnership.

## What Protists Do in the World

The machinery matters. Now the consequences.

Protists have evolved into almost every nutritional strategy available to a eukaryotic cell. Diatoms, dinoflagellates, and green algae photosynthesize, fixing carbon from dissolved CO₂ using their secondarily-acquired chloroplasts. Ciliates like Paramecium are predators, engulfing bacteria by phagocytosis, wrapping them in food vacuoles, digesting them. Amoebae extend pseudopods — flowing projections of cytoplasm — to surround and engulf prey larger than themselves. Plasmodium and Trypanosoma are parasites, living inside host cells and hijacking host metabolism for their own replication.

Some protists refuse to pick a lane. Euglena photosynthesizes in sunlight and switches to eating bacteria in darkness. The zooxanthellae that live inside coral cells photosynthesize and pass the sugar to their host; the coral provides shelter and metabolic waste products (nitrogen, phosphorus) that the algae need for growth. This metabolic flexibility — mixotrophy — is the signature of a cell with multiple inherited machinery sets running simultaneously. An organism with both mitochondria and chloroplasts can generate ATP from light or from organic molecules depending on conditions. It pays for the regulatory complexity of running two systems by having insurance against environmental instability.

The ecological scale of protist photosynthesis is worth quantifying. Total biosphere primary production is roughly 120 gigatons of carbon per year. Protists account for approximately a quarter of that: 30 gigatons annually. Global human carbon emissions are around 10 gigatons per year. Protists are sequestering three times what we emit, every year, through normal photosynthesis. Much of what they fix sinks — dead cells, fecal pellets from animals that ate them, aggregated organic particles — falling out of the sunlit surface waters into the deep ocean. This sinking export is the biological carbon pump, and it has been running for hundreds of millions of years, pulling carbon out of the atmosphere and burying it in ocean sediments. Atmospheric CO₂ would be roughly 50 parts per million higher if this pump stopped.

The structure of ocean food webs rests on photosynthetic protists. Diatoms in the North Atlantic bloom in spring, growing explosively as sunlight returns and nutrients mix upward from deep water. Zooplankton — copepods, krill, small crustaceans — graze on the diatoms. Small fish eat the zooplankton. Larger fish eat the small fish. Whales eat the krill. The entire marine food web, from its base to its apex, is underwritten by what a single-celled organism with a stolen cyanobacterium inside it does when the sun rises.

When we talk about ocean ecosystem collapse, we are talking about what happens when that foundation is disrupted. Warming surface waters stratify, reducing the mixing that brings nutrients up to the sunlit zone. Acidification from dissolved CO₂ threatens the silica shells of diatoms and the calcium carbonate shells of other protists. A disrupted bloom in spring translates, weeks later, into less food for zooplankton, which translates, months later, into less food for fish, which translates, years later, into collapsed fisheries and altered whale migration routes. The invisibility of the problem does not diminish its scale.

## Parasitic Protists: The Other Face of Complexity

The ocean is one context. The human body is another. And in the human body, protists appear not as photosynthesizers but as some of history's most successful killers.

Plasmodium, the parasite that causes malaria, is a protist. It has a life cycle of spectacular complexity, requiring two hosts: an Anopheles mosquito and a vertebrate, usually a human. In the mosquito, Plasmodium undergoes sexual reproduction, producing sporozoites that migrate to the salivary glands. When the mosquito bites a human, sporozoites are injected. They travel to the liver, invade liver cells, and replicate asexually for a week or two. Then they burst out as merozoites, invade red blood cells, and continue replicating. The synchronized bursting of red blood cells — waves of parasites breaking out simultaneously — produces the cyclic fevers that are malaria's clinical signature.

The parasite's relationship with the host immune system is a study in evolved evasion. Plasmodium proteins on the surface of infected red blood cells change repeatedly as the infection progresses — a process called antigenic variation. The immune system begins to recognize and attack one surface protein; the parasite switches to expressing a different one. By the time the immune response catches up, the parasite is presenting a different face. This is not random mutation. It is a programmed switching system, encoded in a large family of genes dedicated entirely to producing novel antigenic diversity.

<!-- → [INFOGRAPHIC: Plasmodium life cycle diagram — two panels: left panel showing mosquito phase (sexual reproduction → sporozoites in salivary glands); right panel showing human phase (sporozoites injected → liver stage → merozoites burst out → red blood cell invasion → cyclic bursting causing fever → some merozoites differentiate into gametocytes → taken up by next mosquito); annotate the two-host requirement and the synchronized red blood cell bursting as the mechanistic basis for cyclic fevers] -->

Trypanosoma brucei, the cause of African sleeping sickness, uses the same strategy — variant surface glycoproteins that switch expression systematically, staying one step ahead of antibody clearance. The parasite's genome contains hundreds of VSG genes, but only one is expressed at a time. When the immune system mounts an antibody response to the current VSG, the parasite switches to expressing a different one. The infection is never eliminated; it smolders for months or years, eventually penetrating the blood-brain barrier and causing neurological deterioration.

These are not simple organisms. A parasite that can sustain chronic infection in the face of an adaptive immune system has solved problems that took evolution millions of years to crack. The complexity is not in the organism's body plan — Plasmodium is a single cell — but in its molecular architecture: thousands of genes dedicated to host manipulation, immune evasion, cell invasion, and life cycle coordination across two host species. What looks like simplicity at the morphological level is extreme sophistication at the molecular level.

This is the corrective to the intuition that protists are primitive. Parasitic protists are not failures at becoming multicellular organisms. They are optimized for a niche — living inside another organism's cells — that multicellular body plans cannot occupy. Their complexity is in the right place for the problem they are solving.

## What Endosymbiosis Actually Means

Let me return to the beginning and say what I think the most important thing in this chapter is.

Eukaryotic complexity did not arise from random mutation and gradual refinement. It arose from catastrophic events: one organism engulfed another, and instead of one of them dying, both survived and merged. The mitochondrion is a bacterium that was never digested. The chloroplast is a second bacterium, or in many protists a bacterium nested inside a eukaryote, that was never digested. Your cells contain the descendants of organisms that were alive two billion years ago, still doing much of the same chemistry, but now inside you, bounded by membranes that record the history of their capture.

This is what protists make vivid in a way that plants and animals do not. When you look at a land plant, the chloroplasts are there, but they are two steps removed from the current organism's complexity — you are looking at the result, not the process. When you look at a diatom with its four-membrane chloroplast and its nucleomorph ghost, or when you look at Euglena switching between photosynthesis and phagocytosis depending on the light, you are watching endosymbiosis in mid-consequence. The mosaicism is still visible. The seams between the stolen machinery and the host machinery are still legible.

The deepest insight is this: the boundary between organism and organelle is not fixed. Two billion years ago, your mitochondrion was an independent organism. Today it is an organelle. One billion years ago, some protist's chloroplast was an independent eukaryotic cell. Today it is folded inside another cell, its nucleus reduced to a nucleomorph, its independent existence erased except in the molecular record. What we call "an organism" is, at sufficient resolution, a coalition of formerly independent things that have negotiated, over billions of years, an arrangement in which cooperation is more stable than independence.

That is the lesson protists teach. Not that they are primitive, not that they are the ancestors of complex life in some linear sense, but that complexity can be assembled by capturing other complexities and keeping them. The theft that made you was not a failure of predation. It was the beginning of something that took two billion years to produce the cell reading this sentence.

**Warm-up**

1. Ernst Haeckel's kingdom Protista is described as paraphyletic. Explain what paraphyletic means using a specific example from the chapter — identify the ancestor, some of the included descendants, and one group of descendants that is excluded. *Tests: understanding paraphyletic groupings and their logical problem.*

2. A chloroplast is observed with two surrounding membranes. A second chloroplast from a different species has four surrounding membranes. For each, state whether primary or secondary endosymbiosis produced it, and explain where each membrane came from. *Tests: reading membrane counts as evidence of endosymbiotic history.*

3. Euglena is described as mixotrophic. Define mixotrophy and explain what cellular machinery Euglena must carry to perform both photosynthesis and phagocytosis. Why does carrying both systems require more regulation than carrying just one? *Tests: linking organelle inheritance to metabolic strategy and its regulatory cost.*

**Application**

4. A diatom bloom in the North Atlantic fixes 5 × 10⁹ kg of carbon over three weeks in spring. Of this fixed carbon, roughly 20 percent sinks to the deep ocean as marine snow before being consumed. Calculate the mass of carbon exported to the deep ocean by this bloom. Then explain why this export matters for atmospheric CO₂ on timescales of decades rather than days. *Tests: applying the biological carbon pump concept quantitatively.*

5. Ocean surface warming increases water column stratification, reducing upwelling of nutrient-rich deep water. Predict the consequence for spring diatom blooms in the affected region. Then trace the downstream effect through two additional trophic levels in the marine food web. *Tests: applying protist ecology to predict ecosystem-level cascades from environmental change.*

6. Plasmodium's surface proteins on infected red blood cells change repeatedly through a programmed switching system. Explain why this is more effective at evading the immune system than simply accumulating random mutations. What does the existence of hundreds of VSG or PfEMP1 variant genes in the parasite genome tell you about the evolutionary pressure the immune system has imposed over millions of years? *Tests: distinguishing programmed antigenic variation from random mutation, and connecting gene family size to evolutionary history.*

**Synthesis**

7. The chapter argues that parasitic protists like Plasmodium are not "primitive" but are instead highly specialized. Construct a specific argument for this claim by comparing two features of Plasmodium's biology — one morphological (body plan) and one molecular (genome organization) — and explaining why each reveals sophistication rather than simplicity. *Tests: applying the distinction between morphological and molecular complexity.*

8. Mitochondria and chloroplasts both retain small genomes despite having transferred most of their ancestral bacterial genes to the host nucleus. Propose two reasons why natural selection might have favored retaining some genes in the organelle rather than transferring all of them to the nucleus. Then explain why this residual organellar DNA is useful as evidence for the endosymbiotic theory. *Tests: reasoning about the evolutionary logic of organellar genome retention, and connecting molecular evidence to the endosymbiotic hypothesis.*

**Challenge**

9. Secondary endosymbiosis occurred independently in multiple protist lineages (diatoms from red algae, euglenids from green algae, etc.). If secondary endosymbiosis is possible and beneficial, why has it not happened a hundred times more, producing an even greater diversity of photosynthetic protists? What limits or constraints might prevent most secondary endosymbiosis attempts from being preserved by natural selection? Identify at least three specific challenges a host cell would face after engulfing a photosynthetic eukaryote. *Tests: reasoning from mechanism to evolutionary constraint — not just what happened, but why it did not happen more often.*

10. Zooxanthellae (dinoflagellate protists) live inside coral cells and provide photosynthetically-fixed carbon in exchange for shelter and nutrients. Ocean warming causes coral bleaching — the expulsion of zooxanthellae — which eventually kills the coral. A researcher proposes engineering heat-tolerant zooxanthellae strains that could recolonize bleached corals and resist future warming. Using what you know about protist cell biology, endosymbiosis, and the coral-zooxanthellae relationship, identify two specific biological challenges that would need to be solved for this approach to work, and explain why each challenge exists at the cellular or molecular level. *Tests: applying protist biology to a novel applied scenario, requiring integration of mixotrophy, symbiosis, and endosymbiotic cell biology.*

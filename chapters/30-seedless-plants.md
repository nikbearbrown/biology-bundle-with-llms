# Chapter 30 — Seedless Plants
*How life solved three of the four problems of living on land — and why the fourth one kept plants wet for three hundred million years.*

---

Here is a substance you have never thought about.

The yellow dust that coats your car every spring is made of a polymer called sporopollenin. Chemists cannot fully synthesize it. Bacteria cannot digest it. Acids cannot dissolve it. Bases cannot dissolve it. Fungi cannot eat it. Paleontologists find sporopollenin walls intact in rocks 400 million years old. When they drill into the polar ice cores and pull up layers of compressed ancient atmosphere, the pollen grains they find are surrounded by sporopollenin walls as structurally sound as the day they were made.

This is not incidental. It is the first evidence for what this chapter is about: the story of how life moved from water to land is a story about solving specific physical problems, and the solutions it found were so good that some of them have not changed in hundreds of millions of years.

---

## Four Problems

When plants first began colonizing land, they faced four problems that did not exist in water.

**Desiccation.** In water, a cell is bathed in moisture. On land, water evaporates into air, and a cell exposed to dry air loses water faster than diffusion can restore it. Dry out the chemistry and life stops.

**Structural support.** Water is buoyant. An organism three feet long floats without effort in the ocean. The same size on land would collapse under its own weight. Something stiff was needed to hold plants upright against gravity.

**Transport.** In water, nutrients diffuse. A single-celled alga absorbs what it needs from the surrounding medium. But a tall plant cannot rely on diffusion — the distances are too great. Water and minerals need to move from roots up to leaves. Sugars need to move from leaves down to roots. That requires dedicated plumbing.

**Reproduction.** In water, sperm swim. An alga releases sperm and they swim chemically toward an egg. On land, there is no medium for swimming. How does a sperm reach an egg without water as a vehicle?

Seedless plants solved the first three problems elegantly. The fourth one they did not solve. That failure — if you can call it that — defines where they live today and tells you more about the logic of evolution than any of the successes do.

---

## Solution One: The Cuticle and Stomata

The first solution to desiccation was chemical: a waxy waterproof layer on the plant's outer surfaces called the cuticle. The cuticle is composed of hydrophobic molecules that seal water inside the plant. Touch a waxy leaf in sunlight and feel it — that sheen is the cuticle, the chemical seal between the plant's water and the drying air.

But a sealed plant cannot breathe. Photosynthesis requires carbon dioxide, which is in the air. A fully waterproof surface blocks CO₂ from entering.

The solution to the solution: stomata. A stoma is a microscopic pore in the cuticle, flanked by two guard cells that swell when filled with water and push the pore open, and relax when they lose water and let the pore close. When the plant needs CO₂, stomata open. When the plant needs to conserve water, they close.

This pair of adaptations — seal the surface, put in controlled gaps — solved the central tension of terrestrial life: you need to be waterproof but not impermeable. The trade-off is real and it never goes away. Every time stomata open to admit CO₂, water vapor escapes. A plant in drought closes its stomata and stops photosynthesizing. The same mechanism that keeps it alive also limits its productivity.

---

## Solution Two: Vascular Tissue

Getting water to leaves fifteen meters above the soil by diffusion alone is physically impossible. The distances are too great and the time required is too long. Vascular plants solved this by evolving two types of specialized transport tissue that together form something close to a circulatory system.

Xylem is the water-conduction system. Xylem cells die after they form and leave behind only their cell walls — hollow cylinders stacked end to end, forming a continuous pipeline from root tip to leaf surface. Water and dissolved minerals are pulled up this pipeline by two forces simultaneously: the pull of transpiration from above (water evaporating from leaves creates negative pressure that pulls a column of water upward through the xylem), and root pressure from below (osmotic activity in roots that pushes water into the xylem against gravity).

The cell walls of xylem are reinforced with lignin, a polymer that is simultaneously hydrophobic and structurally rigid. Lignin is what wood is. It is why wooden beams don't warp when wet and why wooden ships kept floating for centuries. Lignin is also the reason coal exists — but more on that in a moment.

Phloem is the sugar-conduction system. Unlike xylem, phloem cells remain alive. They are connected by channels called plasmodesmata that pass through the cell walls, allowing cytoplasm to flow from one cell to the next. Sugar produced in the leaves dissolves in water and is pumped into phloem cells; pressure pushes this sugar solution to wherever the plant needs it — growing regions, roots, storage.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: cross-section of a vascular bundle showing xylem and phloem side by side — xylem vessels labeled as dead hollow cells with lignin-thickened walls, arrows showing upward water movement via transpiration pull; phloem cells labeled as living cells with plasmodesmata, arrows showing downward sugar movement via pressure flow — student should see the structural difference between the two tissues and understand why xylem cells must die to function while phloem cells must remain alive] -->

Together, xylem and phloem allowed plants to grow tall. And growing tall is not merely an aesthetic choice for a plant. Height is competition. A plant that grows taller than its neighbors intercepts light that would otherwise reach them. Tall plants shade out short plants. Xylem and lignin were the technology that made trees possible.

---

## The Double Life: Alternation of Generations

Here is the part of plant biology that surprises almost everyone the first time they encounter it.

All plants — every moss, every fern, every oak tree — have a life cycle that passes through two entirely different multicellular forms. One form is haploid (one copy of each chromosome per cell). The other is diploid (two copies). Both forms are multicellular. Both live for weeks, months, or years. This is not like human reproduction, where the only haploid cells are the sperm and egg and everything else in the body is diploid.

The haploid form is called the gametophyte. It produces gametes — sperm and eggs — by mitosis. Since the gametophyte is already haploid, its gametes are haploid too, and making them doesn't require meiosis.

When a sperm fertilizes an egg, the resulting zygote is diploid. It grows by mitosis into the diploid form, called the sporophyte. The sporophyte produces spores by meiosis. The spores are haploid. When a spore lands in a hospitable spot, it germinates and grows by mitosis into a new gametophyte.

This is called alternation of generations. The interesting question is not whether it happens — it happens in all plants — but which generation is visible and which is hidden.

In mosses, the gametophyte is the obvious plant. When you see a carpet of moss on a log, you are looking at the haploid form. The sporophyte is the small brown stalk with a capsule at the tip, growing out of the gametophyte's body. Most people do not notice it.

In ferns, the sporophyte is the obvious plant. The fern frond with its intricate leaflets is the diploid form. The gametophyte is a tiny heart-shaped structure about the size of a fingernail, living on moist soil, so small that most people who have spent their whole lives near ferns have never seen one.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: alternation of generations — two-loop cycle diagram for a fern; left loop: sporophyte (diploid, large fern frond labeled) → meiosis → spores (haploid) → spore dispersal; right loop: spore germinates → gametophyte (haploid, tiny heart-shaped structure labeled) → mitosis produces gametes → fertilization → zygote → grows by mitosis into sporophyte; each cell division type (mitosis/meiosis) and ploidy level labeled; student should see that meiosis is only involved in making spores (not gametes) and that fertilization is the only event that restores diploidy] -->

The general trend in plant evolution is for the sporophyte to become larger and more complex and the gametophyte to become smaller and more hidden. In flowering plants, the gametophyte has been reduced to a few cells tucked inside a flower — a pollen grain (the male gametophyte) and an embryo sac inside the ovary (the female gametophyte). The alternation is still there, still fundamental, but the haploid form has shrunk to near-invisibility.

---

## Sporopollenin: The Wall That Outlasts Everything

When the sporophyte produces spores, those spores need to survive the journey from parent to a new place to live. They dry out. They are battered by wind and rain. They sit on soil surfaces for months waiting for conditions to be right. The chemistry that protects them is sporopollenin.

Sporopollenin is a polymer made of chains of organic molecules related to fatty acids and carotenoids. Its structure is not fully understood — despite being one of the most studied molecules in botany, chemists still cannot completely synthesize it — but its properties are well-documented. Acids don't dissolve it. Bases don't dissolve it. Most biological enzymes can't break it down. It can be completely dehydrated and then fully rehydrated without loss of structural integrity. It is, in several important senses, indestructible by ordinary biological means.

When paleontologists drill sediment cores from the ocean floor or from peat bogs, they find pollen grains millions of years old. The soft interior of the grain is long gone. But the sporopollenin wall is intact, sometimes preserving its surface texture with enough fidelity to identify the plant species that made it.

This is why pollen is the basis of paleoclimatology: the sporopollenin record in sediment cores is continuous, detailed, and reliable. If you want to know what plants grew in what place ten thousand years ago, you look at the pollen preserved in the sediment. The walls tell you.

Sporopollenin is also orange-yellow in color, which is why most pollen is yellow. That coating your car in spring is sporopollenin, accumulated from hundreds of species reproducing simultaneously.

## The Problem That Wasn't Solved

Here is where the story turns.

A moss gametophyte produces sperm in a structure called an antheridium and eggs in a structure called an archegonium. The sperm have flagella. They swim. When the moss surface is wet — after rain, in heavy dew, in a splash from a stream — sperm are released and swim through the water film toward the archegonium, guided by chemical signals released by the egg.

If there is no water, there is no swimming. The sperm cannot travel. Fertilization does not happen.

This is the wall that seedless plants never broke through. Every innovation described so far — the cuticle, the stomata, the vascular tissue, the lignin, the sporopollenin wall — all of these freed plants from depending on water for everything except reproduction. But reproduction required liquid water for sperm transport. Every moss, every fern, every horsetail and club moss must have liquid water — a film, a puddle, a wet surface — at the moment of fertilization.

This is why ferns line streams. This is why mosses grow on the shaded side of rocks where dew persists. This is why seedless plants do not live in deserts.

Seed plants later solved this by making the male gametophyte — the pollen grain — waterproof and giving it a tube that grows through plant tissue, allowing sperm to travel from pollen to egg without needing water. But that is the next chapter's story. For three hundred million years, seedless plants lived within this constraint. They were not failures — they dominated the planet. But their dominion was geographically bounded by the distribution of liquid water.

---

## Four Groups

Seedless plants fall into four groups distinguished by anatomy and evolutionary history.

**Bryophytes** — mosses, liverworts, and hornworts — are non-vascular. They have no xylem, no phloem. Water and nutrients move by diffusion and osmotic flow through cell walls. Without vascular tissue, they cannot grow tall — most are a few centimeters. The gametophyte is the visible form. Bryophytes appear in the fossil record about 450 million years ago and probably represent an evolutionary intermediate: plants with a cuticle and spores protected by sporopollenin, but not yet with xylem.

Despite their small size, bryophytes do ecological work that is easy to underestimate. *Sphagnum* moss, which dominates peatlands across the boreal zone, can hold twenty times its dry weight in water, acting as a giant sponge that regulates hydrology across millions of square kilometers. Globally, peatlands cover only 3 percent of land area but hold roughly 30 percent of the soil carbon on Earth — more than all the world's forests. The long-chain organic molecules in peat resist bacterial decomposition partly because of the chemical environment that *Sphagnum* creates around itself (acidic, low-nitrogen). Drain a peatland and oxidize the stored carbon back to CO₂ and you are releasing millennia of sequestration.

**Lycophytes** — club mosses and their relatives — were among the first plants to evolve vascular tissue. In the Carboniferous period, three hundred million years ago, lycophytes called *Lepidodendron* grew to forty or fifty meters, with trunks thicker at the base than a modern oak. Their bark left diamond-pattern scars in the fossils — the attachment points of shed leaves. Today's lycophytes are small, less than a meter, easily overlooked as what look like little Christmas trees in forest understories. The sporophyte is the dominant form. The gametophyte lives in the soil and is rarely seen.

**Monilophytes** — ferns and horsetails — are the most diverse seedless vascular plants alive today. Ferns are especially abundant in tropical forests, where fronds can exceed several meters in length and spores are produced in structures called sori (singular: sorus) on the underside of the frond. Horsetails have jointed stems and whorls of thin branches; their cells are loaded with silica, which is why they were used historically as scouring pads. The sporophyte is the obvious form. The gametophyte is the tiny heart-shaped creature on the forest floor.

**Seed plants**, which solved the water-for-reproduction problem, are discussed next.

## The Carboniferous as Consequence

The Carboniferous forests were not incidental. They were the direct result of what seedless plants could and could not do.

Three hundred million years ago, the land now forming Pennsylvania, England, northern France, Germany, and the Donets Basin in Ukraine sat near the equator in vast equatorial swamps. Lycophyte trees towered above the waterlogged ground. *Calamites* — horsetails the size of small trees — grew in dense thickets along stream edges. Tree ferns spread their fronds into every gap of light. These forests were pulling enormous quantities of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and locking it into wood.

When a lycophyte tree fell into the anoxic swamp water, something unusual happened: it did not fully decompose. The fungi capable of efficiently digesting lignin — white-rot fungi — had not yet evolved to handle the massive lignin concentrations in tree wood. (This is one current hypothesis; the evolutionary history of lignin decomposition is still debated.) The lignin-rich wood accumulated in layers. Sediment buried it. Over tens of millions of years, pressure and heat converted it to coal.

This is the origin of coal. Nearly all the coal that powered the industrial revolution — British coal, Pennsylvania anthracite, Ruhr valley coal — is compressed lycophyte forest from the Carboniferous. When you burn coal, you are oxidizing the carbon that these giant club mosses captured from the Carboniferous atmosphere.

The atmospheric CO₂ was so thoroughly removed by these forests that oxygen levels rose — perhaps to 35 percent of the atmosphere, compared to 21 percent today. At 35 percent oxygen, fires ignite easily and burn intensely. And at 35 percent oxygen, insects, which breathe through diffusion and are size-limited by how far oxygen can diffuse through their tissues, could grow large. *Meganeura* — a dragonfly relative — had a wingspan of about 70 centimeters, roughly two and a half feet, flying through the Carboniferous sky. The giant arthropods of the Carboniferous — the massive millipedes, the enormous dragonflies — were a direct consequence of the oxygen-rich atmosphere that seedless forest trees created.

Then the climate dried. The swamps contracted. The seed plants that had been evolving quietly in drier upland regions expanded. The great seedless forests retreated to the wet margins where their reproductive biology could still function. Their remains stayed underground.

The coal did not move. It waits there, compressed, until someone burns it.

---

## The Pattern

Here is what seedless plants teach you about evolution.

Evolution does not solve all problems at once. It solves problems incrementally, each solution creating new trade-offs, each innovation enabling the next one. The cuticle enabled dryness but required stomata. Stomata required the guard-cell mechanism. Vascular tissue enabled height but required lignin for structural support. Lignin enabled tall trees but was indigestible for three hundred million years, which is why coal exists.

Each solution was added to what already existed. The earliest land plants — the ancestors of today's mosses — had a cuticle and spores with sporopollenin walls but no vascular tissue. Vascular tissue was added later, in a different lineage. Lignin came with vascular tissue. The innovations layered.

What did not change was reproduction. Flagellated sperm swimming through water is the mode inherited from aquatic ancestors, and seedless plants retained it without modification. It was not that no mutation ever appeared in sperm structure. It was that every mutation that affected sperm motility in ways that freed them from water was never successful enough to spread — the surrounding environment was wet enough that water-dependent reproduction worked fine. There was no selection pressure to change it.

The innovation that freed reproduction from water — pollen and the pollen tube — was so consequential that when it appeared, it changed the distribution of plant life on the entire planet. We will see that in the next chapter. For now, the lesson is this: the story of life on land is not a smooth progression from water to dryness. It is a series of partial solutions, each one allowing organisms to reach further from the water's edge, each one creating new dependencies and new constraints. Seedless plants reached very far. They still were not far enough.
